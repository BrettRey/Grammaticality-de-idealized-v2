# Thirty-Fourth Adversarial Pass Synthesis

**Date:** 2026-06-09
**Input:** Kauf et al. (2023) special-issue paper on ANN-to-brain similarity in fMRI responses.
**Card added:** `ann-brain-lexical-semantic-predictivity`

## Result

The source is useful as a measurement-validity discriminator, not as a direct grammaticality card.
It tests whether the workbench can prevent ANN/fMRI predictivity from leaking into grammatical
licensing, syntactic structure, or operator-value claims.

No graph mutation is justified.

## Evaluations

- `PROC` partly survives. It already separates processing, felt naturalness, measurement task,
  reported acceptability, attribution, and licensing. It lacks explicit ANN/fMRI measurement-channel
  constructs, so the card should not promote it.
- `CAT` partly survives. It has the right measurement-discipline posture and blocks direct movement
  from representational evidence to licensing, but its current constructs are category-task
  constructs, not neural/ANN predictivity constructs.
- `OPG` is out of scope as a general account. It can represent recoverability and processing paths
  to reported acceptability, but opportunity/preemption machinery is not the right explanation for
  ANN-to-fMRI similarity.

## Discovery Consequence

The pass adds a new card type: measurement-validity cards. These are useful when a source tells us
what a measurement channel is sensitive to. They should not be treated as ordinary phenomenon cards
unless a grammar-facing contrast cell is present.

## Guardrail

Do not infer:

- high ANN-to-brain predictivity means grammatical licensing;
- low function-word contribution in this fMRI benchmark means low grammatical importance;
- word-order robustness in a sentence-level ANN/fMRI mapping benchmark means word order is not part
  of grammar.

The current workbench can record those guardrails without adding new ontology nodes.
