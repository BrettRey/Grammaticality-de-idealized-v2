# Pressure Test

This pressure test starts with the placement assumption and the method assumption.

## Load-Bearing Claims

1. The workbench should map rival grammaticality explanations rather than automate OVMG.
2. Conceptual node ontology is the first bottleneck.
3. DAGs are useful even if the final result is a diverse graph ecology rather than a single winner.
4. Sub-agents should generate and attack theory-biased graphs, not adjudicate scholarly truth.
5. Quality-diversity preservation matters more than early tournament selection.

## Failure Conditions

The workbench is failing if:

- seed graphs keep reintroducing OVMG as the unmarked default;
- `reported_acceptability` is repeatedly used as grammaticality;
- graphs cannot represent robust stigmatized forms or rare licensed forms;
- graph scoring rewards verbosity or familiarity rather than construct hygiene;
- feedback relations are represented as illegal synchronic cycles;
- phenomenon cards become examples that decorate graphs rather than adversarial tests.

## Anti-Thesis

The strongest objection is that grammaticality may not support a single DAG or stable set of DAG families. It may be a family-resemblance compression over several partially independent practices: judgment, production, correction, pedagogy, analysis, register management, and community licensing.

That would not defeat the workbench. It would become one of its possible results.

## First Adversarial Questions

1. What does the graph predict for a form that is rare but uncontroversial?
2. What does it predict for a form that is frequent but condemned?
3. What does it predict for a stigmatized but robust community form?
4. What does it predict for a grammatical but hard-to-process sentence?
5. What does it predict when production and reported judgment diverge within the same speaker?
6. What changes when the same item is judged under correctness, naturalness, and register-fit framings?
