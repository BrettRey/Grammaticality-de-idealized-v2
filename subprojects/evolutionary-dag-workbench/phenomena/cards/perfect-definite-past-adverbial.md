# Phenomenon Card: perfect with definite past adverbial

**Stress test:** Temporal anchoring and aspect/tense operator fit.

## Description

Ordinary English contrasts:

- `I did it.`
- `I have done it.`
- `I did it yesterday.`
- `*I have done it yesterday.`

The bad cell is transparent: the hearer can recover the intended event and time. The problem is
not semantic opacity. In the relevant standard-English repertoire, the present perfect frame does
not combine with a definite past-time anchor like `yesterday` in the way the simple past does.

## Why It Matters

The card tests whether a graph can represent contextual anchoring as a constraint on a licensed
form-value relation rather than as a free rescue parameter. Recoverability of the intended
meaning should not count as licensing for the present-perfect-plus-definite-past cell.

## Minimum Contrast Cells

- simple past without definite adverbial: `I did it`;
- present perfect without definite past adverbial: `I have done it`;
- simple past with definite past adverbial: `I did it yesterday`;
- present perfect with definite past adverbial: `I have done it yesterday`.

## Potential Construct Pressure

This card may require a more specific construct for temporal-anchor fit or tense/aspect value
alignment if `constructional_frame_fit` and `operator_value` remain too broad to predict which
temporal adverbials conflict with which tense/aspect frames.

## Graph Failure Modes

- Treats recoverability of the intended event as licensing.
- Treats `yesterday` as merely payload content with no effect on tense/aspect frame fit.
- Treats the present perfect and simple past as interchangeable because both can refer to a past
  event.
- Ignores the contrast between no explicit definite past anchor and explicit `yesterday`.

## Predicted Discriminators

- `FRAME` should partly survive by separating frame fit from recoverability, but it may lack a
  temporal-anchor-specific construct.
- `OPG` should partly survive if it represents operator value without collapsing it into
  acceptability.
- `PROC` should not explain the contrast as processing difficulty.
- `DYN` should not explain the contrast through ideology, stigma, or diachronic feedback.
