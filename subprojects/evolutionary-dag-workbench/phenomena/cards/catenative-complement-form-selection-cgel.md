# Phenomenon Card: catenative complement form selection

**Stress test:** Non-finite complement form, meaning, and selection.

## Description

CGEL classifies catenative verbs partly by the form type of the non-finite complement. Some verbs
allow more than one complement form with little change in meaning, while others change meaning
across complement forms:

- `He intends to leave at six.`
- `He intends leaving at six.`
- `He should try to eat less.`
- `He should try eating less.`

In the `try` pair, the first means roughly endeavouring to eat less, while the second means testing
the effectiveness of eating less. Complement form is therefore not free paraphrase or raw
collocation.

## Why It Matters

The card tests whether complement selection can separate at least three cases: same-meaning
alternation, meaning-changing complement-form choice, and unlicensed complement form. A graph that
only has a single complement-selection node may represent the boundary, but not predict the
subclasses.

## Minimum Contrast Cells

- `intend to leave`;
- `intend leaving`;
- `try to eat less` with endeavour meaning;
- `try eating less` with test-effectiveness meaning;
- unlicensed or sharply marked complement-form substitution for a verb whose catenative class does
  not permit it.

## Potential Construct Pressure

This card may require a complement-form subtype or catenative-class construct if `SEL` remains too
broad to distinguish same-meaning alternation from meaning-changing alternation and unlicensed
substitution.

## Graph Failure Modes

- Treats non-finite complement form as free payload choice.
- Treats all complement-form alternation as the same selection fact.
- Treats semantic recoverability as licensing.
- Treats fixedness or frequency as equivalent to catenative class membership.

## Predicted Discriminators

- `SEL` should partly survive as a complement-selection boundary module.
- `FRAME` may partly survive where constructional frame fit is the key contrast.
- `PROC` should not explain the selection contrast as processing difficulty.
