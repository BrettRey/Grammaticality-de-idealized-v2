## Relevance to "The Etiological Account"

### 1. Multimodal Dynamics: Same Mechanisms, Different Channels

The etiological paper's two-module architecture (opportunity-sensitive preemption + coordination equilibria) should, in principle, operate across modalities. Cohn's claim that graphics, gesture, and speech share cognitive building blocks suggests:

- **Module 1 (Bayesian preemption):** Works identically for visual languages. Opportunity mass $N_t(n,c)$ would be counted in visual communicative contexts (e.g., how often does a particular panel-transition niche arise in comics?).
- **Module 2 (Coordination equilibria):** Visual communities (comics readers, diagram users) would exhibit the same equilibrium dynamics—exclusion persists because deviation is penalized.

### 2. Entry/Exit Dynamics for Visual Languages

The paper's unified entry/exit treatment (§3.5) predicts that visual constructions can invade or be expelled through the same mechanisms:
- **Invasion:** A novel visual idiom (e.g., a new panel transition type) gains positive evidence in a community and shifts the coordination equilibrium.
- **Exclusion:** A once-common visual convention falls out of use as alternatives preempt it.

**Testable prediction:** Historical shifts in comics conventions (e.g., the decline of thought balloons in favor of internal monologue boxes) should follow S-curve trajectories consistent with the Beta-update dynamics.

### 3. CAS Acknowledgment: Broader Applicability

The conclusion's acknowledgment of complex adaptive systems (CAS) should perhaps note that the framework extends beyond speech. If Cohn's architecture is correct, the two-module dynamics explain stability across all three modalities, with modality-specific constraints affecting opportunity counts and processing costs.
