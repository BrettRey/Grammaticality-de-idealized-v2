# Notes on Skyrms (2010) - Chapter 14: Learning to Network

### 1. THE STRUCTURE OF NETWORKS
*   **Examples:** Kula Ring (circular exchange of shell valuables), Computer LAN rings.
*   **Bala-Goyal Model:** Players pay to link to others to get information. Information flows one-way.
    *   **The Ring:** In this game, a Ring topology is a strict Nash equilibrium and is efficient.

### 2. LEARNING THE RING
*   **Failure:** Simple reinforcement learning fails to find the Ring (it's too hard to coordinate).
*   **Success:** "Best Response with Inertia" finds it.
*   **Probe and Adjust:** Skyrms introduces a simpler rule: "Keep doing what you're doing. Occasionally try a random change (Probe). If it's better, switch (Adjust)."
    *   *Result:* Probe and Adjust (low rationality) finds the Ring just like Best Response (high rationality).

### 3. FROM RINGS TO STARS (HIERARCHY)
*   **Two-Way Flow:** If info flows both ways, the Ring breaks down.
*   **The Star:**
    *   **Center-Sponsored Star:** Center pays for all links. (Strict Nash, but humans hate it—unfair).
    *   **Periphery-Sponsored Star:** Everyone pays to link to the "Hub." (Strict Nash).
*   **Information Decay:** If info degrades as it travels, the Star becomes even more dominant (short path lengths).
*   **Heterogeneity:** If some people have *better* info, they naturally become hubs.

### 4. CONCLUSION
*   **Network Formation:** Adaptive dynamics (Probe and Adjust) explain how complex social networks (Rings, Stars) emerge from local interactions.
*   **The Big Picture:** Skyrms concludes the book. We have moved from the spontaneous generation of meaning (Ch 1) to complex syntax (Ch 12) and social structure (Ch 14) using only simple adaptive mechanisms.
