# Notes on Richerson & Boyd (2005) - Chapter 7: Nothing About Culture Makes Sense Except in the Light of Evolution

### 1. SUMMARY OF THE ARGUMENT
The authors conclude by synthesizing their core argument: Cultural evolution is a Darwinian process that interacts with genetic evolution to shape human behavior.

*   **Culture is Biological:** It is rooted in brain mechanisms evolved via natural selection.
*   **Culture is Autonomous:** Once established, culture has its own dynamic forces (bias, transmission) that can act independently of (and sometimes against) genetic fitness.
*   **The Synthesis:** We cannot understand humans with *only* genes (sociobiology) or *only* culture (cultural anthropology). We need Dual Inheritance Theory.

### 2. FIVE DOMAINS OF EVIDENCE
They review the "consilience" of their theory across different types of evidence:

1.  **Logical Consistency:** Mathematical models confirm that the proposed mechanisms (e.g., conformist bias favoring group selection) are logically sound and plausible.
2.  **Proximate Mechanisms:** Psychology confirms we are "super-imitators" compared to chimps. We have specific biases (prestige, conformity).
3.  **Microevolution:** Studies of diffusion (e.g., hybrid corn, spread of English dialects) show Darwinian descent with modification in real time.
4.  **Macroevolution:** The timing of the "cultural explosion" fits the climatic data (Pleistocene variability). The slow rise of complexity fits the group selection models (it takes time for group-beneficial traits to beat out selfish ones).
5.  **Maladaptation:** The theory uniquely predicts specific *kinds* of maladaptation (selfish memes, demographic transition), which we actually observe. Pure fitness-maximization theories fail here.

### 3. METHODOLOGICAL UNIFICATION
The authors argue that the social sciences are currently fragmented (Psychology vs. Sociology vs. Economics). Evolutionary theory provides the necessary framework to unite them.
*   **Vertical Integration:** Biology connects chemistry to organisms. Dual Inheritance Theory connects psychology (individual learning) to sociology/anthropology (population outcomes).
*   **The "Micro-Macro" Problem:** Darwinian population thinking solves the longstanding social science dilemma of how individuals create institutions and how institutions shape individuals.

### 4. THE SCIENTIFIC TOOLKIT
*   **Models:** Simple mathematical models are necessary "prostheses for the mind" to check our intuition about complex systems.
*   **Data:** We need more quantitative ethnography. The description of culture should be as rigorous as the description of the genome.

### 5. FINAL THOUGHTS
*   **Human Exceptionalism:** Humans *are* unique, but understanding that uniqueness requires evolutionary theory, not a rejection of it.
*   **Optimism:** Understanding the mechanics of our own evolution (e.g., how institutions form, why we cooperate, why we fight) is the best hope for addressing the massive problems of the modern world.
*   **Grandeur:** They end by echoing Darwin: there is "grandeur in this view of life," where culture is seen not as a separate magisterium, but as part of the entangled bank of evolution.
