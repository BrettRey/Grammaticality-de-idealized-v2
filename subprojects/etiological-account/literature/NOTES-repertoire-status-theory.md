Repertoire Status Theory
I'm developing a theory of why a speech community's repertoire status for linguistic forms (C_t) follows the trajectories it does. This covers three questions:

  1. Arise: How does C_t increase? How do forms enter the repertoire?
  2. Excluded: How does C_t decrease toward zero for structurally possible, interpretable forms?
  3. Stability: Why does C_t remain stable — whether high or low?

  The paper distinguishes:

  - Constitutive question: What makes something grammatical? (State theory: G̃ = mapping viability × speaker knowledge × community repertoire status)
  - Etiological question: What causal processes produce and sustain particular C_t values?

  The two-module framework:

  1. Opportunity-sensitive preemption (proximate mechanism for decrease): A Bayesian learner updates beliefs about whether a form is licensed. Preemption pressure depends on (a) how often the communicative niche arises, and (b) whether a competitor consistently wins. High opportunity + consistent competitor → rapid exclusion. Key variable: effective opportunity mass (opportunities weighted by counterfactual suitability).

  1. Asymmetry: This module explains how C_t goes down, but what drives C_t up? Innovation, contact, analogy, reanalysis — but these aren't formalized in the current framework.
  2. Coordination equilibria (sustaining mechanism for stability): Once C_t settles (high or low), the community is in a self-sustaining coordination equilibrium. Unilateral deviation from the established pattern is penalized. This explains persistence in both directions: excluded forms stay excluded; established forms resist displacement.

  Key claims:
  - Both modules operate through the feeling of ungrammaticality/grammaticality (F) — the subjective signal guiding production and comprehension
  - The interaction predicts stability profiles: doubly-sustained states resist change; singly-sustained states drift under the right conditions
  - "Categorical constraints" are stable outcomes of dynamics, not primitive bans

  Current gap in the framework: The account is better developed for exclusion than for innovation. What drives C_t up?

  Research questions:

  1. Literature on innovation/entry: What work addresses how forms enter a community's repertoire? Grammaticalization, contact linguistics, analogical extension, L1 acquisition overgeneralization that gets retained — what formalizations exist?
  2. Asymmetry: Is there theoretical reason to expect asymmetry between entering and exiting the repertoire? (Cf. frequency effects: rare forms are vulnerable, but what protects common forms beyond coordination?)
  3. Literature gaps: What relevant work from game theory, cultural evolution, sociolinguistics, or language acquisition am I missing? Especially:
    - Formal models of norm emergence (both establishment and extinction)
    - Learning-theoretic work on indirect evidence (absence as evidence)
    - Historical linguistics on how innovations spread vs. how forms die
  4. Potential objections: What are the strongest objections? Where are conceptual vulnerabilities?
  5. Phenomena to explain: What should this framework handle but might struggle with? Stable innovations? Unstable exclusions? Reversals?
  6. Formalization: Tools from evolutionary game theory, cultural evolution modeling, or Bayesian learning that could sharpen predictions — especially for the "arise" side?

1.1.1 Labovian Framework: Shared Norms and Evaluation

This framework distinguishes between change from above—conscious adoption of prestigious forms driven by overt social evaluation—and change from below—unconscious propagation of innovative variants through dense social networks . Labov’s later work in Principles of Linguistic Change, Volume III further elaborates the distinction between social factors (effects of linguistic interaction among group members) and cultural factors (associations with broader social patterns independent of face-to-face interaction), demonstrating how large-scale ideological oppositions can synchronize vowel shifts across millions of geographically dispersed speakers . The Labovian model thus constrains C_t trajectories through shared evaluative frameworks that regulate the rate and direction of change, preventing chaotic divergence while allowing for systematic variation across socioeconomic, ethnic, and stylistic dimensions.

2. Mathematical Models of Trajectory Formation
2.1 Diffusion and Growth Curve Models
2.1.1 The S-Curve Model of Linguistic Change
The S-curve model represents the canonical mathematical description of linguistic diffusion, positing that successful innovations follow a characteristic trajectory: slow initial adoption by a small minority of innovators, followed by rapid acceleration as the innovation reaches critical mass, and final deceleration as saturation approaches . Weinreich, Labov, and Herzog (1968) established the empirical foundation for this model, demonstrating that sound changes progress through communities following "a lawful course, an S-curve from minority to majority to totality" . Mathematically, this pattern follows the logistic function:
Ct​=1+e−k(t−t0​)L​ 
where L represents the carrying capacity (maximum repertoire status), k is the growth rate parameter determining trajectory steepness, and t₀ marks the inflection point of maximum growth velocity . This formulation captures the non-linear nature of linguistic diffusion, where the exponential term drives rapid middle-phase expansion while the denominator ensures asymptotic saturation. The S-curve emerges from the interplay of intra-speaker accommodation (individuals adjusting usage based on interactional frequency) and inter-speaker transmission (forms spreading through network connections), with the initial slow phase corresponding to diffusion within dense early-adopter networks, the acceleration phase representing successful bridging to other communities via weak ties, and the saturation phase reflecting the diminishing pool of potential adopters . Empirical validation from the Corpus of Early English Correspondence (CEEC) confirms this pattern in historical changes such as the shift from third person -th to -s and the replacement of second person ye with you, both showing the characteristic slow-fast-slow progression .
2.1.2 Logistic Growth and Saturation Effects
Logistic growth models extend the S-curve framework to incorporate carrying capacity constraints that prevent indefinite growth of linguistic forms. The differential equation dC/dt = rC(1 - C/K) captures the intuition that growth rate decelerates proportionally to the remaining "space" for adoption, where r represents the intrinsic rate of increase and K the community's carrying capacity . In sociolinguistic contexts, K is not a fixed biological constant but a socially constructed limit that shifts as community norms evolve, potentially resulting in stable variation rather than categorical replacement when forms achieve domain-specific equilibrium . Saturation effects arise from demographic constraints (finite pools of adopters), social stratification (hard ceilings where specific groups resist adoption due to identity concerns), and functional constraints (incoming forms failing to fully replace the functional range of outgoing forms). Research on lexical innovation reveals that saturation levels correlate with network topology: forms spreading primarily through dense networks with few weak ties often reach lower saturation levels (local maxima) compared to forms successfully bridging multiple network clusters . Additionally, volatility of usage affects saturation; highly volatile forms (high coefficient of variance) typically show lower effective saturation because sporadic usage prevents consistent reinforcement across the community . The logistic model can be modified to incorporate Allee effects (where very low-frequency forms face extinction risks due to lack of critical mass) or density-dependent inhibition (where high-frequency forms trigger counter-movements), producing complex trajectories including bistability where C_t can settle into either high or low equilibrium states depending on initial conditions.

2.3.2 Accommodation Mechanisms and Saltatory Change
Accommodation theory operationalized within ABMs specifies that agents adjust variant selection probabilities based on interlocutors' usage patterns, creating micro-mechanisms that aggregate into macro-level trajectories . When agent x interacts with agent y, and y uses variant 1 with probability 0.8, x shifts its own probability for variant 1 upward by a specified accommodation rate (e.g., 0.2), creating dynamic convergence or divergence depending on network structure . Two distinct regimes produce qualitatively different trajectory types: incremental change along continuous scales (gradient accommodation appropriate for phonetic variables) versus saltatory (categorical) change between discrete states (binary switching appropriate for lexical or morphological replacements) .
Saltatory change exhibits threshold effects where variants remain in stable equilibrium for extended periods before rapid transition to near-categorical usage once critical mass adoption occurs . The timing of these transitions depends critically on network density; dense networks maintain stable distributions longer before abrupt shifts, while sparse networks with hubs facilitate earlier but potentially incomplete transitions . The accommodation rate parameter determines trajectory steepness—higher rates produce rapid convergence and sharp S-curves, while lower rates create gradual, extended transitions. This parameter likely correlates with social psychological factors such as solidarity motivation, identity concerns, and cognitive flexibility, though these relationships require further empirical validation.
2.3.3 Baxter-Blythe-Croft-McKane (BBCM) Replicator-Interactor Framework
The BBCM framework integrates evolutionary linguistics with complex systems theory, distinguishing four distinct mechanisms of language change: replicator selection, neutral interactor selection, weighted interactor selection, and neutral evolution . Replicator selection corresponds to fitness-based propagation, occurring when speakers differentially weight variant replicators, leading to asymmetric propagation where one variant spreads while the other declines . This aligns with classic sociohistorical models where differential social valuation drives change, though the BBCM treats differential weighting as an emergent property of speaker behavior rather than an external given.
Neutral interactor selection operates through properties of speakers (interactors) rather than variants (replicators), occurring when differential replication results from varying rates of interaction between speakers, even when variants carry equal weight . This mechanism models the "who you talk to" effect central to social network theories, where network structure determines diffusion pathways independent of intrinsic variant properties. This mechanism is symmetric (affecting both speakers equally) and scales linearly with population size, suggesting larger communities may experience slower change rates under purely neutral interactor selection . Weighted interactor selection combines both effects, occurring when speakers with specific social characteristics (high centrality, particular demographics) disproportionately influence others while also exhibiting variant preferences, creating complex feedback loops where social network position and linguistic preference reinforce each other. Neutral evolution represents drift mechanisms where random sampling effects in finite populations lead to variant fixation without systematic selection pressures, dominating in small, isolated communities or for low-salience variables .

3.3 Cognitive and Cultural Factors
3.3.1 Cultural Attractors and Cognitive Alignment
Cultural attractors represent stable points in the space of possible linguistic forms that accumulate speakers due to shared cognitive biases, creating population-level convergence toward specific variants despite individual variation . These attractors emerge from the interaction between universal cognitive constraints (perceptual salience, memory limitations, processing efficiency) and social transmission dynamics. For example, phonological changes often proceed toward states maximizing perceptual distinctiveness while minimizing articulatory effort, creating attractor basins that channel C_t trajectories toward specific phonetic targets .
Cognitive alignment—the degree to which community members share similar cognitive category structures—is not a precondition for cultural stability but an emergent outcome of social interaction . Populations starting with random cognitive distributions can self-organize into stable attractor configurations through iterated learning and accommodation. The stability of these configurations depends on the balance between faithful transmission (preserving existing forms) and innovation (introducing variation that may discover new attractors). High alignment with dense networks produces rapid convergence on shared norms, while low alignment with sparse networks preserves variation and multiple attractors, potentially supporting stable multilingualism or long-term variation .
3.3.2 Critical Periods in Language Learning
The critical period hypothesis in language acquisition fundamentally shapes C_t trajectories by creating developmental windows during which linguistic forms are most easily acquired and most resistant to change . Research on cultural attractor dynamics suggests that short, critical periods of learning early in life enhance repertoire stability by constraining plasticity during acquisition, while long learning times may destabilize repertoires by allowing excessive drift . This creates generational structure in C_t trajectories; each cohort's repertoire reflects the community state at their time of acquisition, potentially creating discontinuities between generations when community norms shift rapidly.
When community norms shift during a generation's critical period, that cohort may stabilize on a different attractor than their parents, producing apparent time effects that mirror real-time change. The time scale of memory decay determines how quickly communities can reverse trajectories in response to changing social conditions; shorter decay periods enable rapid adaptation but potentially less stable norms, while longer decay periods create momentum effects where trajectories continue in their current direction despite changing social forces. These developmental constraints explain why C_t trajectories often exhibit hysteresis and why community changes frequently lag behind social changes, as older cohorts maintain childhood-acquired forms while younger cohorts adopt new variants.

In linguistic applications, the microsystem encompasses immediate conversational partners and their accommodation dynamics, while the macrosystem includes standard language ideologies, national education policies, and global economic pressures that create prestige asymmetries between languages . The chronosystem (temporal dimension) captures historical changes in these nested systems, explaining how C_t trajectories may shift abruptly when macro-level conditions change (e.g., post-colonial language policy shifts) or gradually through micro-level accumulation of individual choices. This ecological perspective emphasizes that no single level determines C_t; rather, trajectories emerge from the interaction across scales, with micro-level agency constrained by macro-level structure and macro-level patterns emerging from micro-level interactions.

The multi-scalar nature of ecological influences creates complex feedback loops between levels. Microsystem interactions—daily conversations, peer group negotiations—provide the immediate mechanism for variant adoption or maintenance, with accommodation rates and identity performances driving short-term fluctuations in C_t . These interactions are constrained by mesosystem structures such as social network density and community of practice boundaries, which determine which variants individuals encounter and which social meanings they carry.

Changes at any level can perturb the entire system; a shift in macrosystem language policy (e.g., official recognition of a minority language) can rapidly alter exosystem institutional practices, which reshape mesosystem network boundaries, ultimately transforming microsystem interactional patterns and individual C_t values.

Linguistic resilience—borrowed from ecological theory—refers to the capacity of a speech community to maintain distinct repertoire configurations or recover from perturbations without losing functional diversity . Resilient systems exhibit plateau phases or partial recoveries in C_t trajectories rather than monotonic increases or decreases, reflecting community-level resistance to shift despite individual-level accommodation pressures. This resilience emerges from redundancy in the network (multiple pathways for variant transmission), modularity (compartmentalization of repertoires by domain or group), and diversity (maintenance of multiple variants for similar functions).
Repertoire mobility refers to the fluidity with which speakers can shift between varieties or acquire new forms, contrasting with rigid compartmentalization . High mobility enables rapid C_t changes as speakers easily adopt innovative forms, while low mobility creates inertia and stability. The range of repertoire—the internal distance between constituent varieties—determines mobility; communities with high range (multilingual, multidialectal) exhibit greater flexibility in responding to contact situations, while homogeneous communities may resist change but lack resources for adapting to new communicative demands . Resilience and mobility interact to determine trajectory shapes: highly resilient, low-mobility systems maintain stable C_t despite external pressures (maintenance), while low-resilience, high-mobility systems exhibit volatile trajectories with rapid shifts between states (shift or rapid change).
4.2 Adaptive Cycles and Nonlinear Dynamics
4.2.1 The Adaptive Cycle Model (Growth, Conservation, Release, Reorganization)
The adaptive cycle model from resilience theory characterizes C_t trajectories as passing through four phases: growth (rapid adoption and repertoire expansion), conservation (stabilization and entrenchment of forms), release (collapse or rapid decline of established forms), and reorganization (establishment of new repertoire configurations) . During the growth phase, innovative forms increase in C_t through exponential diffusion, accumulating speakers and social meanings. The conservation phase sees these forms becoming entrenched, with high connectedness and rigidity as they become associated with specific identities or institutional functions.
Release occurs when external shocks (demographic collapse, political revolution, sudden contact) disrupt established patterns, causing rapid declines in C_t for previously dominant forms as the system loses resilience. Reorganization follows, with high potential for novelty as the system explores new repertoire configurations before settling into a new growth phase. This cyclical pattern explains why C_t trajectories are rarely linear; long periods of stability (conservation) may be punctuated by sudden shifts (release) or gradual drift (growth). The model predicts that mature systems (high conservation) are particularly vulnerable to catastrophic release, while young systems (early growth) exhibit high adaptability but low stability.

Chaos and complexity theory reveals that C_t trajectories may exhibit sensitive dependence on initial conditions, where small perturbations in early adopter characteristics or network structure lead to vastly different long-term outcomes . This butterfly effect in linguistic systems means that predicting C_t requires precise knowledge of initial conditions that may be practically unknowable, limiting deterministic forecasting while allowing for probabilistic modeling of trajectory types.
Nonlinear dynamics produce phenomena such as bifurcations (sudden qualitative changes in system behavior as parameters cross critical thresholds), strange attractors (bounded but non-repeating trajectories), and phase transitions (abrupt shifts between distinct states). For example, the transition from minority to majority status for a linguistic form may represent a phase transition occurring at a critical threshold of adoption (the "tipping point"), below which the form declines to extinction and above which it rapidly achieves dominance . These nonlinear properties challenge linear regression approaches to modeling C_t, necessitating dynamical systems methods capable of capturing threshold effects, feedback loops, and emergent properties that arise from the interaction of multiple variables.

Self-organization refers to the emergence of macro-level order (stable C_t patterns) from micro-level interactions without centralized coordination . In speech communities, self-organization manifests through the spontaneous alignment of individual linguistic behaviors into community-wide norms, where C_t values emerge as attractor states toward which the system converges. Emergent stability arises when positive feedback loops (accommodation, prestige effects) and negative feedback loops (saturation, resistance) balance to create dynamical equilibria where C_t remains constant despite ongoing micro-level variation . For example, stable diglossia represents an emergent equilibrium where high and low varieties maintain distinct C_t values across different domains, sustained by the mutual reinforcement of social evaluation and functional specialization. The stability of these emergent patterns is dynamic rather than static; constant social interaction is required to maintain C_t values, explaining why isolation or network disruption can lead to rapid destabilization and trajectory shifts.
4.3 Demographic and Environmental Constraints
4.3.1 Population Size and Scaling Laws
Population size imposes fundamental constraints on C_t trajectories through mechanisms of drift, diffusion, and carrying capacity. Process-based simulation models demonstrate that population shocks—sudden increases or decreases in group size—drive range expansion or contraction, leading to language extinction, fragmentation, or contact-induced change . Positive shocks (growth) enable range expansion and contact between previously isolated groups, potentially accelerating C_t changes through increased weak-tie connectivity, while negative shocks (decline) create fragmentation through range splitting or extinction through demographic collapse.
The relationship between population size and linguistic diversity follows scaling laws: larger populations support greater diversity up to a point, after which coordination pressures favor convergence and standardization . Small populations (fewer than ~1,000 speakers) exhibit high volatility, with stochastic drift potentially eliminating variants or driving rapid fixation due to sampling effects. Medium populations support stable diversity through balanced drift and diffusion. Large populations (tens of thousands or more) experience strong normative pressures and reduced network connectivity per capita, favoring simplified repertoires and rapid standardization of C_t for high-prestige forms . These scaling effects explain why endangered languages (small populations) often show erratic C_t trajectories while major world languages (large populations) exhibit smooth, predictable changes.
4.3.2 Migration and Language Contact Dynamics
Migration creates contact between speech communities, initiating C_t trajectories of borrowing, shift, or convergence . Agent-based models of lectal contamination demonstrate that contact inevitably produces mixed repertoires when populations with distinct morphosyntactic preferences and lexical stocks interact regularly . The directionality of influence depends on relative population sizes and prestige asymmetries; larger or higher-prestige groups disproportionately influence smaller or lower-prestige groups, creating asymmetric trajectories where minority forms decline while majority forms spread.
Sprachbund phenomena emerge when multiple communities maintain prolonged contact within broad communication fields, creating structural convergence despite genetic distinctiveness . The rate of shift depends on social factors such as economic opportunity, educational policy, and identity maintenance motivations, with C_t for the heritage language declining gradually while the dominant language C_t increases through intergenerational transmission disruption.
4.3.3 Environmental Carrying Capacity for Linguistic Diversity
Environmental carrying capacity refers to the maximum number of distinct languages or varieties that can be sustained in a region given ecological constraints . This capacity depends on environmental productivity, topography, and social organization, with high-productivity environments supporting dense populations and complex network structures that facilitate rapid diffusion but also maintain distinct varieties through compartmentalization. Low-productivity environments support sparse populations with limited connectivity, preserving archaic features through isolation but limiting innovation spread.
Topographic complexity (mountainous terrain) creates barriers to interaction that maintain linguistic diversity by preventing convergence, while flat, homogeneous terrain facilitates communication and standardization . When populations exceed local carrying capacity, fission-fusion dynamics create new speech communities through migration or splitting, resetting C_t trajectories for resulting subpopulations as they adapt to new ecological and social niches.
5. Multilingualism and Code-Switching Dynamics
5.1 Markedness and Language Choice
5.1.1 Myers-Scotton's Markedness Model
Myers-Scotton's Markedness Model provides a rational-choice framework for understanding how speakers navigate multilingual repertoires by selecting codes based on cost-benefit analyses of markedness readings . The model posits that speakers optimize interactional outcomes by choosing unmarked (expected) codes for specific contexts or marked (unexpected) codes to achieve special effects such as emphasis, social distance, or identity assertion. Unmarked choices align with the "rights and obligations sets" governing specific interactions, requiring no special social accounting, while marked choices carry social meaning through deviation from expectations .
This framework has profound implications for C_t trajectories: a form moving from marked to unmarked status undergoes normalization and increases in repertoire status, while a form becoming marked loses status. The model distinguishes between situational code-switching (predictable changes aligned with external situation shifts) and metaphorical code-switching (unpredictable changes redefining the interactional situation itself) . For example, Flemish-French bilingual employees switching to French for formal business but Flemish for casual conversation exhibit situational switching, while a bilingual in Papua New Guinea switching between Tok Pisin and Buang to demonstrate dual identity exhibits metaphorical switching . These switching types create distinct trajectory patterns; forms restricted to situational switching have stable but limited C_t confined to specific domains, while forms used in metaphorical switching have fluid status capable of indexing multiple social meanings.
5.1.2 Code-Switching as Repertoire Management
Code-switching represents sophisticated repertoire management where speakers deploy their full range of linguistic resources to achieve social and interactional goals, rather than mere interference or language deficiency . In highly multilingual contexts such as South African townships, speakers may draw on multiple languages (Zulu, Sotho, Afrikaans, English) within single conversations, creating complex repertoires where the status of any given form is context-dependent and interactionally negotiated.
Research on Tsotsitaal and Iscamtho—urban vernaculars spoken in South Africa—illustrates how code-switching configurations create new repertoire statuses . Tsotsitaal typically employs Afrikaans as the matrix language with lexical insertions from Bantu languages, while Iscamtho uses Zulu or Sotho as the matrix. These varieties represent stabilized code-switching patterns that have acquired distinct social meanings: Tsotsitaal historically indexed masculinity and criminal subcultures, while Iscamtho functions as a stylistic variety potentially becoming a home language . The matrix language (providing grammatical structure) typically maintains higher C_t than the embedded language (providing content morphemes), constraining the trajectory of embedded forms unless they achieve sufficient frequency to challenge the matrix grammar.
5.1.3 Contextual Factors and Situational Shifts
Contextual factors—including setting, topic, and participant relationships—determine the markedness values of linguistic forms and thereby drive C_t trajectories through domain allocation . Diglossic situations exhibit rigid compartmentalization where High varieties dominate formal domains (religion, education, media) and Low varieties dominate informal domains (home, friendship networks), creating stable bifurcation in C_t across contexts. Code-switching communities exhibit more fluid boundaries, with C_t shifting dynamically within interactions based on the negotiation of rights and obligations.
The trajectory of C_t for forms in multilingual contexts depends on their ability to expand across domains. Forms initially restricted to specific contexts (e.g., technical vocabulary in professional settings) may increase in C_t through domain expansion, acquiring new social meanings and functional loads. Conversely, forms may undergo domain contraction, declining in C_t as they become restricted to fewer contexts or specific ritual uses (e.g., religious language). These shifts are driven by changes in the social evaluation of the languages involved, alterations in the demographic composition of speech communities, and institutional support or suppression of specific varieties.

Gumperz's concept of linguistic range—the internal distance between constituent varieties in a community's repertoire—determines the flexibility and complexity of C_t trajectories . High isolation, reinforced by geographical, social, or cultural barriers, leads to distinct linguistic characteristics and conservative C_t trajectories with high resistance to external innovations. Low isolation (high connectivity) facilitates repertoire expansion through borrowing but may trigger convergence and reduction in range as distinct varieties accommodate to one another. The relationship between isolation and C_t is nonlinear; moderate isolation may maintain distinctiveness while allowing selective borrowing, while extreme isolation may lead to rapid divergence or archaic preservation depending on internal community dynamics.

The competition between language maintenance (preserving heritage language C_t) and language shift (declining heritage C_t in favor of dominant languages) represents a fundamental dynamic in multilingual repertoire trajectories . Maintenance occurs when communities preserve distinct repertoire profiles despite pressure from majority languages, typically requiring institutional support, demographic concentration, and high language loyalty (low volatility in Abrams-Strogatz terms) . The trajectory of shift follows a progression from bilingualism through semi-speaker stages (heritage language attrition) to complete replacement, with the rate determined by social factors such as economic opportunity, educational policy, and identity maintenance motivations . The conditions for reversal include changes in social network composition (marriage, workplace integration), shifts in language ideology (reduced stigma, increased valorization), and institutional support (education, media presence), demonstrating that C_t trajectories are potentially reversible given appropriate ecological interventions.

      <!-- Introduction -->
      <section id="introduction" class="py-16 px-8 bg-slate-50">
        <div class="max-w-4xl mx-auto">
          <div class="highlight-box rounded-lg p-8 mb-12">
            <h2 class="font-canela text-3xl font-light mb-6 text-slate-900">
              Understanding Repertoire Status Through Time
            </h2>
            <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-4">
              The trajectory of linguistic forms within speech communities—captured by repertoire status C_t—represents one of the most fundamental yet complex phenomena in sociolinguistics. This framework explores why linguistic forms follow the trajectories they do, moving beyond descriptive patterns to uncover the underlying mechanisms that drive change.
            </p>
            <p class="leading-relaxed">
              Drawing from <a href="https://qspace.qu.edu.qa/bitstream/handle/10576/8072/Speech%20community-a%20critique%20of%20labov%27s%20definition.pdf;sequence=8" class="citation-link">Labovian foundations</a>, mathematical ecology, and complex systems theory, we examine how social networks, prestige systems, and cognitive constraints interact to produce the characteristic S-curves, peaks, and volatility observed in linguistic diffusion.
            </p>
          </div>
        </div>
      </section>

      <!-- Foundational Concepts -->
      <section id="foundational-concepts" class="py-16 px-8">
        <div class="max-w-6xl mx-auto">
          <h2 class="font-canela text-4xl font-light mb-12 text-center text-slate-900">
            Foundational Concepts &amp; Definitions
          </h2>

          <!-- Speech Community as Dynamic System -->
          <div id="speech-community" class="mb-16">
            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-8 text-slate-800">Speech Community as a Dynamic System</h3>

            <div class="grid grid-cols-1 lg:grid-cols-2 gap-8 mb-8">
              <div>
                <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4 text-slate-800">Labovian Framework</h4>
                <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                  The <a href="https://qspace.qu.edu.qa/bitstream/handle/10576/8072/Speech%20community-a%20critique%20of%20labov%27s%20definition.pdf;sequence=8" class="citation-link">Labovian framework</a> establishes the speech community as the fundamental unit of sociolinguistic analysis, defined not by structural uniformity but by <strong>participation in shared norms</strong> governing linguistic evaluation and use.
                </p>
                <p class="leading-relaxed">
                  This perspective distinguishes between <strong>change from above</strong>—conscious adoption of prestigious forms—and <strong>change from below</strong>—unconscious propagation through dense social networks.
                </p>
              </div>

              <div>
                <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4 text-slate-800">Gumperz &amp; Hymes: Interactional Dimensions</h4>
                <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                  <a href="https://www.academypublication.com/issues/past/jltr/vol04/06/23.pdf" class="citation-link">John Gumperz</a> defined speech communities through <strong>regular and frequent interaction</strong>, emphasizing that repertoire status depends on the density of social connections rather than static linguistic inventories.
                </p>
                <p class="leading-relaxed">
                  <a href="https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED206199.pdf" class="citation-link">Dell Hymes&#39;s</a> ethnography of communication framework further reveals that individuals navigate multiple communities simultaneously, managing multiple C_t values as they shift between repertoires.
                </p>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>

          <div class="section-divider"></div>

          <!-- Defining Repertoire Status -->
          <div id="repertoire-status" class="mb-16">
            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-8 text-slate-800">Defining Repertoire Status (C_t)</h3>

            <div class="highlight-box rounded-lg p-8 mb-8">
              <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Status as Proportion and Prestige</h4>
              <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                <strong>Repertoire status (C_t)</strong> represents a multidimensional construct capturing both <strong>proportional distribution</strong> (frequency of use) and <strong>social valuation</strong> (prestige) within a speech community at time <em>t</em>.
              </p>
              <p class="leading-relaxed">
                Research on <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prestige_(sociolinguistics)" class="citation-link">Arabic dialects</a> illustrates this complexity: investigations of variable (q) in Jordanian Arabic revealed speakers adopting both standard [q] and prestigious urban variants simultaneously, demonstrating bidirectional repertoire expansion.
              </p>
            </div>

            <div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-8">
              <div class="bg-slate-50 rounded-lg p-6">
                <h5 class="font-semibold mb-3 text-slate-800">Overt Prestige</h5>
                <ul class="space-y-2 text-sm">
                  <li>• Standard varieties</li>
                  <li>• Institutional associations</li>
                  <li>• Educational contexts</li>
                  <li>• Media reinforcement</li>
                </ul>
              </div>

              <div class="bg-slate-50 rounded-lg p-6">
                <h5 class="font-semibold mb-3 text-slate-800">Covert Prestige</h5>
                <ul class="space-y-2 text-sm">
                  <li>• Local varieties</li>
                  <li>• Solidarity indexing</li>
                  <li>• Group membership</li>
                  <li>• Authenticity markers</li>
                </ul>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </section>

      <!-- Mathematical Models -->
      <section id="mathematical-models" class="py-16 px-8 bg-slate-50">
        <div class="max-w-6xl mx-auto">
          <h2 class="font-canela text-4xl font-light mb-12 text-center text-slate-900">
            Mathematical Models of Trajectory Formation
          </h2>

          <!-- S-Curve Model -->
          <div id="diffusion-models" class="mb-16">
            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-8 text-slate-800">The S-Curve Model of Linguistic Change</h3>

            <div class="bg-white rounded-lg p-8 shadow-sm mb-8">
              <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Logistic Growth Function</h4>
              <div class="bg-slate-100 rounded-lg p-6 mb-4">
                <p class="text-center text-lg font-mono">C_t = L / (1 + e<sup>-k(t-t₀)</sup>)</p>
              </div>
              <div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4 text-sm">
                <div class="text-center">
                  <strong>L</strong> - Carrying capacity
                </div>
                <div class="text-center">
                  <strong>k</strong> - Growth rate parameter
                </div>
                <div class="text-center">
                  <strong>t₀</strong> - Inflection point
                </div>
              </div>
              <p class="text-sm text-slate-600 mt-4">
                Source: <a href="https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-variation-and-change/article/modeling-language-change-across-the-lifespan-individual-trajectories-in-community-change/41CB5D788EAE99FD1831D44848DE77D9" class="citation-link">Language Variation and Change</a>
              </p>
            </div>

            <p class="leading-relaxed mb-6">
              The <a href="https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/Courses/ParisPapers/WeinreichLabovHerzog.pdf" class="citation-link">S-curve model</a> represents the canonical mathematical description of linguistic diffusion, positing that successful innovations follow a characteristic trajectory: slow initial adoption, rapid acceleration, and final deceleration.
            </p>

            <div class="highlight-box rounded-lg p-6">
              <h5 class="font-semibold mb-3">Empirical Validation</h5>
              <p class="leading-relaxed">
                The <a href="https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-variation-and-change/article/modeling-language-change-across-the-lifespan-individual-trajectories-in-community-change/41CB5D788EAE99FD1831D44848DE77D9" class="citation-link">Corpus of Early English Correspondence</a> confirms this pattern in historical changes such as the shift from third person *-th* to *-s* and the replacement of second person *ye* with *you*.
              </p>
            </div>
          </div>

          <div class="section-divider"></div>

          <!-- Competition Dynamics -->
          <div id="competition-dynamics" class="mb-16">
            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-8 text-slate-800">Competition Dynamics</h3>

            <div class="grid grid-cols-1 lg:grid-cols-2 gap-8">
              <div>
                <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Lotka-Volterra Equations</h4>
                <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                  Adapted from ecological species competition, these equations model how multiple linguistic forms compete for functional space within a repertoire, predicting competitive exclusion, stable coexistence, or unstable oscillations.
                </p>
                <div class="bg-slate-100 rounded-lg p-4 text-sm font-mono">
                  dC₁/dt = r₁C₁(1 - (C₁ + α₁₂C₂)/K)
                  <br/>
                  dC₂/dt = r₂C₂(1 - (C₂ + α₂₁C₁)/K)
                </div>
              </div>

              <div>
                <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Abrams-Strogatz Model</h4>
                <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                  This model formalizes language competition through transition probabilities incorporating prestige and volatility parameters, revealing that <strong>prestige alone does not determine survival</strong>.
                </p>
                <p class="leading-relaxed text-sm">
                  <a href="https://digital.csic.es/bitstream/10261/115765/1/models_language_competition_Castello.pdf" class="citation-link">Analysis</a> shows that the volatility parameter critically determines whether languages can coexist or one will dominate.
                </p>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>

          <div class="section-divider"></div>

          <!-- Agent-Based Models -->
          <div id="agent-based-models" class="mb-16">
            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-8 text-slate-800">Agent-Based &amp; Simulation Approaches</h3>

            <div class="bg-white rounded-lg p-8 shadow-sm mb-8">
              <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-6">Network Structure Effects</h4>
              <div class="overflow-x-auto">
                <table class="w-full text-sm">
                  <thead>
                    <tr class="border-b">
                      <th class="text-left py-2">Network Type</th>
                      <th class="text-left py-2">Density</th>
                      <th class="text-left py-2">Initial Growth</th>
                      <th class="text-left py-2">Saturation</th>
                    </tr>
                  </thead>
                  <tbody>
                    <tr class="border-b">
                      <td class="py-2 font-medium">Dense/Closed</td>
                      <td>High</td>
                      <td>Fast (local)</td>
                      <td>Low (local maxima)</td>
                    </tr>
                    <tr class="border-b">
                      <td class="py-2 font-medium">Loose/Open</td>
                      <td>Low</td>
                      <td>Slow</td>
                      <td>High</td>
                    </tr>
                    <tr class="border-b">
                      <td class="py-2 font-medium">Centralized</td>
                      <td>Variable</td>
                      <td>Fast (hub-dependent)</td>
                      <td>Moderate</td>
                    </tr>
                  </tbody>
                </table>
              </div>
              <p class="text-sm text-slate-600 mt-4">
                Source: <a href="https://pages.ucsd.edu/~eclem/clem-2017-social-networks.pdf" class="citation-link">Social Networks in Linguistic Change</a>
              </p>
            </div>

            <p class="leading-relaxed">
              <a href="https://pages.ucsd.edu/~eclem/clem-2017-social-networks.pdf" class="citation-link">Agent-based models</a> reveal that network structure significantly affects both the rate and qualitative dynamics of linguistic change. Dense networks facilitate rapid local convergence but may resist external innovations, while loose networks with numerous weak ties enable broader diffusion.
            </p>
          </div>
        </div>
      </section>

      <!-- Social Drivers -->
      <section id="social-drivers" class="py-16 px-8">
        <div class="max-w-6xl mx-auto">
          <h2 class="font-canela text-4xl font-light mb-12 text-center text-slate-900">
            Social &amp; Cognitive Drivers of Status Change
          </h2>

          <!-- Prestige and Social Stratification -->
          <div id="prestige-stratification" class="mb-16">
            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-8 text-slate-800">Prestige and Social Stratification</h3>

            <div class="grid grid-cols-1 lg:grid-cols-2 gap-8 mb-8">
              <div class="highlight-box rounded-lg p-6">
                <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Overt vs. Covert Prestige</h4>
                <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                  <strong>Overt prestige</strong> drives &#34;top-down&#34; diffusion of standard language features through conscious adoption, producing smooth, low-volatility trajectories supported by institutional reinforcement.
                </p>
                <p class="leading-relaxed">
                  <strong>Covert prestige</strong> drives &#34;bottom-up&#34; dynamics where local working-class speech patterns serve as innovation sources, creating counter-trajectories with more volatile responsiveness to local social dynamics.
                </p>
              </div>

              <div class="bg-white border rounded-lg p-6">
                <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Acts of Identity</h4>
                <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                  The <a href="http://ndl.ethernet.edu.et/bitstream/123456789/57693/1/198pdf.pdf" class="citation-link">acts of identity framework</a> posits that speakers use linguistic forms to project specific social identities, with cumulative effects driving community-level C_t trajectories.
                </p>
                <p class="leading-relaxed text-sm">
                  Every linguistic choice constitutes an identity act positioning the speaker within the social landscape.
                </p>
              </div>
            </div>

            <div class="bg-slate-50 rounded-lg p-8">
              <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-6">Social Forces Shaping Trajectories</h4>
              <div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-3 gap-6">
                <div>
                  <h5 class="font-medium mb-2 text-slate-800">Social Class</h5>
                  <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed">
                    Linguistic changes often originate in the upper working class or lower middle class, revealing <a href="http://ndl.ethernet.edu.et/bitstream/123456789/57693/1/198pdf.pdf" class="citation-link">curvilinear patterns</a> of innovation.
                  </p>
                </div>
                <div>
                  <h5 class="font-medium mb-2 text-slate-800">Gender</h5>
                  <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed">
                    Women often lead changes toward both standard variants and innovative local forms, utilizing <a href="https://web.stanford.edu/class/linguist156/Gal_1978.pdf" class="citation-link">linguistic resources for identity construction</a>.
                  </p>
                </div>
                <div>
                  <h5 class="font-medium mb-2 text-slate-800">Ethnicity</h5>
                  <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed">
                    Structures C_t through language maintenance, shift, and development of ethnolects as markers of <a href="https://fiveable.me/language-culture/unit-4/social-factors-influencing-language/study-guide/ihSdWcfjr8ep0SMh" class="citation-link">group identity</a>.
                  </p>
                </div>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>

          <div class="section-divider"></div>

          <!-- Social Networks -->
          <div id="social-networks" class="mb-16">
            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-8 text-slate-800">Social Networks and Communities of Practice</h3>

            <div class="mb-8">
              <img src="https://kimi-web-img.moonshot.cn/img/images.pexels.com/81d5e9d7a9a4ab8a0069df44adecc87bb977a40c.jpeg" alt="Adolescent social groups in a school setting" class="w-full h-64 object-cover rounded-lg shadow-sm mb-4" size="medium" aspect="wide" style="photo" query="high school social groups" referrerpolicy="no-referrer" data-modified="1" data-score="0.00"/>
              <p class="text-sm text-slate-600 text-center">
                Communities of practice serve as incubators for linguistic innovation
              </p>
            </div>

            <div class="grid grid-cols-1 lg:grid-cols-2 gap-8">
              <div>
                <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Network Density and Weak Ties</h4>
                <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                  <a href="https://coratcoretku.wordpress.com/2016/06/27/speech-community-network-intersecting-communities-repertoires/" class="citation-link">Dense networks</a> facilitate rapid local convergence but may resist external innovations, while weak ties serve as crucial conduits for innovation diffusion beyond origin communities.
                </p>
                <p class="leading-relaxed">
                  This creates a <strong>two-step flow of influence</strong>: innovations enter through weak ties (often via hub individuals) and then spread through dense strong-tie networks.
                </p>
              </div>

              <div>
                <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Communities of Practice</h4>
                <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                  <a href="https://www.academypublication.com/issues/past/jltr/vol04/06/23.pdf" class="citation-link">Communities of practice</a> represent micro-social units where C_t trajectories are immediately negotiated and enacted, serving as incubators for innovation.
                </p>
                <p class="leading-relaxed">
                  Eckert&#39;s research demonstrates how adolescent groups like &#34;jocks&#34; and &#34;burnouts&#34; develop distinct linguistic profiles that diverge from the adult community, creating trajectories where C_t increases rapidly within the group before potential outward diffusion.
                </p>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>

          <div class="section-divider"></div>

          <!-- Cognitive Factors -->
          <div id="cognitive-factors" class="mb-16">
            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-8 text-slate-800">Cognitive and Cultural Factors</h3>

            <div class="highlight-box rounded-lg p-8 mb-8">
              <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-4">Cultural Attractors and Cognitive Alignment</h4>
              <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                <a href="https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cogs.13183" class="citation-link">Cultural attractors</a> represent stable points in linguistic form space that accumulate speakers due to shared cognitive biases, creating population-level convergence despite individual variation.
              </p>
              <p class="leading-relaxed">
                These attractors emerge from the interaction between universal cognitive constraints (perceptual salience, memory limitations, processing efficiency) and social transmission dynamics.
              </p>
            </div>

            <div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-8">
              <div class="bg-white border rounded-lg p-6">
                <h4 class="font-semibold mb-3">Critical Period Effects</h4>
                <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed">
                  <a href="https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cogs.13183" class="citation-link">Short, critical periods</a> of learning early in life enhance repertoire stability by constraining plasticity, while long learning times may destabilize repertoires through excessive drift.
                </p>
              </div>

              <div class="bg-white border rounded-lg p-6">
                <h4 class="font-semibold mb-3">Resistance to Change</h4>
                <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed">
                  <a href="https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/Courses/ParisPapers/WeinreichLabovHerzog.pdf" class="citation-link">Resistance mechanisms</a> including functional load, social marking, and entrenchment create friction in C_t trajectories, explaining why linguistic changes typically proceed gradually through S-curves.
                </p>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </section>

      <!-- Ecological Systems -->
      <section id="ecological-systems" class="py-16 px-8 bg-slate-50">
        <div class="max-w-6xl mx-auto">
          <h2 class="font-canela text-4xl font-light mb-12 text-center text-slate-900">
            Ecological &amp; Complex Systems Perspectives
          </h2>

          <div class="grid grid-cols-1 lg:grid-cols-2 gap-8 mb-12">
            <div>
              <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-6 text-slate-800">Bronfenbrenner&#39;s Ecological Systems Theory</h3>
              <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">

                This perspective recognizes that C_t is simultaneously shaped by face-to-face accommodation, network bridging, institutional policies, and national language ideologies.

          <h2 class="font-canela text-4xl font-light mb-12 text-center text-slate-900">

          <div class="mb-8">

            <p class="text-sm text-slate-600 text-center">
              Multilingual repertoires create complex C_t dynamics through code-switching and domain allocation
            </p>
          </div>

          <div class="grid grid-cols-1 lg:grid-cols-2 gap-8 mb-12">
            <div class="highlight-box rounded-lg p-8">
              <h3 class="font-canela text-xl font-light mb-6 text-slate-800">Myers-Scotton&#39;s Markedness Model</h3>
              <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                <a href="https://ira.lib.polyu.edu.hk/bitstream/10397/96923/1/Angel_Lin_DCS%20Li_2012_Code-switching_Routledge%20Hbk%20Mlm_pp.470-481.pdf" class="citation-link">The Markedness Model</a> provides a rational-choice framework where speakers optimize interactional outcomes by choosing unmarked (expected) codes or marked (unexpected) codes for special effects.
              </p>
              <div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">
                <div class="bg-white rounded p-4">
                  <h5 class="font-medium mb-2">Unmarked Choices</h5>
                  <ul class="text-sm space-y-1">
                    <li>• Align with expectations</li>
                    <li>• No special accounting</li>
                    <li>• Context-appropriate</li>
                  </ul>
                </div>
                <div class="bg-white rounded p-4">
                  <h5 class="font-medium mb-2">Marked Choices</h5>
                  <ul class="text-sm space-y-1">
                    <li>• Deviate from norms</li>
                    <li>• Carry social meaning</li>
                    <li>• Identity assertion</li>
                  </ul>
                </div>
              </div>
            </div>

            <div class="bg-white border rounded-lg p-8">
              <h3 class="font-canela text-xl font-light mb-6 text-slate-800">Repertoire Management</h3>
              <p class="leading-relaxed mb-4">
                <a href="http://ndl.ethernet.edu.et/bitstream/123456789/17455/1/18.pdf.pdf" class="citation-link">Code-switching represents sophisticated repertoire management</a> where speakers deploy their full linguistic resources to achieve social goals, rather than interference or deficiency.
              </p>
              <p class="leading-relaxed text-sm">
                Research on Tsotsitaal and Iscamtho in South Africa illustrates how code-switching configurations create new repertoire statuses with distinct social meanings.
              </p>
            </div>
          </div>

          <div class="bg-slate-50 rounded-lg p-8">
            <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-6">Maintenance vs. Shift in Contact Situations</h4>
            <div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-6">
              <div>
                <h5 class="font-medium mb-3 text-slate-800">Language Maintenance</h5>
                <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed mb-3">
                  Occurs when communities preserve distinct repertoire profiles despite majority pressure, requiring institutional support, demographic concentration, and high language loyalty.
                </p>
                <ul class="text-sm space-y-1">
                  <li>• Institutional support</li>
                  <li>• Demographic concentration</li>
                  <li>• High language loyalty</li>
                  <li>• Identity maintenance</li>
                </ul>
              </div>
              <div>
                <h5 class="font-medium mb-3 text-slate-800">Language Shift</h5>
                <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed mb-3">
                  Typically follows a three-generation pattern through bilingualism to semi-speaker stages to complete replacement, driven by economic opportunity and educational policy.
                </p>
                <ul class="text-sm space-y-1">
                  <li>• 1st gen: Bilingualism</li>
                  <li>• 2nd gen: Semi-speaker</li>
                  <li>• 3rd gen: Monolingualism</li>
                  <li>• Reversal possible</li>
                </ul>
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>

            <h3 class="font-canela text-2xl font-light mb-6 text-slate-800">Interacting Causal Mechanisms</h3>
            <p class="leading-relaxed mb-6">
              The trajectory of C_t emerges from the <strong>multiplicative interaction</strong> of social network structure, prestige systems, and demographic composition, with no single factor determining outcomes independently. <a href="https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-variation-and-change/article/modeling-language-change-across-the-lifespan-individual-trajectories-in-community-change/41CB5D788EAE99FD1831D44848DE77D9" class="citation-link">Mathematical models</a> must incorporate these cross-level interactions through hierarchical specifications.
            </p>

            <div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-3 gap-6">
              <div class="bg-white rounded p-4">
                <h5 class="font-medium mb-2">Social Networks × Prestige</h5>
                <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed">
                  Network structure determines pathways through which prestige operates; high-prestige variants spread rapidly through well-connected networks but may stall when encountering dense, low-prestige subgroups.
                </p>
              </div>
              <div class="bg-white rounded p-4">
                <h5 class="font-medium mb-2">Individual × Community</h5>
                <p class="text-sm leading-relaxed">

                </p>
              </div>
              <div class="bg-white rounded p-4">
                <h5 class="font-medium mb-2">Cognitive × Social</h5>

                </p>
