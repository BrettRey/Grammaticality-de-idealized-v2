# Notes on O'Connor (2019) - Chapter 6: The Cultural Red Queen and The Cultural Red King

### 1. EVOLUTIONARY SPEED
*   **The Red Queen Effect (Biology):** "It takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place." Fast evolution helps in competitive arms races (predator/prey).
*   **The Red King Effect (Mutualism):** Sometimes it is better to evolve *slowly*.
    *   *Logic:* If I am slow and stubborn, you are forced to adapt to me. (Schelling's example: throwing the steering wheel out the window in a game of Chicken forces the other driver to swerve).

### 2. MINORITY STATUS AND SPEED
*   **Asymmetry:** In a minority/majority population (e.g., 10% Type A, 90% Type B), Type A meets Type B much more often than B meets A.
*   **Learning Speed:** Therefore, the minority group learns how to interact with the majority *faster* than the majority learns to interact with them. The minority is more **reactive**.

### 3. THE CULTURAL RED KING IN BARGAINING
*   **The Result:** In bargaining games (Nash Demand Game), the *slower* group (the Majority) often wins.
    *   *Mechanism:* The Minority quickly learns to "best respond" to the Majority's initial random blunders. If the Majority is accidentally demanding High, the Minority quickly learns to demand Low to avoid conflict. The Majority, being slow and oblivious, stays High. The convention solidifies with the Minority disadvantaged.
*   **Risk Aversion:** If actors are risk-averse, the Red King effect is strengthened. Minorities rush to the "safe" Low demand.

### 4. INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY
*   **Network Density:** Groups with better networks (e.g., "Old Boy Networks") share information effectively. This makes them *less* reactive to individual bad luck (they have more data points).
*   **Result:** Being less reactive (having better memory) acts like the Red King. The well-networked group acts stubborn, forcing the less-networked group to accommodate them.

### 5. INTERSECTIONAL OPPRESSION
*   **The Model:** Actors have two identities (e.g., Race and Gender).
    *   *Scenario:* Black women are a minority in both categories.
*   **Weak Intersectionality:** If learning happens separately for race and gender, Black women suffer the sum of the disadvantages.
*   **Strong Intersectionality:** If actors only imitate their specific intersectional type (e.g., Black women only imitate Black women), the groups are smaller, the Red King effect is stronger, and the disadvantage is compounded.
*   **Conclusion:** Intersectionality isn't just additive; dynamical forces can make multiply-disadvantaged groups suffer disproportionately.
