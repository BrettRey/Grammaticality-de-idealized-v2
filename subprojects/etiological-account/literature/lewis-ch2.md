# Notes on David Lewis (1969) - Chapter II: Convention Refined

### 1. COMMON KNOWLEDGE
*   **The Problem:** How do we justify higher-order expectations? (I expect you to expect me to...).
*   **Definition:** Something is *common knowledge* in $P$ if there is a state of affairs $A$ such that:
    1.  Everyone in $P$ has reason to believe $A$ holds.
    2.  $A$ indicates to everyone in $P$ that everyone has reason to believe $A$ holds.
    3.  $A$ indicates to everyone in $P$ that ___.
*   **Result:** Common knowledge allows for an infinite (but usually truncated) chain of mutual expectations.

### 2. REFINED DEFINITION OF CONVENTION
A regularity $R$ in the behavior of members of a population $P$ in situation $S$ is a **convention** if and only if it is true that, and it is common knowledge in $P$ that, in almost any instance of $S$:
1.  Almost everyone conforms to $R$.
2.  Almost everyone expects almost everyone else to conform to $R$.
3.  Almost everyone has approximately the same preferences.
4.  Almost everyone prefers that any one more conform to $R$, on condition that almost everyone conform to $R$ (Proper Coordination Equilibrium).
5.  There is a possible alternative regularity $R'$ that also meets these conditions (Arbitrariness).

### 3. ALTERNATIVES TO CONVENTIONS
*   **Arbitrariness:** If there is no alternative $R'$, it’s not a convention—it’s just the logical thing to do (e.g., breathing).
*   **Incompatibility:** $R$ and $R'$ must be different enough that you can't conform to both at once.

### 4. DEGREES OF CONVENTION
*   Lewis admits that real-world conventions aren't perfect. He provides a quantitative version of the definition (using fractions/probabilities) to allow for "degrees of conventionality."
*   A regularity can be "more or less conventional" depending on how many people conform and how strongly they expect others to.

### 5. KNOWLEDGE OF CONVENTIONS
*   To be party to a convention is to *know* (in some sense) that it exists.
*   **Implicit Knowledge:** This knowledge doesn't have to be verbal or explicit. It can be like the "knowledge" a bicycle rider has of the laws of physics—manifested in behavior and expectations rather than in a textbook.
