# Notes on David Lewis (1969) - Convention: A Philosophical Study

## Chapter I: Coordination and Convention

### 1. COORDINATION PROBLEMS
*   **Definition:** A situation where two or more agents must choose actions such that the outcome depends on the actions of all.
*   **Key Feature:** The agents have a common interest in matching their actions (coordination) but there are multiple ways to do so.
*   **Example (1): Meeting.** You and I want to meet. We succeed if we go to the same place. It matters little *where* we meet, as long as we both go to the same place.
*   **Example (2): Cut-off Phone Call.** If a call is cut off, we want to restore it. We succeed if one calls back while the other waits. If both call or both wait, we fail.
*   **Example (11): Language.** We want to communicate. We succeed if we use the same language. It matters little *which* language (English, Welsh, etc.), as long as we are uniform.

### 2. COORDINATION EQUILIBRIA
*   **Equilibrium:** A combination of actions where no agent would have been better off acting differently, *given* what the others did.
*   **Coordination Equilibrium:** A combination in which no one would have been better off had *any* one agent alone acted otherwise (including someone else). It is a state of mutual best-response.
*   **Multiplicity:** Coordination problems must have at least two proper coordination equilibria to be non-trivial.

### 3. SOLVING COORDINATION PROBLEMS
*   **Agreement:** The most obvious way (e.g., "Let's meet at the clock tower").
*   **Salience:** In the absence of agreement, agents look for an equilibrium that "stands out" (the focal point).
    *   *Uniqueness:* A place or action that is unique in a conspicuous way.
    *   *Precedent:* The most important source of salience. "We did it this way last time, so we'll do it again."
*   **Higher-Order Expectations:** To solve the problem, I must expect you to do X. To expect you to do X, I must expect *you* to expect *me* to do Y. This leads to a chain of "I expect that you expect that I expect..."

### 4. DEFINITION OF CONVENTION (FIRST PASS)
*   A regularity $R$ in behavior is a convention if:
    1.  Everyone conforms to $R$.
    2.  Everyone expects everyone else to conform to $R$.
    3.  Everyone prefers to conform to $R$ on condition that others do (coordination equilibrium).
    4.  There is at least one other regularity $R'$ that would also have worked (arbitrariness).
