# Notes on O'Connor (2019) - Chapter 3: Social Categories, Coordination, and Inequity

### 1. THE FUNCTION OF SOCIAL CATEGORIES
*   **Efficiency:** As argued in Ch 2, categories allow populations to solve complementary coordination problems efficiently by assigning roles based on type.
*   **Inequity:** This efficiency comes at a cost. If the roles are unequal (e.g., Leader vs. Follower, CEO vs. Clerk), one type captures the benefit.
*   **The Trap:** Even the disadvantaged type prefers the inequitable coordination to the chaos of non-coordination. (Better to be a Follower than to crash).

### 2. REAL-WORLD CATEGORIES
*   **Markers:** Humans use observable markers (sex, age, race, dress) to categorize others.
*   **Type-Conditioning:** We treat people differently based on these categories.
    *   *Thin Definition:* In O'Connor's models, "type-conditioning" just means having a different strategy for different types. It doesn't require animus or complex psychology.
*   **Primary Categories:** Gender, Age, and Race are the "primary categories" used cross-culturally to organize social life (Ridgeway).

### 3. MODELING TYPES
*   **Tags:** In game theory, types are often modeled as "tags" (arbitrary labels).
*   **Pre-Play Signaling:** Types function like pre-play signals, but they are fixed (you can't easily change your sex or race to get a better payoff).

### 4. COORDINATION AND DISCRIMINATION
*   **Homogeneous Groups:** In a Leader-Follower game, a homogeneous group ends up with a mixed strategy where everyone leads 2/3 of the time. Average payoff: 2/3.
*   **Typed Groups:** With types, one group can become "Leaders" and the other "Followers."
    *   *Payoffs:* Leaders get 2. Followers get 1.
    *   *Comparison:* Both do better than the homogeneous average (2/3), so the convention is stable.
*   **The Result:** **Inequity emerges from the drive for efficiency.** We get a discriminatory convention (Men lead, Women follow) not because men are better leaders, but because the categories allowed the group to settle on *an* equilibrium, and it happened to be this one.

### 5. ALTERNATIVE SOLUTIONS
*   **Correlated Equilibrium:** Using an external event (traffic light) to coordinate. (Hard to find universal traffic lights for social interaction).
*   **Uncorrelated Asymmetry:** Using properties like "Owner" vs. "Intruder" to break symmetry (Maynard Smith).
*   **Types as Asymmetry:** Social categories function exactly like uncorrelated asymmetries. They are "information" used to coordinate.
