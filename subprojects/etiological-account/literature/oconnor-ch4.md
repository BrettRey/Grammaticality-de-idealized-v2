# Notes on O'Connor (2019) - Chapter 4: Cultural Evolution with Social Categories

### 1. EVOLUTIONARY DYNAMICS
*   **The Approach:** Using **Replicator Dynamics** to model how conventions emerge over time. Strategies that yield higher payoffs (utility/success) spread in the population.
*   **Rational Choice Comparison:** Unlike rational choice models (Becker), evolutionary models don't require agents to calculate future marriage market returns. They just repeat what works or copy the successful.

### 2. EVOLVING DIVISION OF LABOR
*   **Homogeneous Populations:** In the Dancing Game, a homogeneous population evolves to a **polymorphic state** (50% play A, 50% play B). This is inefficient (mis-coordination happens 50% of the time).
*   **Perfectly Divided Populations (Types):**
    *   *Scenario:* Men meet Women.
    *   *Result:* The population rapidly evolves to one of two stable equilibria: All Men A/All Women B, or All Men B/All Women A.
    *   *Efficiency:* Coordination is perfect (Payoff = 1).
    *   *Symmetry Breaking:* Which outcome occurs is random (history-dependent), but *an* asymmetric outcome is guaranteed.
*   **Basins of Attraction:** In symmetric games, each outcome is equally likely.

### 3. THE LEADER-FOLLOWER GAME
*   **Conflict:** What if one role is better (Leader)?
*   **Result:** Evolution still drives the population to a strict division. One group becomes Leaders, the other Followers.
*   **Inequity:** This confirms that **cultural evolution naturally generates inequality** when types are available to break symmetry in complementary coordination games.

### 4. ROBUSTNESS
*   **Generalizability:** O'Connor cites other models (Axtell, Young, Henrich & Boyd) showing these results are robust.
*   **Bounded Rationality:** Even with limited memory or "best response," typed populations fall into inequitable but efficient equilibria.
*   **Type-Mixing:** Even if populations aren't perfectly divided (e.g., men interact with men *and* women), inequitable conventions still emerge in the inter-type interactions.

### 5. LEARNING FROM "THOSE LIKE US"
*   **Own-Type Bias:** Humans tend to imitate their own gender/type.
*   **Function:** This bias is adaptive because it helps actors learn the correct role for their type. (If women are expected to forage, a girl should copy successful women, not successful men).
*   **Reinforcement:** This creates a feedback loop that cements gender norms.

### 6. CONCLUSION
*   **The Explanandum Explained:** Why do all cultures divide labor by gender? Because cultural evolution drives groups to use the most available symmetry-breaker (gender) to solve the efficiency problem of complementary coordination. Inequity is a side effect of this drive for coordination.
