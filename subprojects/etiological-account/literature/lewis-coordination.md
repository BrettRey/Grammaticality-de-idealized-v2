# 1. COORDINATION PROBLEMS

### Definition
> "Use of language belongs to a class of situations with a conspicuous common character: situations I shall call *coordination problems*." (p. 5)

> "Two or more agents must each choose one of several alternative actions. Often all the agents have the same set of alternative actions, but that is not necessary. The outcomes the agents want to produce or prevent are determined jointly by the actions of all the agents. So the outcome of any action an agent might choose depends on the actions of the other agents." (p. 8)

> "We might say that coordination problems are situations in which several agents try to achieve uniformity of action by each doing whatever the others will do. But this is a dangerous thing to say... What is important about the uniform combinations we are interested in is not that they are—under some description—uniform, but that they are equilibria." (p. 12)

### Examples
Lewis provides several examples, including meeting places and phone calls. Crucially, he includes language:
> "(11) Suppose that with practice we could adopt any language in some wide range. It matters comparatively little to anyone (in the long run) what language he adopts, so long as he and those around him adopt the same language and can communicate easily. Each must choose what language to adopt according to his expectations about his neighbors' language: English among English speakers, Welsh among Welsh speakers, Esperanto among Esperanto speakers, and so on." (pp. 7-8)

**Relevance to Linguistic Conventions:**
Language is fundamentally a coordination problem because the "value" of a linguistic choice (e.g., using the sound "dog" to refer to a canine) depends entirely on others making the same choice. There is no inherent reason to choose one sound over another, only the mutual necessity of choosing the *same* one to ensure communication.

# 2. COORDINATION EQUILIBRIA

### Definition
> "Some combinations of the agents' chosen actions are *equilibria*: combinations in which each agent has done as well as he can given the actions of the other agents. In an equilibrium combination, no one agent could have produced an outcome more to his liking by acting differently, unless some of the others' actions also had been different. No one regrets his choice after he learns how the others chose." (p. 8)

### How they arise
> "Coordination equilibria are equilibria, by the definitions. Equilibria in games of pure coordination are always coordination equilibria, since the agents' interests coincide perfectly." (p. 14)

Lewis explains that agents choose based on their expectations of others:
> "Each must choose... according to his expectations about the others' choice, in order to [succeed] if and only if the other [does the same]." (p. 5)

**Relevance to Linguistic Conventions:**
A specific language (or a specific grammatical rule) represents a *coordination equilibrium*. If a community has settled on English, then for any individual, speaking English is the best strategy given that everyone else is speaking English. No individual has an incentive to unilaterally switch to Welsh, as they would fail to communicate. This stability characterizes linguistic conventions.

# 3. SALIENCE

### How some equilibria become focal
> "They try for a coordination equilibrium that is somehow *salient*: one that stands out from the rest by its uniqueness in some conspicuous respect. It does not have to be uniquely *good*; indeed, it could be uniquely bad. It merely has to be unique in some way the subjects will notice, expect each other to notice, and so on." (p. 35)

> "How can we explain coordination by salience? The subjects might all tend to pick the salient as a last resort, when they have no stronger ground for choice. Or they might expect each other to have that tendency, and act accordingly; or they might expect each other to expect each other to have that tendency and act accordingly; and so on." (pp. 35-36)

> "Indeed, precedent is merely the source of one important kind of salience: conspicuous uniqueness of an equilibrium because we reached it last time. We may tend to repeat the action that succeeded before if we have no strong reason to do otherwise." (p. 36)

**Relevance to Linguistic Conventions:**
Salience explains how a particular linguistic convention gets established when multiple options are arbitrary (e.g., why "dog" and not "ogd"). Once a term is used (precedent), it becomes salient for future use. This "conspicuous uniqueness" derived from history helps solve the coordination problem without need for explicit agreement, allowing conventions to evolve and persist simply because "that's how we did it last time."
