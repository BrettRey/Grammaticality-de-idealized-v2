### 1. LINGUISTIC CONVENTIONS
**How language is conventional**

> "I shall contend that the convention of language whereby the members of a population $P$ use a given possible language $\pounds$ may best be described as a convention of truthfulness in $\pounds$."
> (p. 177)

> "$\pounds$ is an actual language of $P$ if and only if there prevails in $P$ a convention of truthfulness in $\pounds$, sustained by an interest in communication."
> (p. 194)

> "A convention of truthfulness in $\pounds$ is a social contract as well as a convention. Not only does each prefer truthfulness in $\pounds$ by all to truthfulness in $\pounds$ by all but himself. Still more does each prefer uniform truthfulness in $\pounds$ to Babel, the state of nature."
> (p. 182)

### 2. MEANING AS CONVENTION
**Conventional form-meaning pairings**

> "By a language, I mean an interpreted language. So what I call a language is what many logicians would call a language plus an interpretation for it. ... Such a language, we recall, is a function $\pounds$ which assigns to every verbal expression in some finite set—every sentence of $\pounds$—an interpretation consisting of a mood $\mu$ and a truth condition $\tau$."
> (p. 162)

> "The convention of truthfulness is two-sided: the communicator tries to utter a sentence true in the language... the audience acts as seems best on the assumption that he has succeeded in doing so. The coordination sought is coordination between communicator and audience."
> (p. 180)

> "When the language is used by convention, it is the communicator who tries to make his sentences true if $\mu$ is 0, the audience that tries to make the communicator's sentences true if $\mu$ is 1."
> (p. 163)

### 3. LANGUAGE COMMUNITIES
**Shared conventions**

> "We are trying to find out what the members of a population $P$ must do in order to make it the case that a certain possible language $\pounds$ is their actual language—that they *use* $\pounds$, in the sense we are after. It is surely something they do in conformity to a convention: something everyone in $P$ does because he expects his conversational partners in $P$ to do it too, and because a common interest in communicating leads him to want to do his part if they do theirs."
> (p. 177)

> "If our simple language $\pounds$ is the actual language of a population $P$, we will certainly find a regularity $R$ of truthfulness in $\pounds$. Members of $P$ will exchange utterances of sentences of $\pounds$, and they will almost always try to avoid uttering sentences not true in $\pounds$."
> (p. 178)

> "Any member of $P$ will (almost always) want to conform to $R$ if other members of $P$ do; they also will want him to do so; and these preferences will be common knowledge in $P$. We can safely assume that $\pounds$ is a useful language, since otherwise it could not be the actual language of $P$. Then uniform truthfulness in $\pounds$ permits successful communication, which answers to all sorts of interests of the members of $P$."
> (p. 179)
