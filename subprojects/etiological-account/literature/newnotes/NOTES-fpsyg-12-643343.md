# Notes on "Modelling the Cultural Evolution of Language" (Frontiers in Psychology)

**Authors:** Kenny Smith
**Source:** Frontiers in Psychology, Vol 12, Article 643343 (2021/2022?) - *Note: Citation says "fpsyg-12-643343".*
**File:** `fpsyg-12-643343.pdf`

## Abstract
A review of computational and mathematical models of language evolution. It argues that language structure arises from the interaction of three adaptive systems:
1.  **Biological Evolution:** Evolution of the learning/processing hardware (the brain).
2.  **Individual Learning:** Learning from data within a lifetime.
3.  **Cultural Evolution:** Language change over historical time through transmission.

## Key Arguments

### 1. The Three Systems of Adaptation
*   **Timescales:**
    *   *Phylogenetic:* Biological evolution of the language faculty (slow).
    *   *Glossogenetic:* Cultural evolution of languages (intermediate).
    *   *Ontogenetic:* Individual learning (fast).
*   **Interaction:** These systems are coupled. The output of individual learning becomes the input for cultural evolution. The outcome of cultural evolution shapes the fitness landscape for biological evolution.

### 2. Cultural Evolution of Language
*   **Iterated Learning:** The primary engine. Language is transmitted from generation to generation of learners.
*   **Bottleneck:** The "poverty of the stimulus" is a feature, not a bug. It forces compression.
*   **Selection for Learnability:** Languages evolve to be learnable by the specific brains that learn them.
    *   *Result:* Structure (regularity, compositionality) emerges because it is easier to learn and transmit. We don't need a "Universal Grammar" aimed at specific structures; we just need a brain that prefers regularity, and the language *adapts to the brain*.

### 3. Biological Evolution (Co-evolution)
*   **Baldwin Effect:** Learning guides evolution.
*   **Assimilation:** If a linguistic feature is stable (culturally), biology might evolve to learn it faster (innateness).
*   **The "Moving Target" Problem:** If culture changes too fast, biology cannot "catch" it. This suggests language-specific biological adaptations might be minimal (Chater & Christiansen).
    *   *Conclusion:* The brain likely evolved for general cognitive skills (sequence processing, social reasoning), and language evolved culturally to exploit those skills.

### 4. Expressivity vs. Compressibility
*   Language faces competing pressures:
    *   **Compressibility:** Be simple to learn (pressure from the learner).
    *   **Expressivity:** Be distinct and unambiguous (pressure from the communicator/user).
*   The balance leads to linguistic structure (e.g., morphology, syntax).

## Conclusion
Language is primarily a product of **cultural evolution** acting on domain-general cognitive biases. The fit between language and the brain is due to language adapting to the brain, not the other way around.
