# Notes on "Cultural Evolution: A Review of Theory, Findings and Controversies"

**Author:** Alex Mesoudi
**Source:** Evol Biol (2016) 43:481–497
**File:** `Mesoudi_EvolBiol_2016.pdf`

## Abstract
A comprehensive review of the field of Cultural Evolution. It defines culture as socially transmitted information and argues for a Darwinian approach (Variation, Heredity, Differential Fitness) to understanding cultural change.

## 1. Introduction & History
*   **Darwinian Nature of Culture:** Culture changes via a process analogous to biological evolution.
*   **History:**
    *   Early "cultural evolution" (Tylor, Morgan) was progressive/unilinear (savagery -> civilization) and often racist. This tainted the term.
    *   Modern Cultural Evolution (post-1980s, Boyd & Richerson, Cavalli-Sforza & Feldman) separates itself from progressivism. It focuses on *mechanisms* of change.

## 2. Theory of Cultural Evolution
*   **Core Principles:**
    1.  **Variation:** Cultural traits vary (beliefs, skills, words).
    2.  **Differential Fitness:** Some traits are more likely to be copied/preserved.
    3.  **Inheritance:** Transmission via social learning (imitation, teaching).
*   **Key Distinction:** Not just a metaphor. It fits the algorithmic definition of evolution.

## 3. Mechanisms of Cultural Selection (Social Learning Biases)
*   **Content Biases (Direct Bias):** Preferring a trait because of its intrinsic properties (e.g., a better tool, an easier word).
*   **Context Biases (Indirect Bias):**
    *   **Model-based:** Copying successful or prestigious individuals (Prestige Bias).
    *   **Frequency-dependent:** Copying the majority (Conformist Bias) or rarity.
*   **Guided Variation:** Individual modification of a trait followed by transmission (combines individual learning + transmission).

## 4. Key Findings

### Cumulative Cultural Evolution
*   Humans have a unique capacity for *cumulative* culture (ratchet effect) – traits become too complex for a single individual to invent.
*   Requires high-fidelity transmission.
*   **Demography:** Larger populations may support more complex culture (Tasmania effect - loss of complexity in isolation), though this is debated.

### Macroevolution and Phylogenetics
*   Language trees and cultural phylogenies.
*   Using biological phylogenetic methods to reconstruct cultural history (e.g., Indo-European origins, Austronesian expansion).
*   **Galton's Problem:** Phylogenies help control for shared ancestry when testing cross-cultural correlations.

## 5. Controversies and Clarifications

### "Cultural Evolution is Progressive" (Misconception)
*   Correction: Modern theory is *not* Spencerian. It does not assume a ladder of progress. Local adaptation is key.

### "Culture is too complex / Not discrete (Memes)"
*   Critique: Culture doesn't come in "particulate" genes (memes are problematic).
*   Rebuttal: Evolution doesn't require discrete particles (Henrich & Boyd 2002). Continuous traits evolve too. "Blending inheritance" is compatible with evolution if there is enough variation/error.

### Cultural Attraction vs. Selection
*   **Cultural Attraction (Sperber/Claidière):** Emphasizes *transformation* during transmission (reconstruction) rather than high-fidelity copying. Attractors (cognitive biases) shape culture.
*   **Mesoudi's View:** Attraction and Selection are complementary. Attraction can be modeled as "guided variation" or "content bias".

### Homology vs. Analogy (Horizontal Transmission)
*   Critique: Cultural boundaries are porous (borrowing), unlike species.
*   Response: Bacteria also have horizontal gene transfer (HGT). It complicates but doesn't invalidate phylogenetic thinking. Network/reticulated models can handle borrowing.

### Proximate vs. Ultimate
*   Is culture a proximate mechanism (like a tool) or an ultimate cause?
*   Culture acts as a "second inheritance system," creating its own evolutionary dynamics (dual inheritance theory).

## Conclusion
Cultural evolution is a rigorous, quantitative science that unifies social and biological sciences. It has moved far beyond the "memetics" of the 90s into robust empirical and theoretical work.
