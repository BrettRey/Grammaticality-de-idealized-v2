Literature on innovation/entry of new forms: Weinreich, Labov & Herzog (1968) famously posed the
1
actuation problem - why a linguistic innovation occurs in a particular community at a particular time.
Recent reviews (e.g. Alan Yu 2023) confirm that actuation is multifaceted and rare: many contingent factors
must align for an idiolect-level novelty to spread through a population 2
. Historical linguists have
catalogued pathways for new forms: for instance, grammaticalization research shows how lexical items
gradually turn into grammatical morphemes via reanalysis and frequency-driven semantic
"bleaching" (Traugott & Heine 1991; Hopper & Traugott 2003). Contact linguists like Thomason & Kaufman
(1988) document how language contact imports new structures - e.g. countless French/Latin words
("language, change, influence, status...") were borrowed into English and nostrified to the point that
speakers no longer perceive them as foreign 3
. A formal treatment by Wickstrom (2012) models such
borrowing as a two-stage process: an individual introduces an innovation, it diffuses stochastically via
imitation, and eventually becomes absorbed into the community's grammatical system 4
. Analogy and
child innovation are also classic sources: children often overgeneralize patterns (saying "goed" for went).
Most such errors are pruned away, but occasionally an overgeneralization persists into adulthood and
spreads (historically, many irregulars regularized this way). Yang's Tolerance Principle (2016) even formalizes
when a child will generalize a rule: if exceptions are too few relative to the rule's domain (roughly,
E <= N/ln N for exceptions E among N items), the general rule "wins" and new regular forms enter the
language 5
. This helps explain how overgeneralizations can fuel innovation when the input doesn't
sufficiently reinforce the exceptions.
Asymmetry of entering vs. exiting the repertoire: There is considerable evidence that loss (exiting) is
easier and faster than gain (entering). A well-known finding is the "use it or lose it" dynamic in
6
morphology: rare irregular forms tend to be eliminated over time, whereas frequent forms are resilient.
Lieberman et al. (2007) quantified this in English: an irregular verb used 100x less often regularizes about
10x faster - frequency acts as "armor" protecting irregular forms 7 6
. Bybee (2003) likewise notes that
very frequent verbs like ate, broke, gave resist analogical leveling, while infrequent ones succumb to regular
patterns 8
. In short, low frequency forms are vulnerable to exclusion (speakers forget or analogize
them), whereas high-frequency forms enjoy entrenchment. Beyond frequency, coordination equilibrium
effects make common forms self-reinforcing: if everyone uses form A, there is little incentive to adopt novel
form B. Psycholinguistic experiments by Feher et al. (2019) demonstrate this asymmetry in real time: when
"categorical" speakers (always using one variant) converse with "variable" speakers, the variable speakers
9
tend to accommodate and converge on the categorical norm, while categorical speakers rarely budge.
This leads the community toward the dominant variant - essentially, well-established forms are strongly
protected by collective conformity, whereas would-be entrants face uphill battles. There may also be an
asymmetry in cognitive inference: absence of evidence can serve as evidence of ungrammaticality (if a
form never occurs when logically it could, learners infer it's disallowed), yet the converse - creating a new
form from absence - requires some positive spark (innovation or error). Grammaticalization is notably
unidirectional in most cases (lexical -> grammatical, not reverse), suggesting systemic bias favoring
reduction/elimination of complexity over spontaneous addition. Kiparsky (2012), for example, frames
analogical changes as "grammar optimization" that eliminates idiosyncrasies 10
- essentially, simplifying
away marked forms rather than introducing them.
Relevant work on norm emergence, indirect evidence, and cultural evolution: Beyond core historical
linguistics, many fields have modeled how linguistic conventions form and decay. Lewis's Convention (1969)
1
treated language use as a coordination game (each community converges on one solution among many).
Later game-theoretic models made this dynamic: H. P. Young (1993) showed how a population of simple
learners can stochastically converge on a shared convention without central planning 11
. Such
evolutionary game models predict multiple equilibria - e.g. everyone using Form A or everyone using Form
B are both stable states, with coordination pressure keeping communities at one equilibrium until some
perturbation shifts them. This aligns with the user's two-module view: once C_t (community usage of a
form) is near 0% or 100%, it's self-sustaining. In cultural evolution modeling, the Iterated Learning Model
(Kirby et al. 2008) has shown how structured language can emerge as it's transmitted through generations
of learners. These models often highlight biases (e.g. for simplicity or expressivity) that accumulate -
offering clues to the "arise" side: small random innovations can get amplified if they enhance
communication or align with learner biases. Another angle is learning theory and indirect negative
evidence. The long-standing "no negative evidence" puzzle (Baker's Paradox) asked how children learn what
not to say without explicit correction. Recent work suggests learners do use implicit cues. Goldberg (2011,
2016) formalizes statistical preemption: if learners consistently witness an alternative expression for a
given meaning, they infer that a competing form is unacceptable 12
. In her words, "learners avoid using a
construction if an alternative formulation has been systematically witnessed instead" - when two forms compete
and one reliably wins, the loser's probability in the learner's grammar plummets 12
. This Bayesian-like
updating provides a mechanism for indirect evidence of ungrammaticality. Relatedly, experimental studies
confirm that exposing learners to repeated conventional phrasing makes them judge an alternate
formulation as worse, even without overt correction 13
. In sum, cognitive and computational researchers
have tackled how absence of a form in input can signal a ban, lending theoretical support to the
preemption-based exclusion in the framework. On the norm extinction side, models of language change
often mirror models of competition in ecology: for example, analogies to population dynamics (selection
and drift) are drawn in works like Croft (2000) and Niyogi (2006). There is also sociolinguistic work on how
social networks and identity factors drive spread or loss (e.g. Trudgill, Milroy, Labov's studies of diffusion),
as well as how functional load or communicative need can protect certain contrasts from elimination.
Overall, a wide net catches fields from evolutionary game theory (for emergence and coordination) to
Bayesian learning (for usage-based inference) all contributing pieces to how forms rise and fall in a
community's repertoire.
Potential objections and conceptual vulnerabilities: One critique is that this usage-based, equilibrium-
driven account might be too biased toward exclusion, underplaying how innovations arise. Indeed, the
current framework lacks a detailed formal story for the "arise" module - skeptics might ask "If preemption
explains loss, what explains creation?". Innovations are often idiosyncratic: accidents of performance, contact,
or restructuring by learners. Without modeling these, the theory risks being one-sided. Another objection
comes from the generative grammar camp: they argue that not all gaps can be explained by usage or
community convention. Chomsky (1980) pointed out cases like the "Each other" constraint (why "The
candidates wanted to vote for each other" is ungrammatical) where children get no direct evidence of
ungrammaticality, yet reliably avoid the bad form 14
. Nativists claim this implies innate constraints rather
than mere learned avoidance. A critic might say the proposed feeling-of-grammaticality F is just a
descriptive restatement - why do speakers find a form impossible if it's structurally viable? Is it purely
because they've never heard it (and a competitor blocks it), or are there deeper cognitive constraints at
play? There's also the issue of meaning and function: the framework treats competing forms as
interchangeable other than convention, but in reality many near-synonymous constructions survive in
tandem because they serve subtly different functions or sociolinguistic identities. Stable variation (e.g. going
to vs will, or dialectal variants) suggests that not all "single-sustained" states simply drift to extinction; some
persist due to niche differentiation - something the model might struggle to capture without additional
2
parameters. Furthermore, historical reversals and "resurrections" of forms pose challenges. For example, a
form long thought dead in a community can re-enter through prescriptive revival or borrowing (e.g. the
comeback of certain archaic words, or the reintroduction of you-plural forms like "y'all" into wider usage).
How would a strictly equilibrium view handle a sudden shock like language contact or conscious reform that
injects new variants? Such events might knock the system out of a previous equilibrium in ways the gradual
model doesn't predict. Another potential vulnerability is whether penalizing unilateral deviation could
overly limit innovation: if any deviation is immediately "penalized" by the community's incomprehension or
disapproval, how do new forms ever get off the ground? In reality, innovations often start at the periphery
(socially or geographically) where penalties are weaker, then spread - the framework may need to account
for heterogeneity in the community (subgroups, bilingual speakers, etc.) to explain how an innovation can
survive long enough to catch on. In summary, while the opportunity-sensitive preemption and coordination
equilibrium model gives a promising unified view of stability and loss, one must consider these objections:
the difficulty of modeling genesis, possible innate biases or universals not learned from usage, the
existence of stable variation, and the role of sociopragmatic nuance (forms aren't strictly interchangeable
signals). A complete theory might need to integrate these factors, perhaps by drawing on tools from
evolutionary game theory (for tipping-point dynamics and multiple equilibria) and cultural evolution models
(for innovation propagation under various biases) to complement the strong explanation it already offers
for exclusions. Each of these sources and angles helps illuminate pieces of the puzzle, underscoring that
entering the repertoire may indeed follow different dynamics than exiting, and highlighting areas where the
theory can be sharpened or expanded.
