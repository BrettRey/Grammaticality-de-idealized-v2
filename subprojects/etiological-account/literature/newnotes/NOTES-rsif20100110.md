# Notes on "Diversity, competition, extinction: the ecophysics of language change"

**Authors:** Ricard V. Solé, Bernat Corominas-Murtra, and Jordi Fortuny
**Source:** J. R. Soc. Interface (2010) 7, 1647–1684
**File:** `rsif20100110.pdf`

## Summary
This paper reviews recent advances in the modeling of language dynamics using tools from statistical physics and theoretical ecology. It draws parallels between linguistic and biological evolution, focusing on large-scale organization, diversity, and extinction.

## Key Themes and Models

### 1. Introduction: Languages as Species
*   Parallels between language change and species evolution (Darwin, descent with modification).
*   Language extinction as a major problem, similar to biodiversity loss.
*   Goal: To capture the "logic" of underlying processes using simple models (statistical physics approach).

### 2. Lexical Diffusion
*   **Simple Epidemic Model:** Word spreading in a population.
    *   Logistic (S-shaped) growth curve for word adoption.
    *   Phase transition at a critical replication rate ($R_i = 1$). Below this, the word goes extinct; above, it persists.
*   **Naming Games (Steels):**
    *   Agents negotiate a shared vocabulary through local interactions.
    *   S-shaped growth of shared vocabulary.
    *   Can account for emergence of shared conventions without central control.

### 3. Competition and Extinction
*   **Lotka-Volterra Equations:** Used to model competition between two languages.
*   **Abrams-Strogatz (AS) Model:**
    *   Two languages competing for speakers.
    *   Utility/Status ($s$) and frequency ($x$) drive language shift.
    *   Stable states are typically mono-lingual (exclusion of one language).
    *   Predicts inevitable extinction of minority languages if they are essentially the same "niche".

### 4. Coexistence and Bilingualism
*   Extensions to the AS model to include bilinguals.
*   Bilingualism can allow for stable coexistence under certain conditions (e.g., if bilinguals are preferred or if languages are sufficiently similar/compatible).
*   Dynamic models (Mira & Paredes) fitted to data (Castilian vs. Galician).

### 5. Spatial Dynamics
*   Space reduces competition intensity (local interaction).
*   **Reaction-Diffusion Models:** Can lead to spatial segregation (domains where one language dominates).
*   Front propagation: A more fit language can invade the territory of another.

### 6. String Models of Language Change
*   Languages represented as binary strings (features).
*   **Schulze Model / Bit-string Model:**
    *   Populations of agents with bit-string languages.
    *   Mutation and transfer.
    *   Can show speciation-like splitting of languages.

### 7. Global Patterns and Scaling Laws
*   **Species-Area Relation:** Language diversity ($D$) scales with Area ($A$) as $D \sim A^z$.
*   **Language Richness:** Distribution of number of languages per country follows a power law.
*   **Abundance Distribution:** Number of speakers per language follows a log-normal distribution (with deviations in tails).

## Comparison with Biological Systems
*   **Similarities:**
    *   Competition/Exclusion.
    *   Spatial segregation.
    *   Phylogenies (divergence).
    *   Extinction risks.
*   **Differences:**
    *   Language "interbreeding" (borrowing/creolization) is much commoner and faster than in biology (horizontal transmission).
    *   Linguistic features can be acquired independently.
    *   "Lamarckian" aspects (intentional change).

## Conclusion
The "ecophysics" approach provides a quantitative framework to understand language dynamics. While simplifications are necessary, these models capture universal patterns of diversity, extinction, and change.
