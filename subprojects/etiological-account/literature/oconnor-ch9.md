# Notes on O'Connor (2019) - Chapter 9: Evolution and Revolution

### 1. THE CHALLENGE OF CHANGE
*   **The Problem:** Inequitable conventions are stable equilibria. No individual has an incentive to change their behavior (since deviation leads to coordination failure).
*   **Stickiness:** Conventions persist even when the external conditions that favored them (e.g., plow agriculture) disappear. This is why "power concedes nothing without a demand."

### 2. PRECONDITIONS FOR INEQUITY (AND INTERVENTION)
*   **Social Learning:** Can we stop people from learning from their in-group? Probably not.
*   **Typing:** Can we stop people from seeing gender/race? Probably not (categories are functional for coordination).
*   **Type-Conditioning:** Can we stop people from treating types differently?
    *   *Internal:* Reducing implicit bias (hard).
    *   *External:* Laws and rules (e.g., blind review, Title IX). This is the most promising lever.

### 3. SHIFTING EQUILIBRIA
*   **Basins of Attraction:** To change a convention, you need to push the population out of the current basin of attraction.
*   **Moral Preferences:**
    *   If actors develop a preference for fairness, the payoff matrix changes.
    *   *Result:* The basin of attraction for the unfair equilibrium shrinks.
    *   *Insight:* Moral education works *slowly*. It erodes the stability of the unjust norm long before behavior actually changes. It sets the stage for a "tipping point."
*   **Protest:**
    *   *Strategy 1 (Civil Disobedience):* Minorities demand High (disrupting the convention). If done en masse (collective action shock), it can push the population to a new equilibrium.
    *   *Strategy 2 (Disruption):* Lowering the payoffs for the advantaged group (e.g., strikes, suffragette bombings). This shrinks the basin of attraction for the status quo.

### 4. THE PERMANENT REVOLUTION
*   **Rebounding:** Even if a social movement succeeds, the underlying power asymmetries (wealth, network density) often remain.
*   **The Trap:** Because the powerful group retains its structural advantages, the system remains prone to "sliding back" into inequity. Small random shocks will favor the powerful again.
*   **Conclusion:** Social justice is not a state to be achieved once; it is a dynamic process requiring constant vigilance to counteract the natural drift toward inequity.
