# Notes on Richerson & Boyd (2005) - Chapter 3: Culture Evolves

### 1. A DARWINIAN THEORY OF CULTURE
The authors propose that culture evolves through a Darwinian process, but they distinguish their view from 19th-century "progressive" evolution (e.g., savagery -> civilization).
*   **Population Thinking:** Just as biological evolution is about changes in the gene pool, cultural evolution is about changes in the pool of cultural variants (ideas, beliefs, values) in a population.
*   **The "Forces" of Cultural Evolution:** Cultural change is driven by forces analogous to (but distinct from) genetic forces: selection, mutation, and drift.

### 2. WHAT IS CULTURE?
*   **Definition:** Culture is information stored in human brains.
*   **Not Just Artifacts:** Pots and buildings are products of culture, but the culture itself is the knowledge and skills required to make them.
*   **Cultural Variants:** The authors use this term (instead of "memes") to describe the units of culture. They are not necessarily discrete or faithful replicators.

### 3. THE "FORCES" OF CULTURAL EVOLUTION
The authors introduce a taxonomy of forces that change culture:

1.  **Random Forces:**
    *   **Cultural Mutation:** Individual errors in learning or memory.
    *   **Cultural Drift:** Random fluctuations in small populations (e.g., loss of boat-building skills in Tasmania).

2.  **Decision-Making Forces (Guided Variation):**
    *   **Guided Variation:** Individuals learn something, modify it (learning/invention), and transmit the modified version. This is non-random and introduces new variation.

3.  **Biased Transmission (Selection-like forces):**
    *   **Direct (Content) Bias:** Adopting a trait because it is intrinsically easier to learn or perceived as better (e.g., judging that a steel axe is better than a stone one).
    *   **Frequency-Based Bias:**
        *   **Conformity:** Preferentially adopting the most common trait ("When in Rome...").
    *   **Model-Based Bias:**
        *   **Prestige Bias:** Imitating successful or prestigious individuals (e.g., imitating a skilled hunter or a celebrity).

4.  **Natural Selection:**
    *   The change in frequency of cultural variants because of their effect on the *survival and reproduction* of the people holding them. (e.g., a religious belief that encourages large families will spread if the children also adopt the belief).

### 4. BIASED TRANSMISSION vs. NATURAL SELECTION
*   **Biased Transmission:** Change caused by the psychology of the learner (choosing what to copy).
*   **Natural Selection:** Change caused by the survival/reproduction of the learner.
*   **Conflict:** These forces can oppose each other. For example, a "prestige bias" might lead people to imitate successful professionals who have few children (biased transmission favors the trait), even though this reduces genetic fitness (natural selection opposes it).

### 5. THE MEME CONTROVERSY (AGAINST REPLICATORS)
The authors address the debate over whether culture consists of discrete "replicators" (memes) like genes.
*   **Rejection of Strict Memetics:** They argue that cultural variants do *not* need to be discrete particles or be copied with high fidelity for Darwinian evolution to occur.
*   **Averaging/Blending:** Even if cultural transmission involves "blending" (e.g., a child averaging the vowel sounds of many adults), variation is maintained by error and mutation.
*   **Attractors:** Cognitive "attractors" can make non-discrete ideas behave like discrete ones. (e.g., even if you see a variety of sharecropping contracts, you might remember them as simple "50/50" splits because that is cognitively stickier).

### 6. CULTURAL INTEGRATION
*   **Not a Monolith:** Cultures are not tightly integrated, fragile crystals where changing one thing breaks everything.
*   **Loose Coupling:** Some traits (e.g., vocabulary) diffuse easily between cultures (e.g., English borrowing "taco"). Others (e.g., grammar, morphology) are more integrated and resistant to change.
*   **The "Tool Kit" Approach:** We should view culture as a collection of parts that can evolve independently or in correlated clusters, depending on the strength of the functional links between them.

### 7. CONCLUSION
*   **Synthetic Approach:** We need a theory that integrates the "micro" (individual decisions) with the "macro" (population trends).
*   **Value of Models:** Mathematical models are essential to check the logic of verbal arguments about these complex, feedback-driven systems.
