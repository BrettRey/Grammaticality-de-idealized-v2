# Notes on Richerson & Boyd (2005) - Chapter 2: Culture Exists

### 1. THE SKEPTICISM OF SOCIAL SCIENCE
The authors begin by addressing the skepticism often found in economics, psychology, and evolutionary biology regarding the importance of culture.
*   **The "Standard" View:** Many scholars view culture as peripheral, preferring economic or ecological explanations for human behavior.
*   **Evolutionary Skeptics:** Figures like Richard Alexander and David Buss have argued that culture is not an autonomous cause but a proximate mechanism serving genetic fitness.
*   **The Authors' Goal:** To demonstrate that culture is a necessary, autonomous cause of human variation that cannot be reduced to genes or environment.

### 2. THE MAGNITUDE OF CULTURAL VARIATION
*   **Diversity:** Humans exhibit massive behavioral diversity across environments (e.g., Copper Eskimo vs. Trobriand Islanders).
*   **Three Proximate Causes:** Variation can be caused by:
    1.  **Genes:** Heritable genetic differences.
    2.  **Environment:** Plastic responses to different environments.
    3.  **Culture:** Acquired beliefs, values, and skills.
*   **The "Common Garden" Thought Experiment:** If you transplanted Trobrianders to the Arctic and Eskimos to the tropics, would they behave like their ancestors or their new neighbors? The authors argue they would carry their culture with them, at least for a significant time.

### 3. EVIDENCE AGAINST ENVIRONMENTAL DETERMINISM
The authors present several "natural experiments" where groups with different histories inhabit similar environments but behave differently.

*   **Illinois Farmers (Salamon's Studies):**
    *   **German-Americans (Freiburg):** Treat farming as a way of life, value land ownership over profit, discourage selling land, use labor-intensive practices (dairying), have larger families.
    *   **"Yankees" (Libertyville):** Treat farming as a business, buy/rent/sell land aggressively, specialize in grain (profit maximization), value education over farming continuity.
    *   **Result:** Despite identical soils and markets, their behaviors differ profoundly due to transmitted values.

*   **Nuer vs. Dinka (Sudan):**
    *   Both live in the same swampy environment and share common ancestry.
    *   **Nuer:** Larger herds, fewer cattle slaughtered, larger tribes (10k+), patrilineal clans.
    *   **Dinka:** Smaller herds, more slaughter, smaller tribes (3k), territorial groups.
    *   **Expansion:** The Nuer expanded at the expense of the Dinka. Nuer superiority was due to their ability to maintain larger fighting forces (a cultural trait), not environmental adaptation. When Nuer occupied Dinka lands, they remained Nuer; they didn't "become" Dinka.

*   **East African Tribes (Edgerton's Study):**
    *   Compared four tribes (Sebei, Pokot, Kamba, Hehe), each with both pastoral and farming sections.
    *   **Finding:** Cultural affiliation (tribe) was a better predictor of attitudes and values than ecological mode (farming vs. herding).

### 4. EVIDENCE AGAINST PURELY GENETIC EXPLANATIONS
The authors refute the idea that group differences are genetic.

*   **Behavior Genetics:** While genes explain some *individual* variation within groups (e.g., twin studies), this does not imply that *between-group* differences are genetic.
    *   *Analogy:* Heritability of height within a well-fed population doesn't explain why a malnourished population is shorter.
*   **Cross-Cultural Adoption:**
    *   **Korean Adoptees:** Korean children adopted by White American families grow up with American values, skills, and attitudes. They do not retain "Korean" behavioral traits.
    *   **"White Indians":** In the 19th century, Euro-American children captured and adopted by Native American tribes (e.g., Cynthia Ann Parker) often became fully assimilated, culturally indistinguishable from their captors, and resisted "redemption."
*   **Rapid Cultural Change:**
    *   **Plains Indians:** The complex horse-culture of the Plains (e.g., Cheyenne, Sioux, Comanche) evolved in less than 200 years (1650-1850) after the introduction of the horse. This is too fast for genetic evolution.
    *   **The "Flynn Effect":** IQ scores have risen dramatically in a few generations, a purely environmental/cultural effect.

### 5. THE "EVOKED CULTURE" DEBATE
Evolutionary psychologists (Tooby & Cosmides) argue that "culture" is often just a universal genetic program responding to local cues ("evoked culture," like a jukebox playing different records).
*   **Authors' Critique:** While innate mechanisms are crucial, they cannot explain **cumulative** adaptation. A "jukebox" can't play a song it doesn't have. Humans in the Arctic didn't just "evoke" kayaks; they invented, refined, and transmitted the design over generations.
*   **The "Desert Survival" Thought Experiment:** If put in a desert, modern humans (even experts) would likely die because they lack the specific, culturally transmitted knowledge (e.g., finding water, processing plants) that local foragers possess. We cannot simply "evoke" this knowledge from our genes.

### 6. CULTURE EVOLVES BY ACCUMULATION (GRADUALISM)
*   **No "Hopeful Monsters":** Complex cultural adaptations (e.g., watches, compasses, language) are not invented from scratch by lone geniuses.
*   **Cumulative Evolution:** They evolve through the accumulation of small modifications by many individuals over long periods.
    *   *Example:* The marine chronometer (John Harrison) built upon centuries of clockmaking tradition.
    *   *Example:* The magnetic compass evolved from simple magnetized needles to complex mariner's tools over centuries.
*   **Conclusion:** Human culture is a population-level process of cumulative adaptation, not just individual problem-solving.
