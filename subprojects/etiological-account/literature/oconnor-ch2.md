# Notes on O'Connor (2019) - Chapter 2: Gender, Coordination Problems, and Coordination Games

### 1. GENDER AND DIVISION OF LABOR
*   **The Explanandum:** Every known human society divides labor by gender, but the *specifics* of this division vary wildly (e.g., pottery is female work in some tribes, male in others).
*   **The Question:** Why is this division so ubiquitous? Is it biology? Or does it serve a cultural function?
*   **Gender:** O'Connor distinguishes "sex categories" (biological) from "gender" (normative social patterns).
*   **Signaling:** Gender involves elaborate signaling (dress, behavior) to telegraph identity, which is crucial for coordination.

### 2. COORDINATION PROBLEMS
O'Connor distinguishes two types of coordination problems:
*   **Correlative Coordination:** Everyone needs to do the *same* thing to succeed.
    *   *Examples:* Driving on the right/left, linguistic meaning, standard working hours.
    *   *Solution:* A single convention for the whole group (e.g., "Everyone drive right").
*   **Complementary Coordination:** Actors need to do *different* (complementary) things to succeed.
    *   *Examples:* The Tango (one steps forward, one back), household labor (one cooks, one cleans), leader/follower dynamics.
    *   *The Problem:* A single universal rule ("Everyone step forward") causes failure. The group needs a way to break symmetry.

### 3. COORDINATION GAMES
O'Connor introduces game theoretic models for these problems:
*   **Driving Game (Correlative):** Payoffs are (1,1) if both pick A or both pick B. (0,0) otherwise.
*   **Bach-Stravinsky (Battle of the Sexes):** A correlative game with conflict. Both want to coordinate, but Player 1 prefers A, Player 2 prefers B.
*   **Dancing Game (Complementary):** Payoffs are (1,1) if one picks A and the other B. (0,0) if they match.
    *   *Challenge:* In a homogeneous group, it's hard to solve this. Randomizing (50/50) yields a low payoff (0.5).
*   **Leader-Follower / MFEO Games:** Complementary games where one outcome is preferred by both (MFEO) or where there is conflict (Leader-Follower).

### 4. PAIRS AND POPULATIONS
*   **The Insight:** In one-on-one interactions, you can negotiate. In *population* interactions (strangers meeting strangers), you need a rule.
*   **The Failure of Homogeneity:** In a complementary game (like Division of Labor), a homogeneous population cannot develop a simple, efficient rule. If everyone adopts the same strategy (e.g., "Always Lead"), everyone crashes. The best they can do is mix, which is inefficient.
*   **The Solution:** **Social Categories**. If the population is divided into types (e.g., Men/Women), they can use these types to break symmetry.
    *   *Rule:* "If Man, do A. If Woman, do B."
    *   *Result:* Perfect coordination every time. Efficiency jumps from 0.5 to 1.0.

### 5. CONCLUSION
*   Social categories (like gender) persist because they solve the deep problem of **complementary coordination** in large populations. They function as a symmetry-breaking device that allows efficient division of labor without negotiation.
