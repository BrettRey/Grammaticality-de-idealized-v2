# Notes on Richerson & Boyd (2005) - Chapter 6: Culture and Genes Coevolve

### 1. THE PROBLEM OF HUMAN ULTRASOCIALITY
*   **The Puzzle:** Humans cooperate in large, genetically unrelated groups (tribes, nations, economies). This is unique in nature.
    *   *Ants/Bees:* Cooperate in huge groups, but are close kin (siblings/clones).
    *   *Chimps:* Cooperate only in small groups of kin/reciprocators.
*   **Failure of Standard Models:**
    *   **Kin Selection:** Doesn't scale. Relatedness drops off too fast in large groups.
    *   **Reciprocity:** Works in dyads, but collapses in large groups (n-person dilemma) because punishing defectors is a public good (second-order free-rider problem).

### 2. CULTURAL GROUP SELECTION
The authors propose that *cultural* evolution provides the mechanism for group selection, which is usually too weak in genetic evolution.

*   **Why Genetic Group Selection Fails:** Migration mixes groups faster than selection can sort them. Genetic variation between groups is low (Fst is small).
*   **Why Cultural Group Selection Works:**
    1.  **Conformity:** The conformist bias ("copy the majority") maintains variation *between* groups even with high migration. It corrects errors and homogenizes the group.
    2.  **Moralistic Punishment:** Stabilizes norms. If everyone punishes deviants, *any* norm can become stable (including cooperative ones).
    3.  **Result:** Cultural groups remain distinct (high between-group variation).
*   **Mechanism:** Groups with better pro-social norms (e.g., "fight for the tribe," "don't steal," "build irrigation") outcompete other groups.
    *   *Extinction/Replacement:* Losing groups don't have to die; they can be assimilated or imitate the successful traits of the victors. (Example: Nuer expansion vs. Dinka).

### 3. GENE-CULTURE COEVOLUTION (THE TRIBAL SOCIAL INSTINCTS)
*   **The Hypothesis:** Cultural group selection created a new social environment. For thousands of generations, humans lived in groups where non-cooperators were punished (shunned, ostracized, killed) and cooperators were rewarded.
*   **Genetic Response:** This created selection pressure on genes for "pro-social" psychology.
    *   **Tribal Social Instincts:** We evolved innate machinery to function in these cultural groups: empathy, shame, guilt, a desire to identify with a group, and a capacity for "norm psychology."
*   **Layered Psychology:** We have two layers of social instincts:
    1.  **Ancient (Primate):** Self-interest, kin altruism, reciprocity.
    2.  **Derived (Tribal):** Loyalty to the larger group, symbolic ingroup markers, obedience to abstract norms.
*   **Inherent Conflict:** These two layers are often at war (e.g., nepotism vs. civic duty). We are "tortured" by conflicting loyalties in a way ants are not.

### 4. SYMBOLIC MARKERS
*   **Markers:** Dialect, dress, ritual.
*   **Function:** They allow us to identify *who* shares our norms (coordination) and *whom* to learn from (selective imitation).
*   **Evidence:** "Minimal Group" experiments (Tajfel) show that humans are incredibly eager to form group identities based on trivial arbitrary markers (e.g., preference for Klee vs. Kandinsky).

### 5. THE EVOLUTION OF COMPLEX SOCIETIES
*   **Trajectory:** Pleistocene foragers -> Tribes -> Chiefdoms -> States.
*   **Work-Arounds:** Our instincts are adapted for tribal life (egalitarian, face-to-face). To build massive states (hierarchy, anonymity), we use cultural "work-arounds" that tap into tribal instincts:
    *   **Segmentary Hierarchies:** Armies/corporations break large groups into "squads" to mimic tribal bands.
    *   **Ideology/Patriotism:** Fictional kinship ("Brothers and Sisters") and symbolic markers (Flags) used to trigger tribal loyalty on a national scale.
    *   **Coercion:** Needed to police non-cooperators, but legitimate authority relies on the consent of the governed (tribal ethos).
