# Notes on David Lewis (1969) - Chapter III: Convention Contrasted

### 1. AGREEMENT VS. CONVENTION
*   **Origin:** Conventions can start by explicit agreement (voting, promising) or by accidental precedent (someone just starts doing it, and others follow).
*   **Fading Force:** Once a convention is established, the original agreement or promise becomes irrelevant. We conform because we expect others to, not because we "voted for it" 50 years ago.

### 2. SOCIAL CONTRACTS
*   **Definition:** A social contract is a regularity $R$ that we prefer to the "State of Nature" (a state of general non-conformity).
*   **Contrast:**
    *   *Convention:* We prefer $R$ to *lone* non-conformity (given that others conform). It’s about coordination.
    *   *Social Contract:* We prefer $R$ to *general* non-conformity. It’s about mutual benefit vs. chaos.
*   **Overlap:** Most conventions are also social contracts (we prefer speaking English to having no language at all). But some conventions are not social contracts (we might hate our convention but be "trapped" in it because we can't coordinate a switch).

### 3. NORMS
*   **Conventions as Norms:** Conventions are a species of norm. Because conforming to a convention answers to our own preferences and the preferences/expectations of others, there is a **presumptive reason** why we *ought* to conform.
*   **Social Enforcement:** Failure to conform usually evokes unfavorable responses (surprise, reproach, distrust) because it is seen as a failure of rationality or cooperation.

### 4. RULES
*   Lewis clarifies that "Rule" is a messy term.
*   Some rules are conventions (unwritten rules of etiquette).
*   Some rules are not (enforced threats, mathematical laws, or strategic maxims like "cook meat at low temperatures").
*   **Conventionality of Rules:** A rule like "drive on the right" is conventional because it solves a coordination problem. A rule like "don't murder" is a social contract but might not be a convention (murdering might be a dominant choice for a psychopath regardless of what others do).

### 5. IMITATION
*   Not all imitation is convention.
*   **Counterfeits:**
    1.  *Unaware Copying:* Mannerisms. (No preference).
    2.  *Preference Copying:* I start to like tea because you do. (Unconditional preference).
    3.  *Trusting Judgment:* I wear a raincoat because you are. (I take your action as evidence of rain).
*   **Key Distinction:** In a convention, I conform because I want to *match* you, not just because I think you know more than I do.
