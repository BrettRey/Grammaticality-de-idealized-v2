# Notes on Skyrms (2010) - Chapter 2: Signals in Nature

### 1. NATURAL SALIENCE VS. CONVENTION
*   **Darwin's View:** Darwin suggested language might have started with "natural salience" (e.g., imitating a predator's growl to warn of it) and then been modified by evolution.
*   **Epicurean/Conventionalist View:** Language cannot arise from convention because you need language to make agreements (Russell's "parliament of speechless elders").
*   **Hume's Solution:** Convention arises gradually without explicit promises (like rowing a boat together). Skyrms adds that symmetry breaking (Ch 1) shows natural salience is *not* required, though it can help amplify the process.

### 2. PREDATOR-SPECIFIC ALARM CALLS
Skyrms reviews evidence of specific signaling systems in nature that fit the Lewis signaling game model:
*   **Vervet Monkeys (Cheney & Seyfarth):** Distinct calls for Eagle ("Cough"), Leopard ("Bark"), and Snake ("Chutter"). Each elicits a specific, appropriate evasive action (look up/hide, run up tree, stand/scan).
*   **Other Species:** Similar specific alarms found in Diana monkeys, Campbell's monkeys, meerkats (who also signal *urgency*), prairie dogs, and chickens.
*   **Interspecies Signaling:** Vervets understand Starling calls; Hornbills understand Diana monkey calls (eavesdropping).

### 3. COMPLEX SIGNALS (SYNTAX)
*   **Chickadees:** Their calls have a rigid syntax (A, B, C, D notes must appear in order, though can be repeated/omitted).
    *   *Information Content:* Number of "D" notes correlates with predator size (smaller predator = more dangerous = more D notes).
*   **Campbell's Monkeys:** Use a "Boom" prefix to modify alarm calls (indicating a distant/non-immediate threat).
*   **Kanzi (Bonobo):** Spontaneously learned to use lexigrams and understand word order without explicit training.

### 4. BACTERIAL SIGNALING (QUORUM SENSING)
*   **Myxococcus xanthus:** Social bacteria that swarm and form fruiting bodies when starving. This collective action is coordinated by chemical signals.
*   **Quorum Sensing:** Bacteria release signal molecules. When concentration reaches a threshold (indicating a "quorum" of bacteria), it triggers gene expression (e.g., bioluminescence in *Vibrio fisheri*, virulence in pathogens).
*   **Plasticity:** The same basic chemical signaling molecules are used for diverse purposes in different species, showing that while chemistry provides rules, the "meaning" (function) is evolutionarily plastic (conventional).

### 5. CONCLUSION
*   Philosophy of mind (intentions, consciousness) is not necessary to understand signaling. Bacteria and bees signal successfully without it.
*   The focus should be on **information flow**, not mental states.
