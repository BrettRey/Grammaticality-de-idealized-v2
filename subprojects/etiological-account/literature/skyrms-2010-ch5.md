# Notes on Skyrms (2010) - Chapter 5: Evolution in Lewis Signaling Games

### 1. THE PROBLEM
*   **Initial Optimism:** In Chapter 1, Skyrms showed that for 2-state/2-signal/2-act games with equiprobable states, signaling systems are the only stable attractors.
*   **The Question:** Does this generalize? What if states are *not* equiprobable? What if there are more states/signals?

### 2. THE BAD NEWS: INEFFICIENT EQUILIBRIA
*   **Unequal Probabilities:** If State 1 is very common (e.g., 90%) and State 2 is rare (10%), a "pooling equilibrium" can become stable.
    *   *Pooling Equilibrium:* Sender ignores the state (sends Signal A always). Receiver ignores the signal (does Act 1 always).
    *   *Why it's stable:* Since Act 1 is right 90% of the time, the Receiver does well. A mutant who tries to signal differently will be ignored (or misunderstood) by the Receiver.
*   **Partial Pooling:** In a 3-state game, you can get equilibria where 2 states are pooled (treated the same) and the 3rd is distinct. This is inefficient but can be stable.

### 3. THE GOOD NEWS: MUTATION & CORRELATION
Skyrms shows how "structural instability" (from Ch 4) can be a friend here.
*   **Mutation:** Adding a small amount of mutation (replicator-mutator dynamics) often destabilizes these inefficient pooling equilibria.
    *   In the 2-state case, if mutation is added, signaling almost always evolves unless the probabilities are extremely skewed (e.g., >78% for one state).
    *   In the 3-state case, mutation destroys the "partial pooling" equilibria and leads to efficient signaling.
*   **Correlation (Assortment):** If interactions are not random—if signalers tend to meet receivers with similar strategies (e.g., kin, neighbors)—this destabilizes pooling.
    *   *Mechanism:* A rare mutant pair of "perfect signalers" meeting each other will get a higher payoff than the "poolers" who just guess the most common state. If they meet often enough (correlation), they can invade.

### 4. CONCLUSION ("EATING CROW")
*   Skyrms retracts his earlier claim (1996) that the emergence of meaning is a "moral certainty."
*   **Nuance:** It is not *guaranteed* in every single case (especially with high inequality of state probabilities without mutation/correlation).
*   **Verdict:** However, under a wide range of plausible biological conditions (mutation, spatial correlation), signaling *does* spontaneously emerge. Democritus is still right in principle, but the dynamics are subtler.
