**Seeded:** 2026-01-29

## The Central Puzzle

Speakers have a *feeling* that something is wrong with certain utterances. This feeling is:
- Often reliable (tracks grammatical status)
- Sometimes misleading (false positives and negatives)
- Gradient (not just yes/no)
- Task-sensitive (different elicitation methods yield different profiles)
- Socially inflected (ideology modulates responses)

The OVMG framework treats this feeling as a **detector** whose output is evidence about grammatical status, but not identical to it. This paper asks: what *is* that detector?

---

## Framing Options

### Option A: Signal Detection Theory

The feeling is a noisy observation of a latent state ($\widetilde{G}_t$). The detector has:
- Sensitivity (d'): ability to discriminate grammatical from ungrammatical
- Bias (c): tendency toward liberal or conservative responding

This framing makes measurement a central topic: how do we estimate $\widetilde{G}_t$ from noisy ratings?

### Option B: Metacognitive Feeling

The feeling belongs to a family of metacognitive states:
- Feeling of knowing (FOK)
- Tip-of-tongue (TOT)
- Feeling of rightness/wrongness
- Disfluency signals

These feelings share properties: fast, involuntary, heuristic-based, often accurate but fallible. The feeling of ungrammaticality may be a domain-specific instance.

### Option C: Error Monitoring

The feeling is an output of error-monitoring systems that detect prediction violations during comprehension. Related to:
- P600 / syntactic positive shift
- Prediction error in surprisal-based models
- Conflict monitoring (N400 for semantic, P600 for syntactic)

This framing connects to neurolinguistics and computational modeling.

## Phenomenology in Art and Philosophy

*Writers and philosophers rarely isolate "grammaticality" from style, register, and social propriety, treating it instead as an immediate sense of "rightness" or "false notes".*

### 1. The Ear as Judge (Classical Rhetoric)
Classical rhetoric treats prose as something the audience’s ear continuously evaluates.
- **Aristotle:** Prose must be rhythmical enough to be bounded. The hearer’s experience (trust, satisfaction) is the metric.
- **Cicero:** "Ear-pleasure" (sound and rhythm) drives the listener's anticipation of completion.
- **Quintilian:** The ear is "the best judge," detecting "limping" measures and rejecting excess. Even the untrained take pleasure.
*Linguistic translation:* Not "syntax alone" but a composite of prosodic expectation, packaging, and closure. Immediate, affectively valenced, trainable.

### 2. Decorum and "What Fits" (Situated Appropriateness)
"Rightness" as aptness to task, genre, speaker, and occasion.
- **Quintilian:** Appropriateness is tested by relocating material to see if it loses effect.
*Implication:* "Feels ungrammatical" is entangled with "feels pragmatically infelicitous."

### 3. Taste and "Common Feeling" (Aesthetics)
Normativity grounded in sensibility rather than rules.
- **Hume:** "Standard of taste" based on cultivated sensibility ("delicate sentiment ... improved by practice").
- **Kant:** *Sensus communis* allows judging "by feeling rather than concepts" with a claim to communal validity.
*Implication:* Ungrammaticality feeling is immediate/compelling but socially cultivated.

### 4. Rule-Following and Practice (Wittgenstein/Austin)
"It just sounds right" is not private insight but shared practice.
- **Wittgenstein:** Obeying a rule depends on custom ("uses, institutions"). Bedrock practice: "This is simply what I do."
- **Austin:** Recovering "what we should say when" as distinct from reporting hidden mental objects.
*Implication:* The feeling is the phenomenological surface of being trained into a technique.

### 5. Embodiment and Gesture (Merleau-Ponty)
Speech as bodily expression.
- **Merleau-Ponty:** Language is not encoding ready-made thoughts but a form of bodily expression.
*Implication:* Grammaticality-feeling is experienced as ease or resistance in the gesture of speaking ("the phrase takes").

### 6. Habitus and the Market (Bourdieu)
Correctness feels moralized and high-stakes.
- **Bourdieu:** "Linguistic habitus" meeting a field. The "feel for the game" anticipates what fits without calculation.
*Implication:* "Feels ungrammatical" can mean "this will not be ratified as legitimate here."

### 7. The Writer's Laboratory
Writers describe a pre-analytic rightness.
- **Virginia Woolf:** "Style is a very simple matter; it is all rhythm."
- **T. S. Eliot:** "Auditory imagination" - feeling for syllable and rhythm.
- **Flaubert:** *Gueuloir* (bellowing drafts) to test for sonic necessity.
*Implication:* Grammaticality as an auditory or kinetic sensation.

### Synthesis
The feeling of grammaticality is a **trained responsiveness—auditory, embodied, and social—that presents itself as immediate "rightness"**.

## The Psychological View (Scientific Account)

*Psychology treats "this feels grammatical" not as a transparent readout of grammar, but as a multicomponent percept arising from processing ease, prediction, and decision dynamics.*

### 1. Construct: Acceptability vs Grammaticality
- **The phenomenon:** An immediate reaction (percept) that can be sampled by tasks.
- **The model:** Output of interacting systems (parser, memory, pragmatics), not a direct look at competence.

### 2. Processing Fluency and Surprisal
- **General Principle:** Easy processing feels "good" (fluent = true/beautiful/familiar).
- **Linguistic Application:** Low surprisal/high probability strings are processed faster and feel more acceptable.
- **Evidence:** Probabilistic language models predict gradient acceptability judgments well (Lau, Clark, Lappin).

### 3. "Good-Enough" Processing
- **Concept:** Comprehension often constructs shallow representations if they suffice for the goal (Ferreira).
- **Result:**
    - **False Positives:** Interpretation succeeds despite violation -> feels OK.
    - **False Negatives:** Processor crashes (garden path) -> feels bad despite validity.

### 4. Illusions of Grammaticality
- **Key Case:** Agreement Attraction (*The key to the cabinets are...*).
- **Mechanism:** Noisy memory retrieval (cue-based retrieval). The system retrieves the wrong controller ("cabinets") because it matches the plural cue.
- **Implication:** The feeling of "OK" is an emergent property of falible retrieval, not a structural license.

### 5. Neural Correlates (The ERP dissociation myth)
- **N400 (Semantic):** Expectancy/integration difficulty.
- **P600 (Syntactic?):** Reanalysis/Integration. Can be elicited by "semantic" anomalies or difficult but licit syntax.
- **Synthesis:** Late positivity reflects broader integration dynamics, fitting the "felt well-formedness ≈ integration ease" model.

### 6. Plasticity: Satiation and Adaptation
- **Satiation:** Repeated exposure can make ungrammatical strings feel acceptable.
- **Adaptation:** Listeners adjust their "error" threshold based on speaker/context reliability.
- *Implication:* The detector is dynamic and experience-sensitive.

### 7. The Judgment Task as Decision Process
- **Signal Detection Theory (SDT):** Separates **sensitivity** (d' - discrimination) from **bias** (c - willingness to say "yes").
- **Task Effects:** Likert vs Magnitude Estimation vs Binary Choice tap different mixes of percept and reflection.

### Synthesis (Psychological)
The "feeling" is an **affectively tinged, metacognitive summary of incremental comprehension effort**, prone to systematic illusions and shaped by recent experience.

## Interdisciplinary Perspectives (The "Fit" Framework)

*The feeling of "rightness" tracks different kinds of fit across disciplines. It is not a single sensor but a convergence of multiple fitness landscapes.*

### 1. Sociolinguistics (Social-Legitimacy Fit)
- **Concept:** The feeling as recognition and enregisterment.
- **Key Insight:** "Sounds wrong" can be a rapid indexical judgment about speaker/audience relation ("not how people like us say it").
- **Phenomenology:** Recognition with affect (embarrassment, irritation) tracking social evaluation.

### 2. Linguistic Anthropology (Ideological Fit)
- **Concept:** Ideology plus metapragmatic calibration.
- **Key Insight:** "Correctness" is naturalized and moralized. The feeling is a culturally shaped skill in policing boundaries.

### 3. Conversation Analysis (Interactional Fit)
- **Concept:** Feeling as interactional success/breakdown.
- **Key Insight:** "Grammar" is emergent from turns and repair. "Wrongness" is often action-formation failure (e.g., failed uptake) rather than clause-internal.
- **Method:** Study repair initiation rather than ratings.

### 4. Pragmatics & Information Structure (Pragmatic Fit)
- **Concept:** Felicity, coherence, packaging.
- **Key Insight:** Categorical "ungrammaticality" often conflated with "pragmatically unplaceable" (topic/focus mismatch, presupposition failure).

### 5. Usage-Based & Corpus (Structural/Schematic Fit)
- **Concept:** Entrenchment and schematic fit.
- **Key Insight:** "Well-formed" = "close to what I've heard." Gradience tracks distributional probability.

### 6. NLP (Predictive Fit)
- **Concept:** Probability/Surprisal.
- **Key Insight:** Models formalize "expectation." Mismatches between model and human feel reveal where the human signal is driven by non-probabilistic factors (or diverse training data).

### 7. L2 & Bilingualism (Proficiency Fit)
- **Concept:** Split knowledge and language-mode.
- **Key Insight:** Dissociations between automatized sensitivity and reflective knowledge. "Rightness" is context-conditioned (e.g., code-switching).

### 8. Developmental (Developmental Fit)
- **Concept:** The feeling as a learned percept.
- **Key Insight:** Adult-like rejection develops. The feeling consolidates over time.

### 9. Clinical (Monitoring Fit)
- **Concept:** Dissociations in monitoring.
- **Key Insight:** Aphasia shows "knowing it's off" is distinct from "being able to repair." The signal depends on functional monitoring systems.

### 10. Writing Studies (Institutional Fit)
- **Concept:** Trained editorial perception.
- **Key Insight:** Schooling recalibrates the feeling. "Grammar" fuses with virtue and credibility.

### Synthesis: The Many Faces of "Fit"
The "conflicted phenomenology" (e.g., "I know it's fine but it sounds wrong") arises when an utterance succeeds on one dimension of fit but fails on another.
- **Structural Fit** (Formal/Usage)
- **Processing Fit** (Psychology)
- **Interactional Fit** (CA)
- **Pragmatic Fit** (Info Structure)
- **Social-Legitimacy Fit** (Sociolinguistics)
- **Institutional Fit** (Pedagogy)
