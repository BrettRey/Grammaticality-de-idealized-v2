## Relevance to "The Feeling of Ungrammaticality"

### 1. The Feeling Is Modality-General (But May Have Modality-Specific Signatures)

If Cohn is right that graphics, gesture, and speech share cognitive building blocks, then the **feeling of ungrammaticality** should have analogues across modalities:

- **Speech:** The "crash" or "wince" when syntax fails.
- **Sign language:** Equivalent sensations of structural collapse (well-documented).
- **Visual language:** A sense that a panel sequence "doesn't work" or a diagram is "unreadable"—potentially the same underlying detector.

**Hypothesis:** The feeling ($F_{i,t}$) is the subjective surface of stability detection ($\widetilde{G}_t$), and this detector is **modality-neutral** at its core, though calibrated differently for each sensory channel.

### 2. The "Broken Tool" Phenomenology (Operator Stratum Connection)

Cohn's emphasis on function—operators as public coordination infrastructure—connects to the existing NOTES.md speculation about the "broken tool" feeling. If operators are coordination tools, then:

- The **sensation of structural failure** is the sensation of a tool that can't do its job.
- This should apply equally to visual operators (e.g., speech-balloon conventions, panel borders, etc.).

### 3. Iconicity and the Feeling

Cohn notes that graphics are particularly strong at iconicity. This may mean that violations in visual languages are harder to "feel" as ungrammatical because iconic inferencing can rescue failed structures. Conversely, highly symbolic visual conventions (e.g., speed lines, motion blurs) might produce sharper feelings of wrongness when violated, paralleling morphosyntax in speech.

**Prediction:** Visual constructions that are more symbolic (less iconic) will produce sharper subjective rejection when violated, mirroring the satiation-resistance of high-opportunity-mass gaps in speech.

### 4. New Dimension: Visual "Acceptability Judgments"

The paper could include a section on visual acceptability:
- Comics studies have informal notions of "readable" vs. "confusing" page layouts.
- Could these judgments be probed experimentally, parallel to syntactic acceptability?
- Would they show the same dissociations (false positives due to processing cost, false negatives due to good-enough parsing)?
