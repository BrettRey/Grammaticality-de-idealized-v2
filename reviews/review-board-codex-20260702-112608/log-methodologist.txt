# Review Board

Simulate a panel of 3-6 expert reviewers providing structured critique of a paper or chapter. Each reviewer runs as an independent subagent to avoid cross-contamination.

Use this skill for failure analysis and presentation quality. Do not let simulated reviewers decide whether Brett's central idea is worth pursuing: unanimous resistance to a strange, cross-paradigm move can be a novelty signal. Separate "is this argued well?" from "is this worth arguing?"

## Setup

### 1. Read the target file

Read the file specified in the argument (or `main.tex` if none given). Read it fully -- the reviewers need the complete text to give useful feedback.

### 2. Find or compose the panel

**First, look for a named board.** Check these locations in order:

1. `advisory-board.md` (project root)
2. `notes/advisory-board.md`
3. `docs/advisory-board.md`

If Brett asks for named researchers, use real scholars whose expertise matches the paper's venue and contribution. Do not substitute generic roles when named researchers are requested.

If a board file exists, use its personas. The file should define reviewer names, expertise areas, and any specific concerns each reviewer should focus on.

**If no board file exists,** compose a panel of 3-6 reviewers based on the paper's topic. For a typical HPC/linguistics paper, a good panel might include:

- **Philosopher of science** (natural kinds, HPC theory, scientific realism)
- **Formal linguist** (generative syntax, category theory)
- **Usage-based/functional linguist** (construction grammar, typology)
- **Methodologist** (experimental design, corpus methods, statistics)
- **Domain specialist** (whatever the paper's empirical focus is)
- **Hostile reviewer** (someone predisposed to reject the framework)

Adjust the panel composition to match the paper. A metaphor paper needs a cognitive linguist. A grammaticality paper needs a philosopher of language. Use judgment.

**Mandatory for any paper using HPC theory:** One reviewer must be the **Projectibility Reviewer**. This reviewer's sole concern is whether the paper treats projectibility as the epistemic payoff of the framework or buries it as a footnote. Specifically:

- Does the paper frame the contribution as "here are mechanisms" (wrong) or "the category is projectible -- it generates predictions -- and here's why" (right)?
- Are mechanisms presented as *explaining* the projectibility, or as *being* the point?
- Does the conclusion cash out in what the category lets you *predict, explain, or inductively generalize*, not just what holds it together?
- Boyd's insight: natural kinds are projectible for a purpose. The slogan is "a profile, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose." If the third clause is missing or decorative, this reviewer flags it as a major structural problem.

This reviewer exists because LLMs (including the one generating these reviews) have a deep training-data bias toward the mechanism/clustering side of HPC and systematically underplay projection. The bias is so strong that explicit instructions in CLAUDE.md files are routinely ignored. This reviewer is the structural countermeasure.

**For papers that model human or expert positions as ground truth:** Add a **Target-Heterogeneity Check** to at least one relevant reviewer, usually the methodologist or hostile reviewer. This reviewer asks whether the paper distinguishes stable disagreement from other sources of variance: diachronic change, context-sensitive answering, internal contradiction, terminological drift, survey-item mismatch, ambiguity, and plain confusion. Do not let the review treat all heterogeneity as signal or all smoothing as failure. Ask what kind of heterogeneity the method is supposed to preserve.

**Review-board self-check:** The review board is itself a synthetic expert panel and is vulnerable to the same smoothing problem. Do not treat simulated reviewer agreement as evidence of real field consensus. Watch for over-neat convergence, stereotyped reviewer roles, or reviewers whose criticisms follow too predictably from their labels. Preserve contradictions between reviewers, and in the synthesis flag when the board may be collapsing messy expert heterogeneity into a clean centroid.

   206	\begin{abstract}
   207	    This paper introduces the Operator-Value Model of Grammaticality (OVMG), a framework for (un)gram\-maticality distinct from grammars focused solely on language production or description. The model treats (un)grammaticality as conditioned stability in form--value relations accepted or rejected within communicative situations, integrating mapping viability, operator-value coherence, population licensing, and speaker-level feelings of anomaly. Previous approaches, including generative grammar, Construction Grammar, and psycholinguistic models, have each captured part of the picture but haven't jointly explained why semantically coherent constructions may nonetheless be deemed ungrammatical, why certain transparent structures remain systematically unentrenched, and why categorical grammaticality clusters where it does. The framework derives categoricality from conventionalized norms, opportunity structure, and preemption dynamics within communicative situations, rather than from structural membership or intuition alone. It earns its keep by projection: it predicts which rejected constructions should soften under repeated exposure and which should resist, how learners should treat zero evidence under different opportunity structures, how early second-language rejections should track first-language preemption, and where community treatment of a form should change discontinuously as licensing crosses a situational threshold.

   215	\section*{Introduction}
   216	
   217	Every competent speaker of English knows that \ungram{\mention{Can the have running}} is impossible, but the source of this certainty proves remarkably elusive. What does it mean to say a sentence is ungrammatical? Consider these examples:
   218	
   219	\ea \label{ex:starting-stars}
   220	\ea\label{ex:nonsense} \ungram{\mention{Can the have running?}}
   221	\ex\label{ex:colorless-grammatica} \mention{Colorless green ideas sleep furiously.} \autocite{chomsky1957}
   222	\ex\label{ex:tense} \ungram{\mention{I've finished it yesterday.}}
   223	\ex\marg{\mention{I saw Joan, a friend of whose was visiting.}}\label{ex:whose} 
   224	\ex\label{ex:center} \mention{The bread the baker the apprentice helped made is delicious.}
   225	\ex\label{ex:age} \textbf{A:} \mention{How old are you?} \textbf{B:} \ungram{\mention{I have 25 years.}}
   226	\ex\label{ex:lbe} \ungram{\mention{Which did you buy car?}}
   227	\z\z
   228	
   229	While all might receive asterisks in many analyses, they represent fundamentally different types of unacceptability. Some constructions, like (\ref{ex:nonsense}), fail to establish any form--value relationship in English. Others, like (\ref{ex:tense}), involve a clash between the time semantics of clause tense and the word \mention{yesterday}. Still others, such as (\ref{ex:whose}), show gradient or indeterminate status. (\ref{ex:center}) illustrates what might be called theoretically predicted acceptability: constructions that linguistic theory predicts should be grammatical but that are consistently rejected by native speakers in ordinary judgment tasks. Still others, like (\ref{ex:age}), are transparent and grammatical in related languages but lack licensing in English age predication. And a few, like the left-branch extraction of (\ref{ex:lbe}), seem to be ruled out entirely, despite being apparently short and interpretable.
   230	
   231	Different traditions emphasize different pieces: formal approaches focus on abstract principles, usage-based theories on frequency and entrenchment, processing accounts on cognitive constraints. Each captures part of the picture, but none unifies categorical constraints, gradient acceptability, cross-linguistic variation, and change over time. I'll argue that the obstacle is a shared idealization, and that removing it reorders the questions.
   232	
   233	The de-idealization in the title is specific. Early generative grammar idealized grammaticality to the knowledge of an ideal speaker--listener in a completely homogeneous speech community \autocite[3]{chomsky1965}, treating variation, uncertainty, and change as noise around a categorical competence fact. That idealization bought tractability, but it priced out the phenomena in (\ref{ex:starting-stars}). Removing it reorders the questions: ask first what the category \term{grammatical} lets us predict~-- its projectibility, what observing some features licenses us to expect about others \autocite{Goodman1955}~-- and only then what worldly profile supports those predictions and what stabilizes it \autocite{boyd1999, reynolds2026kindsProjectibilityProfiles}. Mechanisms matter because they explain why the projection holds, not because naming them settles the kind.
   234	
   235	This paper develops that answer as the \textbf{Operator-Value Model of Grammaticality} (OVMG). Its unit is the \term{operator} value: a closed-paradigm, update-configuring contrast~-- tense, agreement, clause type, and their kin~-- identified by function rather than by expressive substance, so that tone, particles, word order, and inflection all count when they realize such a contrast \autocite{reynolds2026operators}. The framework rests on three premises, which track the order just named~-- profile, projection, support:
   236	
   237	\begin{enumerate}
   238	\item \textbf{Profile.} Grammaticality is a conditioned form--value relation, specified by whether an operator value is structurally realizable, coherent, and licensed within a communicative situation (community, dialect, register). This profile is disturbed in distinct ways~-- no licensable analysis, clashing construals, absent situational licensing, processing overload~-- so the phenomena traditionally lumped as \enquote{ungrammatical} in (\ref{ex:starting-stars}) fall into separable modes rather than one kind.
   239	\item \textbf{Projection.} The profile earns its standing by what it lets us predict, not by the mechanisms that hold it together: which rejections should soften under repeated exposure and which resist (\ref{ex:whose}) vs.\ (\ref{ex:lbe}), how faithfully forms transmit, and how they change over time.
   240	\item \textbf{Support, graded.} The categorical appearance of grammaticality is an emergent, stabilized feature of the population's licensing state, maintained through usage, rather than a primitive ban; and the subjective feeling of ungrammaticality is a separate, gradient signal, not the thing itself.
   241	\end{enumerate}
   242	
   243	These premises address limitations that have kept the traditions apart. The profile premise moves beyond the abstract competence model while building on its analyses of systematic constraints, giving one account of both categorical cases like (\ref{ex:lbe}) and gradient ones like (\ref{ex:whose}) and aligning with \posscite{francis2022} case for building gradience into core grammatical theory. The projection premise is what keeps the account testable rather than merely redescriptive: it commits to distinctive, falsifiable predictions~-- that satiation should be inducible for low-evidence constructions but not for high-opportunity stable gaps, that artificial-language learners should distinguish zero evidence from strong preemption, and that early second-language rejections should track first-language preemption. The support premise resolves the long-standing tension between formal and processing accounts by modeling their interaction rather than treating them as rivals, and explains why some rejected forms improve with exposure while others never do.
   244	
   245	Section~\ref{sec:previous} traces the impasse in grammaticality theory to the attempt to treat it as one phenomenon. Section~\ref{sec:framework} presents OVMG, defining the three constitutive variables and the recurrent instability modes that route a form to one diagnostic category or another. Section~\ref{sec:formalism} gives the formal core: a state theory that specifies the profile as a population licensing posterior, and Section~\ref{sec:dynamics-new} a dynamics module that shows how such states arise and stabilize, together deriving categoricality from usage rather than stipulating it. Section~\ref{sec:implications} situates the account against generative grammar, Construction Grammar, usage-based, logicality, and relevance-theoretic approaches, and states the predictions. The paper closes with limitations and a research program spanning corpus, experimental, and cross-linguistic methods.

   247	\section{The impasse in grammaticality theory}\label{sec:previous}
   248	
   249	The concept of grammaticality remains elusive despite its centrality to linguistic theory. After decades of research, the field still lacks a comprehensive account of what makes an utterance grammatical or ungrammatical. This theoretical impasse stems from trying to treat grammaticality as a unified phenomenon, when it actually arises from the interaction of three distinct sources of failure. To understand why previous approaches have struggled, it is necessary to disentangle three components: structural viability, interpretive coherence, and situational licensing.
   250	
   251	\subsection{The problem of structural viability}\label{sec:impasse-viability}
   252	
   253	The first challenge in defining grammaticality is distinguishing between structures that fail because they are fundamentally structurally unsound and those that fail for other reasons. \textcite{chomsky1957}, building on formal production systems developed by \textcite{post1943}, treated grammaticality as a categorical property defined by membership in a set of well-formed strings. This approach captures the intuition that some strings, like (\ref{ex:nonsense}), resist structural analysis entirely:
   254	
   255	\ea
   256	\ungram{\mention{Can the have running?}}
   257	\z
   258	
   259	Here, the input crashes the structural analysis independent of meaning. The \enquote{categorical} view correctly identifies that a viable structural analysis is necessary for grammaticality. But by elevating this single condition to the \emph{definition} of grammaticality, formal approaches struggled with evidence that speakers consistently provide gradient judgments for structures that are clearly analyzable but degraded. The competence-performance distinction \autocite{chomsky1965} attempted to preserve categorical grammar by attributing gradience to processing limitations. But as \textcite[71]{schutze2016} notes, this often leads to circularity, where problematic results are dismissed as performance artifacts. The OVMG framework resolves this by recognizing that while structural viability (the successful mapping of form to category) is a necessary, categorical prerequisite, it isn't the sole determinant of grammaticality.

   261	\subsection{The problem of interpretive coherence}\label{sec:impasse-coherence}
   262	
   263	The second tension arises from the interplay of form and meaning. Chomsky's famous example (\ref{ex:colorless-grammatica}) shows that operator-valued form--value relations can be intact even when compositional semantics fails: the sentence is structurally a declarative, the subject is correctly identified both positionally and through agreement, and the tense suggests a general claim. What breaks is the compositional semantic relation: the lexical values don't cohere. Conversely, (\ref{ex:tense}) \textit{*I've finished it yesterday} shows compositional semantics can be transparent (the intended meaning is clear) while an operator-valued form--value relation fails. Here, the clash is between the present perfect's temporal value and the adverb's deictic anchoring.
   264	
   265	Generative semanticists like \textcite{lakoff1971} and \textcite{mccawley1968} demonstrated early on that many constraints have semantic motivations. Construction Grammar \autocite{goldberg1995constructions} has further shown how constructions carry meanings that interact with lexical semantics. Novel uses that align with established constructional meanings (like \mention{She texted him the address}) are accepted, while those that clash (like \textit{*She disappeared him the evidence}) are rejected. A complete theory therefore needs to account for \textbf{interpretive coherence}: the degree to which a structurally viable form yields a stable, non-contradictory interpretation.
   266	
   267	\subsection{The problem of situational licensing}\label{sec:impasse-licensing}
   268	
   269	Finally, even structurally viable and semantically coherent forms may be ungrammatical if they violate community conventions. Sociolinguistic research \autocite{labov1972} demonstrates that grammaticality references community norms. Cross-linguistic variation shows that each language community conventionalizes particular form--value mappings through historical processes. These conventions become entrenched through statistical preemption \autocite{Goldberg2011}~-- speakers learn that certain forms are ungrammatical precisely because they encounter alternative expressions in contexts where the ungrammatical form would otherwise be expected.
   270	
   271	Consider (\ref{ex:age}), repeated here:
   272	
   273	\ea
   274	\textbf{A:} \mention{How old are you?} \textbf{B:} \ungram{\mention{I have 25 years.}}
   275	\z
   276	
   277	This utterance is structurally viable and semantically transparent (the intended meaning is clear), but it is categorically rejected in English. The same form is grammatical in French (\olang{J'ai 25 ans}) and Spanish (\olang{Tengo 25 años}), demonstrating that the constraint is community-specific. Usage-based approaches \autocite{bybee2006} emphasize that such rejection arises from the absence of \textbf{situational licensing}. The English-speaking community has conventionalized a different way to express age, leaving this form without positive evidence.
   278	
   279	\bigskip
   280	These three problems~-- structural viability, interpretive coherence, and situational licensing~-- correspond exactly to the three constitutive variables of the OVMG framework presented in Section~\ref{sec:framework}. Acknowledging them as distinct but interacting components makes it possible to move beyond the impasse of trying to force all grammaticality judgments into a single \enquote{competence} definition or a purely probabilistic usage model.

   282	\section{The Operator-Value Model of Grammaticality}\label{sec:framework}
   283	
   284	This section presents the proposed OVMG framework. The term \term{value} is used here in the Saussurean sense of \mention{valeur} \autocite{saussure1916}: the identity of a linguistic unit is constituted not by intrinsic properties but by its position in a system of contrasts, what it patterns with, what it opposes, and what interpretations it makes available. It is not to be confused with feature-value assignment or decision-theoretic value. Throughout, I treat grammatical knowledge as a conditioned \textbf{form--value relation}; when this relation is sufficiently stable in a communicative situation, with a dominant value reliably recoverable and socially licensed, I refer to it informally as an established \textbf{form--value relationship}.
   285	
   286	The model's name marks its unit: operator values~-- closed-paradigm, update-configuring contrasts~-- rather than any particular expressive substance. Tone, particles, word order, inflection, and suprasegmental material all fall within its scope when they realize an operator contrast \autocite{reynolds2026operators}.
   287	
   288	The formal architecture rests on three constitutive variables: mapping viability ($\mathsf{map}$), operator-value coherence ($K$), and situational licensing ($C_t$). Together they specify a \term{profile} in the projectibility-first sense: a pattern whose partial observation licenses further expectations \autocite{reynolds2026kindsProjectibilityProfiles}. Their interaction determines grammaticality (\S\ref{sec:formalism}); what the profile projects, and what supports those projections, is the business of \S\ref{sec:formalism} and \S\ref{sec:dynamics-new}. Figure~\ref{fig:profile-spine} lays out this order of explanation, which the rest of the paper fills in. Table~\ref{tab:vars} summarizes these variables; the formal definitions appear in \S\ref{sec:formalism}.

   330	\subsection{Diagnostic categories}\label{sec:core-components}
   331	
   332	The diagnostic categories described below aren't primitives of the model but rather convenient labels for the most common routes to ungrammaticality. Each can be traced to one or more of the constitutive variables, as the formal core in \S\ref{sec:formalism} makes explicit.
   333	
   334	\subsubsection{Operator-valued form--value relations within communicative situations}\label{sec:f-m-pair-in-community}
   335	
   336	At its foundation, grammaticality depends on stable relationships between linguistic forms and the operator values they realize within specific communicative situations. That form--value relations exist at all levels of grammatical organization, from morphemes to abstract syntactic patterns, is a core insight of Construction Grammar \autocite{goldberg1995constructions}. OVMG narrows the target of categorical grammaticality to role-functional contrasts: closed-paradigm, high-opportunity, update-critical resources that configure how an utterance enters public coordination. A companion paper develops this operator-stratum account in detail and argues that the boundary is functional rather than substance-based \autocite{reynolds2026operators}.
   337	
   338	Operator exponents, like words, are typically polysemous: a single form participates in multiple form--value relationships, usually with one dominant value \autocite{Kilgarriff2004}. The English past tense, for instance, usually denotes past time, but it can also mark social distance (\mention{Could you help me?}) or low likelihood (\mention{If I went tomorrow\dots}). What matters for grammaticality is whether a stable relationship exists within the relevant communicative situation, not whether the form has a single fixed meaning.
   339	
   340	Neuroimaging work provides converging evidence for this integration of form and meaning at the processing level. Fedorenko and colleagues find that brain regions activated by syntactic manipulations overlap substantially with those activated by semantic ones \autocite{Fedorenko2011, Fedorenko2012, Fedorenko2024}, suggesting partly shared processing resources rather than wholly separate modules.
   341	
   342	Following \textcite[3]{wiese2023}, grammaticality is conditioned by regularities within a \enquote{communicative situation}. The emergence claim defended below is narrower: categoricality emerges from the licensing dynamics in \S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}, not from an unanalyzed appeal to usage.
   343	
   344	Communicative situations aren't static or mutually exclusive. A speaker navigates overlapping contexts that shift on multiple dimensions: the immediate situation (formal meeting vs.\ casual chat), durable social positions (regional, generational), and the norms they're orienting to (which can shift with audience). This fluidity explains style-shifting, dialect formation, and the gradual acceptance of initially marginal constructions. What matters for grammaticality is the stability of form--value relationships within the relevant communicative situation.
   345	
   346	This is visible in early child language, where toddlers produce utterances that deviate from adult norms but remain internally consistent within the child's developing system. A toddler in a monolingual English household might say:
   347	
   348	\ea
   349	\mention{Ava cookie.} (intended as `Ava = I want a cookie')
   350	\z
   351	Although this differs from adult English norms, it may not be perceived as ungrammatical by the child's regular caregivers. Used with the same pragmasemantic force among anglophone adults, the construction would be judged ungrammatical.
   352	
   353	Multiple modal constructions provide another clear example of variety-relative grammaticality:
   354	
   355	\ea
   356	\mention{I might could help you with that.}
   357	\z
   358	This combination of modal auxiliaries is systematically possible for some American English speakers, who can productively generate similar constructions \autocite{morin2024semantics}. Speakers from communities where only single modals are grammatical, though, typically reject such combinations as ungrammatical.
   359	
   360	A similar dynamic appears in code-mixing among bilingual speakers, where combinations of forms from different languages can be grammatical within that bilingual community's norms but not without. Consider a Spanish-English bilingual speaker who uses a Spanish progressive auxiliary with an English lexical verb:
   361	
   362	\ea[]{
   363	\gll \textit{Ayer}, \textit{estábamos} \textit{lifting} \textit{en} \textit{el} \textit{gym} \textit{durante} \textit{una} \textit{hora.}\\
   364	yesterday be.\textsc{impf-1pl} lifting in the gym for an hour\\
   365	\glt `Yesterday, we were lifting in the gym for an hour.'} \label{ex:estábamos-lifting}
   366	\z
   367	Within the right communicative situation, this utterance is grammatical. The Spanish auxiliary \olang{estábamos} combines with an English participial form \mention{lifting} to form the progressive aspect. This cross-linguistic combination of morphology and a lexical verb is consistent with local norms, where code-mixed utterances are common and meaningful. In contrast, a standard monolingual Spanish community, which expects fully Spanish progressive structures (\olang{estábamos levantando pesas}), may judge the example in (\ref{ex:estábamos-lifting}) as ungrammatical. The use of intransitive \mention{lifting}, specific to the gym community, further illustrates just how localized grammaticality judgments can be.
   368	
   369	This doesn't mean bilingual communities simply accept any combination of languages. As \textcite{Toribio2001} reports, Spanish--English bilingual speakers judge examples like (\ref{ex:enanitos-failed}) as unacceptable, showing that even in bilingual communities, there are systematic constraints on which language combinations are permitted.
   370	
   371	\ea[*]{
   372	\gll \textit{Los} \textit{enanitos} \textit{intentaron} \textit{pero} \textit{no} \textit{succeeded} \textit{in} \textit{awakening} \textit{Snow} \textit{White} \autocite{Toribio2001}\\
   373	the dwarfs try.\textsc{pst-3pl} but not succeeded in awakening Snow White\\
   374	\glt `The dwarfs tried but did not succeed in awakening Snow White.'}\label{ex:enanitos-failed}
   375	\z
   376	
   377	In a slightly different case, as a second-language speaker of Japanese, I used to say
   378	\ea[*]{\gll \textit{Kawaii} \textit{da}.\\
   379	cute-\textsc{pres} \textsc{cop.pres}\\
   380	\glt `(That)'s cute.' (intended)}
   381	\z
   382	Conventionally, though, the redundant tense marking has no accepted meaning, making the use of the copula ungrammatical to most Japanese speakers, though it felt meaningful and grammatical to me. The example underscores that stable form--value relations emerge from and depend on the shared linguistic routines of a particular community, and individuals with differing trajectories of acquisition may diverge in their grammatical judgments.
   383	
   384	The specific linguistic context can matter too:
   385	\begin{quote}
   386	To take an obvious case which Jerry Morgan [(\citeyear{morgan1973})] discussed recently, certain combinations of words are extremely strange if presented in isolation but are perfectly normal as answers to certain questions. \textit{Spiro conjectures Ex-Lax} would generally be felt to be unintelligible if presented out of context but is a perfectly normal answer to the question \textit{Does anyone know what Mrs. Nixon frosts her cakes with?} \autocite[252, italics added]{McCawley1974}\footnote{The humour derives from political tensions of the Watergate era, when both Vice-President Spiro Agnew and President Nixon would ultimately resign from office.}
   387	\end{quote}
   388	
   389	These examples illustrate that grammaticality is communicative-situation-relative. Conversants who differ in immediate situation, social position, or the norms they orient to may disagree on whether a form is grammatical~-- and both may be right within their respective contexts. The linguist's task is therefore to determine whether a form is grammatical from any position in a communicative situation, or systematically excluded from all.
   390	
   391	The stability of form--value relations within speech communities can be empirically modeled, as demonstrated by \textcite{blythe2009speech}. Their utterance selection model treats speech communities as networks where speakers track and reproduce linguistic variants based on their interactions. When applied to dialect formation, these models show how competing linguistic variants spread and eventually stabilize, with initial variant frequency strongly predicting which form will prevail.
   392	
   393	Different language communities encode different distinctions as grammatically obligatory, reflecting what each community deems relevant enough to systematically mark. Over time, communities establish patterns~-- grammatical constructions~-- that reliably signal these chosen distinctions. As a result, what counts as a grammatical necessity in one language may be optional or absent in another.
   394	
   395	The progressive aspect provides a useful example. In Standard English, it's not merely an option but an obligatory grammatical marker for ongoing, incomplete actions:
   396	\ea
   397	\textit{She is studying right now.}
   398	\z
   399	Here, the progressive form \textit{is studying} isn't just a stylistic choice. It's the recognized, grammatical way to express a currently unfolding activity. English speakers strongly prefer (and in many contexts, demand) the progressive construction to convey immediacy and ongoingness.
   400	
   401	French, by contrast, doesn't treat the progressive aspect as a grammatically mandatory distinction. While one can signal ongoing activity through adverbs or periphrastic constructions, standard French doesn't have a dedicated progressive form. The sentence:
   402	\ea
   403	\olang{Elle étudie maintenant.}\\
   404	`she studies/is studying now'
   405	\z
   406	can comfortably describe a currently ongoing action without any need for special morphology. The community hasn't defined this aspectual distinction as an obligatory operator contrast. What is grammatically necessary in English~-- employing the progressive to signal ongoingness~-- is simply not a requirement in French.
   407	
   408	A similar dynamic appears with evidentiality, the grammatical marking of information sources. In Turkish, evidentiality is systematically encoded through verb forms and particles that distinguish between directly witnessed events and those inferred or reported:
   409	\ea
   410	\textit{Gelmiş}\\
   411	`He/she came (apparently)' \\(i.e.\, the speaker wasn't a witness but inferred or heard about it.)
   412	\z
   413	The language community treats evidential distinctions as central enough to be baked into the grammar. A speaker can't simply omit evidential marking without sounding ungrammatical.
   414	
   415	Of course, while English speakers can say \mention{I heard that he arrived} or \mention{He must have arrived}, these are optional lexical or modal resources rather than required elements of the grammar. The English community simply doesn't regard evidential distinctions as something that has to be encoded as an obligatory operator contrast.
   416	
   417	\textcite{kilani2005} demonstrate this principle systematically in their analysis of verbal morphology across French and other Romance languages. They show how apparently similar verbal systems can encode quite different semantic distinctions as grammatically obligatory, reflecting each community's conventions about which meaning distinctions have to be systematically marked. For instance, while both French and Italian mark aspect morphologically, they differ in which aspectual distinctions are grammaticalized versus left to optional lexical expression.
   418	
   419	Establishing a form--value mapping is a prerequisite for grammaticality, but it isn't sufficient. Individuals routinely interpret strings that are nevertheless judged ill-formed within the relevant communicative situation. That very flexibility forces the grammar to police \emph{how} a relation is licensed, not merely \emph{whether} one can be imagined in principle. In other words, additional factors accumulate evidence against candidate mappings: (i) conflict with conventional constructional values contributes negative evidence (\S\ref{sec:value-compatibility}), (ii) high recovery cost down-weights the mapping (\S\ref{sec:processing-constraints}), and (iii) absence of conventionalization leaves the mapping with insufficient positive evidence (\S\ref{sec:community-acceptance}). Only by integrating these weighted contributions does the framework predict the empirical fact that *\textit{Furiously sleep ideas green colorless} elicits universal rejection while (\ref{ex:center}) feels bad in everyday quotation yet is accepted as \enquote{grammatical but hard to process} once speakers are walked through the intended structure. The combined weighting therefore concentrates probability mass on the much smaller subset of imaginable relations recognized as bona fide grammatical resources.
   420	
   421	One might object that the community's verdict alone should settle the matter: if speakers converge on using a form, why not define grammaticality as simply \enquote{whatever the community accepts}? That move is circular and empirically empty: it can neither flag incipient innovations whose status is still contested nor explain why stable dialectal differences persist despite mutual intelligibility. A purely sociological definition would also mis-classify well-attested performance errors (e.g.\\ agreement slips in live speech) as \enquote{grammatical} whenever they pass unnoticed, and it would leave us with no basis for diagnosing why learners systematically avoid forms that the target community fully endorses. Separating the mapping, compatibility, and acceptance layers therefore avoids trivializing the concept of grammaticality while still granting the community the final say on which relationships endure.
   422	
   423	\subsubsection{Operator-value compatibility}\label{sec:value-compatibility}
   424	
   425	Beyond the existence of form--value relations, grammaticality requires compatibility among the operator values those relations encode. This component captures cases where individual elements are well formed but their combination creates conflicts among grammaticalized contrasts activated in the communicative situation.
   426	
   427	Consider the temporal incompatibility in:
   428	\ea[*]{\mention{I've finished it yesterday.}}\label{ex:tense-sem}
   429	\z
   430	Here, the present perfect construction encodes current relevance while \mention{yesterday} specifies completed past time. Unlike lexically incongruous combinations like \mention{colorless green ideas}, which remain grammatical because the clash doesn't mis-set an operator value, this example shows a direct conflict between the temporal value encoded by the grammatical construction and the lexical temporal specification.
   431	
   432	The age example belongs elsewhere:
   433	\ea[*]{\textit{I have 25 years.} \hfill intended as `I'm 25 years old'}\label{ex:have-years-sem}
   434	\z
   435	In English, \mention{have}+\mention{years} is a licensed frame for duration, remaining time, or experience, but ordinary age predication is licensed through a different frame. The intended value is transparent, so this is not a K failure in the narrow sense. It is a licensing failure for an age-predication construction in English, even though cognate-looking frames are licensed in French and Spanish.
   436	
   437	Sometimes value incompatibility involves conflicting information-structural requirements:
   438	\ea[*]{\textit{Who did the lifeguard who saved \_ work in New Jersey?} \hfill\label{ex:lifeguard-sem}\hfill\autocite{cuneogoldberg2023}}\z
   439	Here there is a clash in information structure: the same participant is simultaneously focused through fronting \mention{who} while being backgrounded by the relative clause construction \autocite{cuneogoldberg2023}.
   440	
   441	These examples also delimit $K$. The live $\phi_j$ in the energy model are constraints on grammaticalized, operator-like features in $c$: tense--aspect anchoring where tense--aspect is obligatory, argument-linking requirements where a construction licenses roles, information-structural requirements where a construction grammatically packages focus or backgrounding, and indexical contrasts only when they have entered a closed accountable repertoire. This makes $K$ partly derivative of licensing facts at the feature level. That dependence is a virtue, because the model should not treat arbitrary lexical implausibility as grammatical incoherence.
   442	
   443	\subsubsection{Processing constraints}\label{sec:processing-constraints}
   444	
   445	Language processing engages both dedicated language networks and domain-general cognitive systems \autocite{Fedorenko2024}. When constructions overload these systems~-- particularly through multiple long-distance dependencies or heavy embedding~-- they may trigger feelings of ungrammaticality despite being structurally well-formed.
   446	
   447	\ea
   448	\textit{The bread the baker the apprentice helped made is delicious.}\label{ex:center-process}
   449	\z
   450	
   451	Evidence for these constraints comes from multiple sources. Studies of sentence processing show that dependencies spanning multiple intervening elements increase processing difficulty, with new referents between dependent elements compounding memory load \autocite{gibson2000,gibson2026}. In (\ref{ex:center-process}), while each individual relation (like \textit{the apprentice helped} and \textit{the baker made}) is interpretable in isolation, their nested combination overwhelms incremental processing. The construction is grammatical, but excessive bridging costs from its nested structure trigger feelings of ungrammaticality.
   452	
   453	Rather than reflecting simple memory limitations, processing constraints emerge from the interaction between specialized language networks and other cognitive systems \autocite{Fedorenko2024}. On this processing-level interpretation, what speakers experience as difficulty may reflect demands distributed across specialized brain systems rather than a single cognitive bottleneck.
   454	
   455	Dependency locality is the best-documented such cost. Integration difficulty rises with dependency length and peaks at loci of long-distance integration, with locality accounting for 59--73\% of variance in reading times \autocite{GrodnerGibson2005}. Because the effect is so robust, the formal treatment in \S\ref{sec:formalism} gives dependency locality a dedicated component rather than hiding it inside a residual term. High-locality constructions feel worse even when structurally well-formed, and because they are harder to produce and parse, speakers tend to avoid them.
   456	
   457	That avoidance is a pathway from processing to change, developed formally in \S\ref{sec:change-mechanisms} and \S\ref{sec:dynamics-new}: lower-processing forms are easier to store and reuse, so simpler patterns can spread and conventionalize. The processing evidence supports this as a plausibility claim about one route into stability, not a direct neural explanation of grammatical change.
   458	
   459	Processing constraints also interact with other components. A construction that is marginal due to low situational acceptance may become completely unacceptable when combined with processing difficulty. Conversely, highly entrenched constructions may remain acceptable despite considerable processing demands, suggesting that strong conventionalization can partially offset processing costs.
   460	
   461	\subsubsection{Socio-pragmatic indexicality}\label{sec:socio-indexical}
   462	
   463	The value of a construction extends beyond semantic content to include socio-pragmatic dimensions~-- how linguistic forms index aspects of social context, speaker identity, group membership, stance, or interpersonal relationships \autocite{Eckert2012, Silverstein1976}.
   464	
   465	In many varieties of Latin American Spanish, for example (e.g.\, the Río de la Plata region), speakers use \olang{vos} and its associated verb forms instead of the \olang{tú} forms used elsewhere in the Spanish-speaking world \autocite{bertolotti2016}:
   466	
   467	\ea[]{
   468	\gll ¿\textit{Vos} \textit{querés} \textit{un} \textit{café?}\\
   469	you.\textsc{sg} want.\textsc{2sg-vos} a coffee\\
   470	\glt `Do you want a coffee?'}
   471	\z
   472	
   473	Here, the use of \olang{vos} rather than \olang{tú} not only denotes the second-person singular hearer~-- the person being offered a coffee~-- but also indexes the speaker's regional identity and familiarity with the local dialect. This indexical value may convey closeness, solidarity, or membership in a particular geographic and social community.
   474	
   475	This situational view of grammaticality can manifest asymmetrically. Speakers from the Río de la Plata region may view a conversational situation as accommodating both their own norms and those of \olang{tú}-using interlocutors~-- the situation itself can encompass both \olang{vos} and \olang{tú} as grammatical options. But speakers from \olang{tú}-only regions might conceptualize the same situation more restrictively, defining it in a way that categorically excludes \olang{vos} as a grammatical possibility. This asymmetry doesn't depend on different understandings of the forms themselves, but can arise from different ways of defining what the communicative situation allows, influenced by the indexical values attached to \olang{vos} versus \olang{tú} in their respective communities.
   476	
   477	The indexical value of constructions extends to phonology, but as long as there is no conflict with operator value, grammaticality isn't at question. \textcite{Babel2025} provides an example. In a study conducted in Bolivia, participants were presented with audio stimuli where only the vowels were manipulated to reflect either a highland or lowland accent. This presented participants with incongruent identity cues (e.g.\, vowels from one accent along with consonants from the other). But this didn't trigger feelings of ungrammaticality. Instead, vowel contrasts activated expectations about consonant features and discourse markers, resulting in some participants' \enquote{hallucinating} identity-linked features that weren't present in the signal.
   478	
   479	This demonstrates that the value of a construction goes beyond semantic features to include socio-pragmatic aspects that shape how speakers and hearers negotiate authority, identity, solidarity, and other interpersonal relations. Recognizing these indexical dimensions is essential for understanding why certain forms feel natural and grammatical to some speakers and out of place or even ungrammatical to others.
   480	
   481	\subsubsection{Situational entrenchment and acceptance}\label{sec:community-acceptance}
   482	
   483	Even well-formed constructions with clear meanings may be judged ungrammatical if they lack conventionalized acceptance. This component captures the degree to which a form--value relation has become entrenched within relevant communicative situations.
   484	
   485	\ea[*]{\textit{We sheared three sheeps.}}\label{ex:sheeps-entrench}
   486	\z
   487	
   488	The regular plural marking is semantically transparent and structurally parallel to other English plurals, but the irregular form \mention{sheep} is entrenched across English-speaking communicative situations, making the regularized version unacceptable. Without a metaphorical or playful justification (as in \mention{the black sheeps of the family}, where the irregular plural might signal a figurative usage), the utterance is ungrammatical.
   489	
   490	Situational entrenchment operates independently from the other components. A construction may have perfect value compatibility and minimal processing demands but still be rejected because a different form has been conventionalized for that meaning. This is particularly evident in cases of extreme rarity. Some constructions are so infrequent that speakers lack a shared consensus about their status. 
   491	
   492	The independent relative genitive pronoun \mention{whose} provides an example, being so unusual that \textcite{hankamer1973whose} deem it non-existent. The contexts that license this construction require the simultaneous convergence of distinct pragmatic and syntactic conditions~-- sufficient accessibility of both possessor and possessum, the appropriate information structure, and an environment allowing ellipsis~-- which are seldom met all at once \autocite{reynolds2024whose}:
   493	
   494	\ea[\textsuperscript{?}]{\label{ex:whose-entrench}
   495	\textit{I saw Joan, a friend of whose was visiting.}\\\hfill(adapted from Huddleston \& Pullum \citeyear[472]{huddleston2002})}
   496	\z
   497	
   498	A search of the 1-billion-word Corpus of Contemporary American English returned no instances of this construction. Crucially, though, the opportunity set is also tiny: contexts requiring an independent relative genitive are vanishingly rare. Speakers therefore have little evidence either way~-- neither positive tokens nor the systematic absence that would accumulate if the construction were regularly passed over. The result is high \emph{uncertainty} rather than confident rejection. Some speakers can make the analogical leap from similar constructions to accept examples like (\ref{ex:whose-entrench}); others remain unsure; still others, lacking sufficient exposure, can't construct a stable form--value relation at all. Even among those who grasp the construction analytically, \textcite{shain2020fmri}'s \abbr{fmri} research suggests that its unexpectedness would trigger high surprisal, compounding the uncertainty with processing difficulty.
   499	
   500	This contrasts sharply with stable gaps like left-branch extraction (\S\ref{sec:categorical-constraints}), where the opportunity set is large and the construction is systematically untaken~-- giving speakers overwhelming evidence that it isn't licensed.
   501	
   502	\subsubsection{Apparent categorical gaps}\label{sec:categorical-constraints}
   503	
   504	Some constructions face rejection so persistent that it \emph{appears} categorical. Left-branch extraction is the textbook case:
   505	
   506	\ea[*]{\textit{Which did you buy }[\_\_\textit{ car}]\textit{?}}\label{ex:LBC-cat}
   507	\z
   508	
   509	The intended meaning is easily grasped; nothing in the semantics or pragmatics prevents understanding the speaker's intent. Nor does the construction seem excessively complex to process. Yet English speakers categorically reject it, and \textcite[22]{Snyder2022} treats this kind of pattern as a core case of \enquote{ungrammaticality}.
   510	
   511	Under OVMG, such patterns are analyzed not as hard representational bans but as \textbf{stable gaps}: situational licensing is driven toward zero by persistent preemption. Because the communicative job is common and an established competitor always wins (e.g., \mention{Which car did you buy?}), speakers accumulate overwhelming evidence that the construction isn't part of the repertoire. Additional processing penalties~-- such as systematic garden-path reanalysis when the bare \textit{wh}-phrase is encountered~-- can strengthen the subjective categoricality without any independent structural veto being posited. The formal dynamics appear in \S\ref{sec:formalism}.
   512	
   513	Cross-linguistic comparison doesn't threaten this analysis, because the dynamics are defined over a language's own niches. \term{Left branch} is a descriptive class tied to a particular phrase-structure analysis, not a comparative concept \autocite{haspelmath2010}; whether discontinuous nominals in case-rich, article-less languages instantiate the same configuration as the English gap is far from clear, and the companion corpus study deliberately brackets the comparison \autocite{reynolds2026lbe}. What the model inherits instead is a prediction about opportunity structure: superficially similar discontinuous-nominal patterns should be learnable where inflectional marking makes the dependency recoverable, a productive source of discontinuous order supplies scaffolding, and a discourse niche makes the separated element useful. English meets none of the three, so the candidate has no scaffolding of its own and the contiguity-preserving competitors win every opportunity.
   514	
   515	Several diagnostic criteria help identify these stable-gap constructions:
   516	\begin{enumerate}
   517	\item \textbf{Persistent unacceptability:} The construction never gains acceptance; familiarity and repetition don't shift judgments.
   518	\item \textbf{Categorical rejection:} Speakers find the constructions simply impossible, with no intermediate ratings.
   519	\item \textbf{Resistance to satiation:} Unlike processing-heavy cases, repeated exposure doesn't improve acceptability.
   520	\end{enumerate}

1449:\section{Theoretical implications}\label{sec:implications}
1451:The organizing implication is that grammaticality supports a kind claim in the projectibility-first sense: a category earns standing by tracking a profile whose partial observation licenses declared inferences, with stabilizing support graded by the evidence \autocite{boyd1999, reynolds2026kindsProjectibilityProfiles}. Here the profile is conditioned form--value stability; the projections are repair rates, transmission fidelity, satiation profiles, and change trajectories (\S\ref{sec:projectible-G}); and the support grades are explicit. Bimodal licensing dynamics supply stabilization, the production--repair loop is a maintenance claim, and corrective control~-- the strict reading of \term{homeostatic}~-- remains an open empirical question rather than a label inherited from the framework \autocite{reynolds2026notEveryStableCluster}. The kind claim earns its keep by those projections, not by naming a cluster of mechanisms.
1455:\subsection{Macro-typological constraints on grammatical design}
1459:From an OVMG perspective, these findings identify recurrent attractors in the global design space of operator-valued form--value relations rather than exceptionless grammatical laws. 1548:\subsection{Relationship to usage-based approaches}
1552:Two distinctions that organize the formal core have clear precedents in this tradition, and it is worth marking the debts precisely rather than claiming novelty for the distinctions themselves. The individual/population split~-- $\Lambda_{i,t}$ for a speaker's own licensing state, $C_t$ for the population rate~-- restates \posscite{schmid2020} separation of \term{entrenchment} (the cognitive reorganization of a speaker's knowledge) from \term{conventionalization} (the social establishment of a community's regularities), the two coupled feedback cycles of his Entrenchment-and-Conventionalization model. The population dynamics themselves descend from \posscite{croft2000} utterance-selection theory, in which variants are replicated and selected within a community; the S-curve results the model reuses are already worked out in that lineage \autocite{BlytheCroft2012}. Frequency per opportunity is likewise not new: it is the core of \posscite{Stefanowitsch_2006_NegativeEvidence} case that corpora contain negative evidence, where a form counts as \enquote{significantly absent} rather than \enquote{accidentally absent} only once its observed frequency is weighed against the frequency expected given its opportunities. Variationist sociolinguistics has normalized by opportunity since Labov's principle of accountability \autocite{labov1972}.
1554:What OVMG adds is not these distinctions but their joint formalization and a prediction. The licensing-versus-selection factorization $\pi_t \approx C_t \cdot \rho_t^\star$ turns Croft's replication-versus-selection into an estimable product; effective preemption mass $p_t = N_t \cdot \rho_t^\star$ puts a quantity on the gap that \citeauthor{Stefanowitsch_2006_NegativeEvidence} left open, since he showed how to establish that a form is significantly absent but stressed that significant absence \enquote{does not, in itself, provide any clues as to why}. Licensing-versus-selection is exactly that missing clue: a significantly absent form may be unlicensed ($C_t \approx 0$) or licensed but dispreferred ($\rho_t^\star \approx 0$), and the two project differently. The Beta dynamics then convert these quantities into the bimodality result (\S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}) and the prediction about which contrasts attract categorical policing. The relation to \posscite{goldberg2019} \term{coverage} and statistical preemption is one of formalization in the same spirit: her \enquote{explain me this} puzzle~-- a high-coverage double-object frame preempted by the prepositional dative~-- is a preemption-mass story in which the competitor wins a high-opportunity niche. One caution follows from \textcite{AmbridgeEtAl2015}, who find that overall form frequency (entrenchment) predicts the retreat from overgeneralization more robustly than competitor frequency (preemption), and who propose collapsing the two into a single construction-competition process. OVMG keeps them analytically separate~-- error evidence $e_t$ and preemption mass $p_t$ are distinct streams~-- but they enter the Beta update additively (\S\ref{sec:update}), so the model can represent Ambridge and colleagues' single-process view as their sum while leaving the separability an empirical question rather than a stipulation.
1558:The difference is not that OVMG exempts the operator stratum from usage dynamics. It makes the dynamics more specific. A rare form can remain grammatical when the opportunity set is tiny or when higher-grain constructions license the token by partial pooling. A transparent candidate can become categorically rejected when the opportunity set is large, the analogical candidate would have had high $\rho_t^\star$, and an entrenched competitor wins every time.
1560:This position lets OVMG incorporate usage-based findings about frequency and entrenchment while predicting when frequency becomes preemption. Cases like independent relative \mention{whose} demonstrate the need for a theory that distinguishes simple rarity from systematic non-licensing. The same machinery explains why high-opportunity operator contrasts often yield sharp judgments: the evidence stream is dense enough for effective preemption mass to accumulate.
1562:\subsection{Relationship to logicality of language accounts}
1566:OVMG offers a potentially broader and more unified account. While `logicality' approaches excel at explaining restrictions tied to functional vocabulary via L-triviality, OVMG aims to cover a wider spectrum of ungrammaticality, including cases not easily reducible to logical contradiction, such as absent form--value relations (\ref{ex:nonsense}), strong deviations from conventional community forms (\ref{ex:sheeps-entrench}), or extreme, unexpected rarity (\ref{ex:whose}). By grounding grammaticality in community-specific conventions (\S\ref{sec:f-m-pair-in-community}) and allowing for gradient compatibility ($K(u,c)$) and acceptance ($C_t(u,c)$), OVMG inherently accommodates cross-linguistic variation and degrees of acceptability, aspects less central to the L-triviality filter. While LF+RESCALE provides a specific mechanism for acceptable `trivialities', OVMG suggests these might arise from more general principles of interpretation within community norms, potentially avoiding the need to posit a dedicated deductive module and specific operators like RESCALE, and offering a clearer distinction between objective grammatical status and subjective processing effects or `feelings' (§\ref{sec:feeling-new}). Thus, OVMG frames grammaticality as an emergent consequence of communicative practice in the formal sense of \S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}, rather than a direct output of logical computation within syntax.

1658:\section{Conclusion}
1660:Grammaticality, de-idealized, isn't a categorical fact about an ideal speaker in a homogeneous community but a conditioned form--value relation. Its profile~-- whether an operator value is structurally realizable ($\mathsf{map}$), coherent ($K$), and licensed across the population ($C_t$)~-- is specified relative to a communicative situation and read through a graded licensing posterior. The old competence--performance split becomes two layers with separate evidence streams, an objective, conventional status and a subjective feeling of anomaly, identified by different measures so that they can falsify each other (\S\ref{sec:identification-c}). Categorical grammaticality is then derived rather than stipulated, an emergent bimodality of the licensing state under preemption (\S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}), which leaves a hard representational ban as a last resort rather than the default.
1662:What the profile is for is projection. A category earns its standing not from the mechanisms that hold it together but from what its partial observation licenses us to expect: repair, transmission fidelity, satiation, and trajectories of change, over the licensing state as such, its concentration as well as its mean (\S\ref{sec:projectible-G}). The supporting claims are graded separately. Bimodal dynamics stabilize the profile and the production--repair loop maintains it, while the stronger claim of corrective control stays open rather than being read off the word \enquote{emergent}. On this account grammaticality is a projectible kind, and its mechanisms matter exactly insofar as they explain why the projections hold.
1664:These commitments are testable, and some put the framework at risk. The model predicts that satiation should be more readily inducible for low-evidence constructions than for high-opportunity stable gaps, that artificial-language learners should distinguish zero evidence from strong preemption, and that early second-language rejections should track first-language preemption mass rather than second-language frequency alone. The categorical predicate itself is exposed: community treatment of a form should change discontinuously as licensing crosses the situational threshold, or the predicate demotes to bookkeeping over the graded score (\S\ref{sec:Methodological-Considerations}).
1666:The program these predictions define is measurement-first. Estimate $C_t$ from converging non-rating indicators (corpus rate per opportunity, elicited production, repair), fix the conditioning state before predicting rather than after, and prioritize cross-linguistic contrasts whose operator status differs, since that's where the model expects categoricality to form or dissolve. De-idealizing grammaticality trades a tractable idealization for a profile that answers to evidence. What it buys is not a tidier definition but a category that predicts: which rejections soften, which forms transmit, and which gaps hold.

   579	\subsubsection{Degrees of violation}\label{sec:degrees}
   580	
   581	Grammaticality isn't binary; rather, it reflects the scope and intensity of component mismatches. Violations can range from mild, easily recoverable deviations to complete breakdowns in interpretability.
   582	
   583	Minor violations might involve a single component with partial conflict~-- for instance, a construction with moderate processing difficulty but full value compatibility and strong situational entrenchment. Such cases often receive intermediate acceptability ratings and may improve with exposure.
   584	
   585	Severe violations typically involve multiple components or complete failure of a single critical component. A construction lacking any viable form--value relation represents total breakdown, while violations of categorical constraints produce consistent, strong rejection regardless of other factors.
   586	
   587	The interaction between components can amplify or mitigate violations. Strong situational entrenchment can partially offset processing difficulty, while semantic transparency can make marginal syntactic patterns more acceptable. Conversely, multiple minor violations can compound to produce strong ungrammaticality judgments.

   589	\subsection{The subjective experience of grammaticality}\label{sec:subjective}
   590	
   591	While the constitutive variables determine conventional grammatical status (\S\ref{sec:objective-G}), speakers experience violations through subjective feelings and judgments that don't always align perfectly with that status.
   592	\footnote{The distinction between grammatical status and the subjective feeling has a philosophical analogue in \posscite{Cavell1969KnowingAcknowledging} distinction between knowledge and acknowledgment. For Cavell, acknowledgment isn't a separate capacity from knowing but an inflection of it~-- bearing knowledge toward the situation that produced it. Speakers don't merely \emph{know} that a string is ungrammatical; they \emph{register} it as a violation, with metacognitive consequences. This matters for LLM-based grammaticality research: LLMs can produce judgments that correlate with human ones, but whether they acknowledge the norms they're applying~-- or merely process patterns that correlate with those norms~-- remains an open question \autocite{Techio2026ClaimTrainingData}.}
   593	
   594	\subsubsection{The feeling of ungrammaticality}\label{sec:feeling}
   595	
   596	The subjective \textsc{feeling of ungrammaticality} is speakers' metacognitive response to linguistic violations. This distinction parallels other well-studied metacognitive feelings like the \textsc{feeling of knowing} \autocite[FOK;][]{hart1965}, which arises when one feels certain of knowing something but can't retrieve it.
   597	
   598	Note that there is no positive feeling of grammaticality, just as there is no feeling of having sufficient oxygen, only negative feelings experienced in the absences. The feeling of ungrammaticality, then, can be seen as the negative response triggered when an utterance violates expected form--value patterns.
   599	
   600	This notion echoes Edward Sapir's concept of \enquote{form-feeling}~-- an often unconscious grasp of language patterns \autocite{Sapir1921, Sapir1927b}. Evidence from aphasia supports the distinction between grammaticality and its subjective experience. Patients with Broca's aphasia, who produce agrammatic speech, often exhibit self-monitoring behavior, attempting self-correction and expressing frustration with their grammatical errors \autocite{oomen2005}.
   601	
   602	The feeling of ungrammaticality can be conceptualized as a negative response speakers experience when unstable or missing form--value relations are detected during processing. This response wouldn't define grammaticality itself but would instead serve as a speaker-level heuristic for detection. This distinction helps explain:
   603	
   604	\begin{enumerate}
   605	    \item Gradient Judgments: Varying degrees of certainty about marginal constructions reflect differences in the strength of the negative response rather than categorical grammatical status.
   606	
   607	    \item Satiation Effects: Repeated exposure to marginal constructions might not change grammatical status but could attenuate negative responses by increasing familiarity.
   608	
   609	    \item Cross-Linguistic Variation: Differences in how reliably operator-value violations are detected affect the consistency and intensity of negative responses.
   610	
   611	    \item Mismatch Between Intuition and Reality: Constructions can be objectively grammatical but trigger negative responses due to processing difficulty, or vice versa. That is (un)grammaticality can be illusory \parencite{Fanselow2021}.
   612	\end{enumerate}

   642	The distinction also explains certain puzzling phenomena in the experimental literature. Satiation effects may reflect changes in subjective feeling without necessarily altering grammatical status. A construction might remain ungrammatical in the community's system while triggering progressively weaker negative responses through familiarization. Similarly, the persistence of gradient judgments for certain phenomena suggests that subjective response operates on a continuum even when the population licensing state is effectively categorical (\S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}).
   643	
   644	Most importantly, recognizing this distinction prevents circular reasoning in grammatical theory. Rather than defining grammaticality through acceptability ratings~-- which would conflate objective status with subjective response~-- the framework treats ratings as one type of evidence among many for inferring the underlying grammatical system.
   645	 This approach maintains the empirical vulnerability of grammatical hypotheses while acknowledging the complex relationship between competence and performance, between the linguistic system of a communicative situation and individual speakers' responses to it.
   646	
   647	\subsubsection{Misattribution effects}\label{sec:misattribution}
   648	
   649	Sometimes the subjective feeling of ungrammaticality doesn't accurately reflect objective grammatical status. Both false positives (grammatical constructions feeling ungrammatical) and false negatives (ungrammatical constructions escaping detection) occur systematically.
   650	
   651	\textsc{When grammatical constructions feel ungrammatical.}
   652	
   653	Listeners and readers can misanalyze utterances, triggering feelings of ungrammaticality even though the construction conforms to standard patterns:
   654	\ea
   655	\mention{The old man the boats.} \autocite{ritchie1984}\label{ex:the-old-mislabel}
   656	\z
   657	On first reading, one might treat \mention{old man} as a noun phrase, resulting in nonsense. But the sentence actually has \mention{the old} as the subject (meaning `the elderly'), and \mention{man} as a verb. Interpreted correctly, the sentence means `The elderly operate the boats', and is fully grammatical.
   658	
   659	Processing overload provides another source of misattribution. Heavily nested structures like (\ref{ex:center-process}) are grammatically well-formed but trigger negative responses due to cognitive limitations rather than grammatical violations.
   660	
   661	\textsc{When ungrammatical constructions escape detection.}
   662	
   663	Conversely, objectively ungrammatical utterances may fail to trigger negative responses when semantic content is compelling:
   664	
   665	\ea[*]{\textit{In Michigan and Minnesota, more people found Mr. Bush's ads negative than they did Mr. Kerry's.} \autocite{pullum2009}}\label{ex:negative-ads-escape}
   666	\z
   667	
   668	The intended interpretation is clear, and hearers readily infer the meaningful comparison. Syntactically, however, the structure means something else. The first clause suggests comparing numbers of people, but the second clause has \mention{they} (`more people'), making the meaning `more people did x than more people did y', which is nonsensical. But this violation often escapes detection.
   669	
   670	Agreement errors can similarly hide within complex noun phrases:
   671	\ea[*]{\mention{The patchwork of laws governing background checks, addressing assault-weapons limits, and regulating open-carry practices help explain why people continue to be wounded and killed.}\\~\hfill\autocite[modified from][]{corbett2016}}
   672	\z
   673	The singular subject \mention{patchwork} clashes with plural \mention{help}, but the intervening plural nouns mask this violation. Cognitive resources devoted to processing complexity can prevent speakers from registering the agreement error.
   674	
   675	\subsection{Mechanisms of grammatical change}\label{sec:change-mechanisms}
   676	
   677	The stability of form--value relations isn't static. Various motivations drive the acceptance of new forms and the rejection of established ones, explaining how grammatical systems evolve over time. \label{sec:motivations}
   678	
   679	\subsubsection{Semantic motivations for reanalysis}\label{subsec:semantic-motivations}
   680	
   681	Speakers regularly deploy metaphors, analogies, and context-induced reinterpretations that stretch or shift the meaning of existing forms. Over time, such re-analyses can yield new grammatical constructions that weren't previously part of the language's repertoire.
   682	
   683	A well-documented example is the development of the \mention{going to} futurate construction. Historically, \mention{going to} described physical motion toward a place:
   684	
   685	\ea
   686	\mention{I am going to London.}
   687	\z
   688	
   689	Frequent usage in contexts where motion implied subsequent action (e.g.\, \mention{I am going to fetch some firewood}) led to semantic shift. The directional component was reinterpreted as marking future intention rather than spatial goals:
   690	
   691	\ea
   692	\textit{It's going to rain.}
   693	\z
   694	
   695	What once expressed physical trajectory now functions as a predictive futurate marker. The form--value relation changed through semantic reanalysis, driven by communicative utility.
   696	
   697	\subsubsection{Social motivations for accepting or rejecting forms}\label{subsec:social-motivations}
   698	
   699	Linguistic variants often serve as markers of regional background, class, ethnic identity, or age group. These social cues motivate speakers to favor some forms over others, leading to shifts in what counts as grammatical.
   700	
   701	The decline of the simple past (\olang{le passé simple}) in modern spoken French illustrates social motivation. Forms like \olang{il alla} ('he went') became associated with formal, literary, or provincial speech. To avoid signaling undesirable social affiliations, speakers gravitated toward compound forms like \olang{il est allé}, which lacked these status-laden connotations.
   702	
   703	This transformation underlines how grammatical acceptance reflects changing social dynamics. Forms that were once fully grammatical can become socially marked and gradually excluded from normative grammar.
   704	
   705	\subsubsection{Structural motivations}\label{subsec:structural-motivations}
   706	
   707	Structural considerations arise from cognitive and communicative demands, including processing limitations, the need for clarity, and the influence of analogy.
   708	
   709	\textsc{Processing constraints and memorability.}
   710	Languages favor patterns that minimize processing demands. Forms requiring multiple long-distance dependencies or heavy embedding are less likely to achieve stable transmission across generations. This pressure toward processability shapes grammatical evolution, with simpler patterns spreading through communities.
   711	
   712	\textsc{Analogical extension.}
   713	When speakers encounter new communicative challenges, they often extend known patterns to new contexts:
   714	\ea[\textsuperscript{?}]{\mention{I asked about what did she do.}}
   715	\z
   716	Though currently marginal, such forms appear to gain traction by analogy with main-clause interrogatives (\mention{What did she do?}). If accepted, this analogical extension would represent structural motivation reshaping the grammar.
   717	
   718	\subsubsection{Iconic motivations}\label{subsec:iconic-motivations}
   719	
   720	Forms are sometimes accepted precisely because their structure reflects their meaning. Reduplication for intensity provides a simple example:
   721	\ea[]{\mention{a big big problem}}\label{ex:big-big-change}
   722	\z
   723	
   724	The formal repetition iconically mirrors the magnitude being expressed. Such patterns are readily interpretable and likely to spread because the form--value link is immediately apparent.
   725	
   726	Note that iconicity remains syntactically constrained. It occurs in pre-head modifiers but is ungrammatical as predicative complement:
   727	\ea[*]{\mention{the problem was big big}}\label{ex:big-big2-change}
   728	\z
   729	
   730	These diverse motivations~-- semantic, social, structural, and iconic~-- can interact and sometimes conflict. While Optimality Theory has productively modeled such competing pressures through ranked constraints \autocite{prince2004}, OVMG views their resolution as arising from usage within communicative situations rather than solely from fixed rankings. The specific weighting of motivations reflects historically established patterns and communicative needs.
   731	
   732	\section{A formal core: grammaticality as conditioned stability}
   733	\label{sec:formalism}
   734	
   735	The framework separates two explanatory targets:
   736	(i) a \term{state theory} specifying what it is for an utterance type to be grammatical
   737	\emph{for a population in a communicative situation} at time $t$;
   738	(ii) a \term{dynamics module} specifying how such states tend to arise and persist.
   739	In earlier vocabulary, the state theory is constitutive and the dynamics module etiological. A more exact phrasing: the state theory specifies the \term{profile}, and the dynamics module states its \term{support}~-- what stabilizes the profile and, where the evidence warrants, what maintains it. The phrasing matters because support claims come in grades that have to be earned separately: stability, maintenance, and corrective control are different achievements, and none is inherited from the framework's name \autocite{reynolds2026kindsProjectibilityProfiles, reynolds2026notEveryStableCluster}.

   778	For many purposes it is useful to think of $c$ as a bundle that may include
   779	situational features (activity type, medium, footing), stable baselines tied to
   780	speaker ascription, and norm-orientation (discourse-community identification),

   784	\subsection{Objects}
   785	\label{sec:objects}
   786	
   787	Let $u$ range over utterance tokens or fine-grained utterance types, with
   788	structural representation $M(u)$. Let $\sigma(u,c)$ be the utterance-level
   789	interpretation made available in $c$ (lexical content plus pragmatic enrichment,
   790	indexical resolution, discourse update, etc.).
   791	
   792	Utterance types inherit licensing from a hierarchy of constructional nodes
   793	$\mathcal{H}$. A novel token such as \mention{She texted him the address} need
   794	not have been encountered before; it can still be covered by licensed nodes for
   795	ditransitive transfer, pronominal object order, and the relevant lexical-class
   796	relations. Let $A(u)\subset\mathcal{H}$ be the nodes activated by $u$, and let
   797	$\lambda_g(u,c)$ be non-negative coverage weights with
   798	$\sum_{g\in A(u)}\lambda_g(u,c)=1$. Each node $g$ has its own licensing
   799	posterior. A token-level licensing estimate can then be derived by partial
   800	pooling:
   801	\begin{equation*}
   802	C_t(u,c)=\sum_{g\in A(u)}\lambda_g(u,c)\,C_t(g,c).
   803	\end{equation*}
   804	The sum is schematic; a similarity kernel over representations would do the
   805	same work. What matters is that the learner updates constructional regions, not
   806	only memorized sentence types. This prevents the dynamics from predicting that
   807	every first-heard sentence has zero licensing.
   808	
   809	The framework distinguishes three state quantities.
   810	
   811	\textsc{(i) Mapping viability.}
   812	Let $\mathsf{map}(u,c)\in\{0,1\}$ indicate whether structural analysis can yield at least one well-typed representation for $u$ (category assignment + structural composition) throughout the derivation.
   813	\begin{equation*}
   814	\mathsf{map}(u,c)=
   815	\begin{cases}
   816	1 & \text{if }\exists \text{ valid analysis } M(u) \text{ yielding } \mu(M)\\
   817	0 & \text{otherwise.}
   818	\end{cases}
   819	\end{equation*}
   820	$\mathsf{map}$ is prior to \emph{situational licensing} in one specific sense: it registers whether any covering analysis exists at all (word salad and category errors defeat it) while enforcing nothing about whether the covered analysis is licensed in $c$. Situational non-licensing belongs only in $C_t$. But $\mathsf{map}$ isn't pre-conventional; \S\ref{sec:map-clarification} spells out its dependence on the constructional hierarchy, stored wholes included.
   821	
   822	\textsc{(ii) Coherence as satisfied concentration of interpretation.}
   823	Even when $\mathsf{map}(u,c)=1$, interpretation can be unstable: multiple
   824	incompatible construals may compete, or the best available construal may violate
   825	an operator constraint. A normalized distribution over ordinary interpretations
   826	would measure ambiguity but not violation: a single catastrophic construal would
   827	still receive probability 1. To avoid that bug, the model adds a null outcome
   828	$\omega_0$ representing rejection or failed operator-value satisfaction. Let
   829	$\Omega^+(u,c)=\Omega(u,c)\cup\{\omega_0\}$ and fix
   830	$E(\omega_0;u,c)=\kappa_c$:
   831	$$
   832	p(\omega\mid u,c)=
   833	\frac{e^{-E(\omega;u,c)}}{e^{-\kappa_c}+\sum_{\omega'\in\Omega(u,c)}
   834	e^{-E(\omega';u,c)}}.
   835	$$
   836	$$
   837	K(u,c)=\max_{\omega\in\Omega(u,c)} p(\omega\mid u,c)\in[0,1].
   838	$$
   839	I choose the null-outcome repair because it preserves the probabilistic
   840	interpretation of $K$. An unnormalized score $e^{-E(\omega^\star)}$ or an
   841	explicit $K_{\text{peak}}\times K_{\text{satisfaction}}$ decomposition would
   842	make similar predictions, but the null outcome keeps ambiguity and
   843	satisfaction in a single race. A good single construal has $E(\omega^\star)\ll
   844	\kappa_c$ and $K\approx 1$; a transparent but operator-violating construal has
   845	$E(\omega^\star)\gg\kappa_c$, so probability mass shifts to $\omega_0$ and
   846	$K\approx 0$.
   847	
   848	The energy model $E(\omega;u,c)$ is \emph{open-ended}:
   849	\begin{equation*}
   850	E(\omega;u,c)=\sum_{j\in\mathcal{J}(c)} w_j\,\phi_j(\omega;u,c),
   851	\end{equation*}
   852	where $\mathcal{J}(c)$ includes constraints on grammaticalized features live in
   853	$c$. In the demonstrations below, I assume a minimal inventory: (1) temporal
   854	alignment where tense--aspect is grammaticalized, (2) argument-structure
   855	satisfaction where a construction licenses roles, (3) information-structural
   856	fit where topic/focus packaging is grammatically specified, and (4) indexical
   857	consistency only when the contrast belongs to a closed accountable repertoire.
   858	This criterion makes $K$ partly derivative of licensing facts. A value can
   859	enter $E$ only after the community has conventionalized it as a grammaticalized
   860	constraint.
   861	
   862	This decomposition bridges the narrative diagnostic categories of \S\ref{sec:core-components} to the formal variables. For instance, coherence demands may be grouped by their subsystem~-- denoting the sum over purely semantic demands as $E_{\text{sem}}$ and over indexical/pragmatic demands as $E_{\text{index}}$~-- to yield an expositional factorization $K \approx K_{\text{sem}} \cdot K_{\text{index}}$. In practice, however, the framework assumes that stability depends on the joint concentration of all constraints live in the communicative situation $c$.
   863	
   864	\textsc{(iii) Situational licensing (entrenchment) under conditioning.}
   865	Each individual $i$ maintains a graded licensing state for $u$ in $c$: a credence
   866	$\pi_{i,t}(u,c)\in[0,1]$ that $u$ is a community resource there. \S\ref{sec:update}
   867	models this state as a Beta posterior. The licensing \emph{attitude}
   868	$\Lambda_{i,t}(u,c)\in\{0,1\}$~-- treating the relationship as a legitimate option
   869	rather than a performance slip or nonce error~-- is a thresholded read-out of that
   870	graded state, not a separate primitive.
   871	
   872	The population-level licensing state is the distribution of $\pi_{i,t}(u,c)$
   873	across the population induced by $c$. Its mean,
   874	\begin{equation*}
   875	C_t(u,c)=\mathbb{E}_i\bigl[\pi_{i,t}(u,c)\bigr]\in[0,1],
   876	\end{equation*}
   877	is the object previously called \enquote{entrenchment}, explicitly
   878	conditioned on $c$. The population quantified over isn't a separate primitive; it is induced by the norm-centre and communicative situation bundled in $c$.
   879	
   880	The mean doesn't exhaust the state. A community in which half the speakers
   881	confidently license $u$ and half confidently reject it, and a community in which
   882	every speaker sits at credence one-half, share $C_t=0.5$ but differ in
   883	dispersion, both across speakers and within each speaker's posterior. Stable
   884	mixture and shared uncertainty are different conditions with different
   885	signatures (bimodal versus unimodal individual judgment distributions, high
   886	versus low within-speaker consistency), and \S\ref{sec:projectible-G} shows that
   887	some of the model's projections run over exactly that difference. Where only the
   888	mean matters, I write $C_t$; where concentration matters, I refer to the
   889	licensing state.
   890	
   891	\subsection{Grammatical status as a conditioned state property}
   892	\label{sec:objective-G}
   893	
   894	I write $\mathbb{I}[\cdot]$ for the indicator function:
   895	$\mathbb{I}[\phi]=1$ if $\phi$ is true and $0$ otherwise.
   896	
   897	The state theory distinguishes a graded stability score from a categorical
   898	membership predicate.
   899	
   900	\subsubsection{A graded stability score}
   901	
   902	Recall that $\mathsf{map}(u,c)\in\{0,1\}$ indicates whether $u$ has at least one
   903	available structural analysis in conditioning state $c$ that yields a
   904	well-defined operator value. Let $K(u,c)\in[0,1]$ be coherence as satisfied
   905	concentration of the interpretation distribution, and let $C_t(u,c)\in[0,1]$ be
   906	the population licensing rate at time $t$ in $c$.
   907	
   908	Define the graded stability score:
   909	\begin{equation*}
   910	\widetilde{G}_t(u,c)=\mathsf{map}(u,c)\cdot C_t(u,c)\cdot K(u,c)\in[0,1].
   911	\end{equation*}
   912	This quantity is the workhorse for modeling gradience and for linking the
   913	state theory to behavioural measures.
   914	
   915	\subsubsection{Projectible use of the graded score}
   916	\label{sec:projectible-G}
   917	
   918	$\widetilde{G}_t$ is not bookkeeping after the real explanation has happened.
   919	It is a macro-state over which grammaticality projects. But the projections
   920	split into two grades, and the split matters.
   921	
   922	Some generalizations run over $\widetilde{G}_t$ as such. Production and repair
   923	behaviour at time $t+1$ depend partly on $\widetilde{G}_t$:
   924	\begin{align*}
   925	\Pr(\text{produce }u\mid n,c,t) &\propto
   926	\widetilde{G}_t(u,c)\,\rho_t^\star(u\mid n,c),\\
   927	\Pr(\text{repair }u\mid c,t) &= r_0+r_1\bigl(1-\widetilde{G}_t(u,c)\bigr),
   928	\qquad r_1>0.
   929	\end{align*}
   930	For these, a low score licenses the same expectations regardless of whether it
   931	comes from mapping failure, operator-value incoherence, or non-licensing, and
   932	high-$\widetilde{G}$ forms show the converse profile.
   933	
   934	Other generalizations need the finer state: the licensing posterior with its
   935	concentration, not just its mean. Two constructions can share a low
   936	$C_t$ while differing in how much evidence backs it, and the update rule
   937	\eqref{eq:beta-update} makes them behave differently under new exposure: a
   938	diffuse posterior should move with framing and familiarization, while a
   939	concentrated one shouldn't. Satiation profiles, framing sensitivity
   940	(\S\ref{sec:categorical-constraints}), and actuation
   941	(\S\ref{sec:dynamics-new}) are therefore projections over the posterior state.
   942	This is what separates the independent relative \mention{whose} (low evidence,
   943	diffuse posterior) from left-branch extraction (heavy preemption, posterior
   944	concentrated near zero): the two can approach similar means while projecting
   945	differently.
   946	
   947	The feedback loop closes as before. Production and repair generate the later
   948	evidence streams $s_t$, $e_t$, and $p_t$, so individual behaviour updates
   949	population licensing, and the resulting population state projects back into
   950	future production and repair. The projectible generalizations are satiation
   951	profiles, transmission fidelity, repair rates, and change trajectories~-- some
   952	over $\widetilde{G}_t$ as such, some over the posterior state that
   953	$\widetilde{G}_t$ summarizes.
   954	
   955	Figure~\ref{fig:state-space} makes the two-dimensional state visible. The
   956	horizontal axis is the licensing mean, which $\widetilde{G}_t$ reads; the
   957	vertical axis is the posterior's concentration, which it discards. Stable gaps
   958	and defective cells share a low mean and separate only on the vertical axis, so
   959	any account that projects from the mean alone cannot tell confident rejection
   960	from unresolved uncertainty.

   989	\subsubsection{A categorical membership predicate}
   990	
   991	Communities often treat grammaticality as a constitutive membership fact:
   992	either a relationship counts as an available resource in $c$ or it doesn't.
   993	A categorical predicate can be defined by thresholding the graded score:
   994	\begin{equation*}
   995	G_t(u,c)=\mathbb{I}\!\left[\widetilde{G}_t(u,c)\ge \tau(c)\right].
   996	\end{equation*}
   997	The threshold $\tau(c)$ isn't a hidden grammatical parameter. It is a decision
   998	criterion associated with the communicative situation: some situations are
   999	strict about what counts as \enquote{in the repertoire}, others are permissive.
  1000	
  1001	\subsubsection{A decision-theoretic motivation}
  1002	Suppose a judge in $c$
  1003	is choosing between two labels, $\ell\in\{\textsc{in-repertoire},\textsc{not-in-repertoire}\}$,
  1004	and faces asymmetric losses. Let $L_{\textsc{fa}}(c)$ be the loss of treating an
  1005	item as \textsc{in-repertoire} when it isn't, and $L_{\textsc{fr}}(c)$ the loss
  1006	of treating an item as \textsc{not-in-repertoire} when it is. Under the
  1007	simplifying assumption that $\widetilde{G}_t(u,c)$ is a calibrated signal of
  1008	stability, the optimal rule is to accept $u$ as \textsc{in-repertoire} whenever:
  1009	\begin{equation*}
  1010	\widetilde{G}_t(u,c)\;\ge\;\tau(c)\;=\;\frac{L_{\textsc{fa}}(c)}{L_{\textsc{fa}}(c)+L_{\textsc{fr}}(c)}.
  1011	\end{equation*}
  1012	On this view, $\tau(c)$ is induced by payoff structure: high-stakes institutional
  1013	contexts raise $L_{\textsc{fa}}$ (and thus $\tau$), whereas in-group, low-stakes
  1014	contexts raise $L_{\textsc{fr}}$ (lowering $\tau$).
  1015	
  1016	Two clarifications keep the categorical predicate from overclaiming. First,
  1017	calling the status \enquote{objective} means population-level and
  1018	convention-constituted, not judge-independent in any stronger sense. The status

  1175	
  1176	\begin{itemize}

  1306	\subsection{Dynamics: preemption as effective preemption mass}
  1307	\label{sec:update}
  1308	
  1309	The core idea is that
  1310	learners update licensing not only from positive uses of $u$ but also from
  1311	structured non-occurrence when $u$ is a plausible competitor and is repeatedly
  1312	not chosen. The update shouldn't treat all \enquote{opportunities} as equally
  1313	informative: absence is evidential only to the extent that $u$ would have had
  1314	non-trivial probability of being chosen if it were licensed.
  1315	
  1316	For a target variant $u$ associated with niche $n(u)$, define the \term{effective
  1317	preemption mass} in a time window as the expected number of $u$-tokens that would
  1318	have been produced if $u$ were a licensed competitor:
  1319	\begin{equation*}
  1320	p_t(u,c)
  1321	=\sum_{j=1}^{N_t(n(u),c)}
  1322	\rho_t^\star\!\bigl(u\mid n(u),c\bigr),
  1323	\end{equation*}
  1324	where $N_t(n(u),c)$ is the number of niche opportunities observed in that window.
  1325	This quantity is large only when (i) the opportunity set is large and (ii) $u$
  1326	would have been chosen at non-trivial rates if licensed.
  1327	
  1328	A simple Bayesian learner for licensing represents each construction node
  1329	$(g,c)$ with a Beta posterior $\mathrm{Beta}(a_{g,t},b_{g,t})$, updated by three evidence streams:
  1330	$s_t$ (positive evidence), $e_t$ (error evidence), and $p_t$ (effective preemption mass).
  1331	An observed token or missed opportunity distributes its evidence over activated
  1332	nodes by the coverage weights $\lambda_g(u,c)$:
  1333	\begin{align*}
  1334	a_{g,t+1} &= a_{g,t}+\lambda_g(u,c)s_t(u,c),\\
  1335	b_{g,t+1} &= b_{g,t}+\lambda_g(u,c)\{e_t(u,c)+p_t(u,c)\}.
  1336	\end{align*}
  1337	The implied node-level mean licensing rate is then:
  1338	\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-update}
  1339	C_{t+1}(g,c)=\frac{a_{g,t+1}}{a_{g,t+1}+b_{g,t+1}}.
  1340	\end{equation}
  1341	Token-level $C_t(u,c)$ is obtained by the partial-pooling equation in
  1342	\S\ref{sec:objects}.
  1343	
  1344	The streams themselves are ascription-weighted. The definition of $\Lambda$
  1345	already excludes performance slips and nonce errors, and the conditioning state
  1346	already fixes whose norms are being estimated; making this explicit, each
  1347	token's contribution to $s_t$, $e_t$, or $p_t$ is scaled by the hearer's
  1348	ascription weight: the probability that the token is a competent realization of
  1349	the norms of $c$, given cues about the producer (age, learner status, group
  1350	membership) and about the token (disfluency, self-repair). Child and learner
  1351	errors barely move adult posteriors even in error-dense environments, not
  1352	because they go unheard but because they're attributed to development or
  1353	transfer rather than to the norm-centre. The same machinery as $q_h(c\mid e)$
  1354	does the work: a token is evidence about the norms of whatever conditioning
  1355	state its producer is taken to competently instantiate. Contact-driven change
  1356	(koinéization) then falls out as a shift in ascription weights rather than a
  1357	separate mechanism.
  1358	
  1359	The two kinds of evidence also attach at different grains. Positive evidence
  1360	distributes over activated nodes by the coverage weights: a heard token confirms
  1361	every construction that covers it. Error and preemption mass instead concentrate
  1362	on the most specific node that individuates the candidate within its niche,
  1363	because that node is what distinctively predicts the missing tokens. In Bayesian
  1364	terms, the likelihood penalty for structured non-occurrence falls on the
  1365	hypothesis that wastes probability mass on sentence types never observed;
  1366	likelihood is a quantitative measure of the weight of implicit negative evidence
  1367	\autocite{PerforsEtAl2011}. So wh-fronting, question formation, and
  1368	modifier--noun packaging keep their high licensing~-- each is confirmed by its
  1369	own abundant tokens~-- while the left-branch-extraction configuration node, the
  1370	one node that distinctively predicts \ungram{\mention{Which did you buy car?}}
  1371	tokens, absorbs the preemption mass from every fronting opportunity where a
  1372	contiguity-preserving competitor wins.
  1373	
  1374	This asymmetry also answers an acquisition worry. If token-level licensing were
  1375	just pooled parent licensing, learners should pass through a stage of treating
  1376	left-branch extraction as live. They don't need to: the candidate is generated
  1377	by analogy, so it's on the table with non-zero prior support, but its dedicated
  1378	node has no scaffolding of its own, fronting opportunities are dense in the
  1379	input, and each one adds preemption mass. A companion corpus study quantifies
  1380	the evidential situation: across 1{,}228 fronting opportunities in a
  1381	sixteen-corpus Universal Dependencies sample, zero genuine determiner--head
  1382	discontinuities occur (95\% upper bound 0.24\%), while contiguity-preserving
  1383	alternatives~-- pied-piping, fused-head construals, and the \mention{big mess}
  1384	family~-- occur at measurable rates \autocite{reynolds2026lbe}. Comprehension
  1385	pushes the same way: a fronted \mention{whose} or \mention{which} defaults to a
  1386	fused-head parse, so the discontinuous analysis also loses the parsing race
  1387	\autocite{Culicover_Varaschin_Winkler_2022_Radical}. The non-satiation of
  1388	left-branch extraction, confirmed in a meta-analysis of 25 experiments
  1389	\autocite{Lu2024}, is exactly what a concentrated near-zero posterior predicts
  1390	(\S\ref{sec:projectible-G}).
  1391	
  1392	\begin{note}
  1393	\textbf{From decaying-gain learning to logistic dynamics.} The discrete update \eqref{eq:beta-update} is the primary dynamics. Applied to a fixed environment (exogenous evidence streams), it is a decaying-gain estimator: the posterior mean follows a hyperbolic trajectory of the form $a_0/(a_0+b_0+\bar{e}\,t)$, as plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:posterior-means}, and converges without bifurcating. The logistic approximation $\dot{C}=r(g,c)\,C(1-C)$ arises when the loop in \S\ref{sec:projectible-G} is closed: positive evidence is generated by production at a rate that grows with the current licensing state, while error and preemption mass accrue when the target isn't chosen. Replacing those endogenous evidence streams by their expectations yields a logistic drift, with $r(g,c)$ a net evidence rate and a gain that is constant under bounded evidence memory (decaying otherwise). I use this ODE only for qualitative fixed-point and comparative-statics arguments; the fixed points at 0 and 1 belong to the closed-loop population dynamics, not to individual open-loop learning.
  1394	\end{note}

  1396	\subsection{Emergent categoricality as bimodality}
  1397	\label{sec:emergent-categoricality}
  1398	
  1399	The sense of \term{emergence} used here is weak and mechanistic. Population-level
  1400	categoricality is derived from learner-level updating, selection, and repair; it
  1401	is not a label for whatever remains unexplained. This differs from Hopper's
  1402	\enquote{emergent grammar}, where grammar is perpetually provisional
  1403	\autocite{hopper1987}. The present model allows conditioned states with
  1404	near-absorbing attractors.
  1405	
  1406	In the mean-field approximation, $\dot{C}=rC(1-C)$ has fixed points at 0 and 1.
  1407	When $r>0$, positive evidence dominates and the licensing posterior is driven
  1408	toward 1. When $r<0$, error evidence and effective preemption mass dominate and
  1409	the posterior is driven toward 0. Across construction nodes, a community with
  1410	many evidence rates bounded away from zero therefore develops a bimodal
  1411	distribution of $C_t$: most nodes cluster near licensed or non-licensed
  1412	attractors, while only low-evidence or contested nodes remain diffuse.
  1413	
  1414	This is the formal sense in which categorical grammaticality emerges. Closure
  1415	raises $\rho_t^\star$ by keeping the competitor set small and role-specific;
  1416	large $N_t(n,c)$ supplies repeated opportunities; their product is the effective
  1417	preemption mass. Closed, high-opportunity, update-critical contrasts are
  1418	therefore exactly the parameter regime in which the licensing posterior becomes
  1419	bimodal. A finite-population stochastic version should strengthen this pattern:
  1420	sampling drift near 0 and 1 makes the boundaries sticky, while middle states
  1421	remain vulnerable to renewed evidence. This connects the present dynamics to
  1422	complex-adaptive and cultural-evolutionary accounts of language change
  1423	\autocite{beckner2009,kirby2014,BlytheCroft2012}, and to invisible-hand
  1424	explanations, where macro-level order emerges as the unintended consequence of
  1425	micro-level choices \autocite{keller1994}.
  1426	
  1427	\subsection{Actuation}
  1428	\label{sec:actuation}
  1429	
  1430	The same dynamics define \term{actuation}: a sign change in the expected evidence balance for licensing. Actuation occurs when the expected positive stream $s_t(u,c)$ begins to outweigh the combined negative streams $e_t(u,c)+p_t(u,c)$ across successive windows, so that the posterior mean $C_{t+1}(u,c)$ in \eqref{eq:beta-update} increases rather than decays. In the mean-field approximation (box in \S\ref{sec:update}), this is the regime in which the net evidence rate $r(u,c)$ becomes positive, yielding the familiar S-curve at the population level.
  1431	
  1432	The factors that drive such bifurcations align with the motivations discussed in \S\ref{sec:motivations}. Semantic reanalysis may increase utility (and thus $\rho_t^\star$) by making a form--value relation more transparent or useful. Social pressures may shift utilities (prestige effects) or alter the relevant community standard. Structural motivations such as analogical extension can systematically raise licensing probability by borrowing strength from frequent neighbors. Processing innovations may reduce error rates ($e_t$) or locality costs, improving the net evidence balance.
  1433	
  1434	Actuation isn't simply a matter of individual innovation; it requires coordinated community-level change. A few speakers adopting a marginal construction doesn't guarantee its success; actuation demands that the underlying dynamics shift such that acceptance systematically outweighs rejection across the speech community. This explains why many individually sensible innovations fail: they appear before the community conditions are right for the evidence balance to become positive. Near the critical point, small perturbations in community attitudes can trigger rapid shifts between rejection and acceptance, and as the balance becomes more strongly positive the construction moves further from the unstable rejection state, making reversal increasingly unlikely. This produces the characteristic S-curve of documented language changes \autocite{kroch1989}. For constructions in the marginal zone, with near-zero evidence balance, the model predicts heightened sensitivity to external factors and greater cross-community variation, so small or peripheral communities may show volatile behaviour near bifurcation points before a change spreads to larger population centres.
  1435	
  1436	\subsection{How apparent categorical gaps arise without hard bans}
  1437	\label{sec:categorical-without-bans}
  1438	
  1439	When $\mathsf{map}(u,c)=1$ and $K(u,c)$ is high but $C_t(u,c)$ is driven toward zero by
  1440	persistent preemption in a large opportunity set, speakers converge on a sharp,
  1441	non-satiating rejection profile.  Additional processing penalties in $\mathbf{R}_i(u,c)$
  1442	(e.g.\\ systematic garden-path repair due to an entrenched fused-head construal) can strengthen
  1443	the subjective categoricality without any independent structural veto being posited.

  1449	\section{Theoretical implications}\label{sec:implications}
  1450	
  1451	The organizing implication is that grammaticality supports a kind claim in the projectibility-first sense: a category earns standing by tracking a profile whose partial observation licenses declared inferences, with stabilizing support graded by the evidence \autocite{boyd1999, reynolds2026kindsProjectibilityProfiles}. Here the profile is conditioned form--value stability; the projections are repair rates, transmission fidelity, satiation profiles, and change trajectories (\S\ref{sec:projectible-G}); and the support grades are explicit. Bimodal licensing dynamics supply stabilization, the production--repair loop is a maintenance claim, and corrective control~-- the strict reading of \term{homeostatic}~-- remains an open empirical question rather than a label inherited from the framework \autocite{reynolds2026notEveryStableCluster}. The kind claim earns its keep by those projections, not by naming a cluster of mechanisms.
  1452	
  1453	This recasts the competence--performance relation. Licensing and feeling aren't two fundamentally different phenomena but different manifestations of the same conditioned state: processing and other performance factors help shape which form--value relations stabilize, and those stable relations in turn constrain performance. The identification strategy in \S\ref{sec:identification-c} keeps this from becoming an escape hatch, since licensing and feeling are measured by different evidence streams and can falsify each other. The rest of this section develops the consequences: the account's reading of macro-typological universals, its distinctive predictions, and its relation to neighbouring frameworks.
  1454	
  1455	\subsection{Macro-typological constraints on grammatical design}
  1456	
  1457	Recent macro-typological work sharpens the question of how strongly grammar is constrained across languages. \textcite{verkerk2025} test 191 implicational universals from the Universals Archive against Grambank's 2{,}430-language morphosyntactic sample, using Bayesian models that control explicitly for genealogical and areal non-independence and then follow up with spatiophylogenetic analyses of evolutionary rates. A naïve analysis that ignores relatedness appears to support the vast majority of proposed universals, but once phylogeny and geography are accounted for, only about a third (60 of 191) remain statistically supported. Support is concentrated in relatively narrow domains: most hierarchical universals and a substantial minority of \enquote{narrow} word-order universals survive, whereas \enquote{broad} word-order universals and miscellaneous others fare poorly. Diachronically, supported universals typically correspond to \enquote{harmonic} combinations of features (for instance, consistent head--dependent orders) that languages are more likely to evolve into than out of, with these preferred configurations recurring independently across lineages.
  1458	
  1459	From an OVMG perspective, these findings identify recurrent attractors in the global design space of operator-valued form--value relations rather than exceptionless grammatical laws. On the present account, such attractors correspond to configurations for which the net evidence balance (positive evidence $s_t$ vs.\ preemption/error mass $e_t+p_t$) in the dynamics of \S\ref{sec:update} is positive across a wide range of communities. They are cognitively and communicatively favourable enough that repeated episodes of change tend to push $C_t(u,c)$ toward fixation in lineage after lineage. The fact that roughly two-thirds of the tested universals fail once autocorrelation is controlled for also fits a de-idealized view of grammaticality. Community-specific conventions still have considerable freedom in how they realize operator values, with only some regions of the space strongly preferred. Large-scale comparative work of this kind therefore complements OVMG: it circumscribes which parts of the operator-value design space are globally stable, while the present framework provides a meso-level account of how local community dynamics and form--value coherence give rise to those long-run macro-typological regularities.

  1461	\subsection{Predictions}
  1462	
  1463	The model's distinctive predictions come from separating positive evidence,
  1464	opportunity structure, and counterfactual choice probability. Cross-linguistic
  1465	gender remains a useful boundary case, but it is not the strongest test. Any
  1466	framework that treats grammaticalized concord as obligatory predicts stronger
  1467	judgments where concord is more tightly grammaticalized. The companion
  1468	operator-stratum account also complicates the simple gender story: gender
  1469	concord can be policed through paradigm inertia even when its public-update
  1470	value is attenuated \autocite{reynolds2026operators}. The sharper predictions
  1471	concern stable gaps, artificial learning, and L2 transfer.
  1472	
  1473	\textsc{Satiation framing.}
  1474	Repeated exposure should attenuate negative feelings most strongly when a form
  1475	is low-evidence rather than strongly preempted. Independent relative
  1476	\mention{whose}, as in \textsuperscript{?}\ungram{\mention{The packages are still
  1477	here, but Nathan, whose was open, just left.}}, should be sensitive to framing:
  1478	participants told that tokens come from an intentional dialectal, literary, or
  1479	ingroup resource should show more improvement than participants told that the
  1480	same tokens are errors. Left-branch extraction should resist the same treatment
  1481	because its opportunity set is large and its effective preemption mass is high.
  1482	The dependent variable is not only rating change but later repair behaviour and
  1483	elicited production.
  1484	
  1485	\textsc{Artificial-language learning.}
  1486	An artificial-language-learning experiment can hold positive evidence constant
  1487	at zero while manipulating opportunity-set size. Learners could see a system in
  1488	which a target variant is never produced. In the high-opportunity condition,
  1489	competitor variants repeatedly occupy the relevant niche and the target has high
  1490	$\rho_t^\star$; in the low-opportunity condition, the niche rarely arises or the
  1491	target would have low counterfactual utility. OVMG predicts categorical
  1492	rejection in the first condition and uncertainty in the second, even though both
  1493	conditions contain identical zero positive evidence. This puts numbers on the
  1494	Figure~\ref{fig:posterior-means} contrast and is compatible with artificial-language
  1495	learning paradigms that manipulate simplicity and communicative structure
  1496	\autocite{culbertson2016simplicity}.
  1497	
  1498	\textsc{L2 preemption transfer.}
  1499	Early L2 rejections should track L1 preemption mass, not only L2 frequency. A
  1500	learner whose L1 strongly preempts a candidate in a high-opportunity niche
  1501	should reject the L2 analogue early, even if the L2 input frequency is low enough
  1502	to leave native speakers uncertain. Conversely, low L1 preemption should make
  1503	learners more willing to treat rare L2 patterns as unresolved rather than
  1504	impossible. As learners join the L2 speech community, $q_h(c\mid e)$ and the
  1505	constructional hierarchy should shift toward the target norm-centre. This view
  1506	aligns with \posscite{selinker1972} concept of \term{interlanguage}, but gives a
  1507	more specific prediction: transfer is strongest where the L1 has accumulated
  1508	large effective preemption mass.
  1509	
  1510	\textsc{Intonational operators.}
  1511	Nothing in the model privileges segmental material, so tunes are predicted
  1512	operator exponents wherever they form closed, high-opportunity, update-critical
  1513	repertoires: the English polar-question rise and nuclear-accent placement
  1514	configure what an utterance does to the common ground much as tense and
  1515	agreement do \autocite{reynolds2026operators}. The test is whether tune--text
  1516	misassignment (\eg nuclear accent on a function word under broad focus) is
  1517	policed like grammar~-- categorical rejection, repair, resistance to framing~--
  1518	or like style. A worked analysis parallel to the Turkish appendix is future
  1519	work; the prediction stands either way, and failure would count against the
  1520	channel-agnosticism that motivates the model's name.

  1522	\subsection{Relationship to generative grammar}
  1523	
  1524	The generative tradition has provided linguistics with foundational contributions into the systematic nature of grammatical knowledge. Most fundamentally, it demonstrates that grammaticality can't be reduced to semantic plausibility, processing ease, or frequency of attestation. When Chomsky introduced \textit{Colorless green ideas sleep furiously}, he showed definitively that speakers can recognize syntactically well-formed sentences even when they are semantically bizarre. The framework coherently explains categorical constraints like the impossibility of extracting determiner-adjective sequences in English (*\textit{Which do you prefer car?}), and critically, it captures the fact that such sequences remain ungrammatical even when their intended meaning is clear and processing demands are low. 
  1525	
  1526	OVMG shares with generative grammar several foundational observations: that grammaticality judgments reflect systematic, real patterns \autocite{dennett1991}; that these patterns can't be reduced to meaning or processing alone; and that certain syntactic configurations appear to be categorically excluded regardless of context. The analysis of multiple center embeddings presented in \S\ref{sec:processing-constraints} builds directly on ideas about recursive structure, and my treatment of stable gaps (\S\ref{sec:categorical-constraints}) acknowledges the generative discovery that some constructions appear to be universally excluded by the grammar itself.
  1527	
  1528	The generative tradition has also identified fascinating puzzles that any theory of grammar needs to address, such as the independent relative \mention{whose} (\ref{ex:whose}). As \textcite{hankamer1973whose} observe, this construction appears to violate no syntactic principles: independent genitives are possible (\textit{Mine was visiting}), independent interrogative \mention{whose} is grammatical (\textit{Whose was open?}), and \mention{whose} functions perfectly well as a dependent relative pronoun (\textit{the student whose friend was visiting}). The generative tradition's careful documentation of such cases, where seemingly parallel constructions show puzzlingly different grammatical status, has been invaluable in pushing theoretical development forward.
  1529	
  1530	Where OVMG departs from generative grammar is in its explanation of such patterns. Rather than positing an autonomous syntactic component, OVMG suggests that grammatical constraints are produced by the interaction of form--value relations within specific communicative situations. The independent relative \mention{whose} construction illustrates this difference. For this construction to be felicitous, multiple conditions have to converge: the possessor has to be sufficiently accessible in the discourse while the possessum is predictable enough to license ellipsis, but the possessive relationship needs to be semantically significant enough to warrant explicit marking, and this configuration has to occur in a context where a relative clause is the optimal way to package this information. The extreme rarity of contexts satisfying all these conditions appears to prevent the construction from becoming conventionalized in the grammar at all~-- speakers encounter it so rarely, despite perfectly common components, that even when all conditions align perfectly, the construction feels alien.
  1531	
  1532	This approach draws on the generative observations about systematic constraints while providing a different perspective on their source. Where generative theory has to explain why a syntactically possible and pragmatically useful construction is systematically avoided, OVMG suggests that extreme mismatches between predicted and observed frequency may themselves be evidence of strong negative weighting, even when the exact source of that evidence remains unclear. The framework thus preserves what is most valuable in generative theory~-- its recognition of systematic grammatical constraints~-- while embedding those insights in a broader theory of how form--value relations become established and maintained in language communities.
  1533	
  1534	Other cases that generative grammar struggles to explain are those that are grammatical with one meaning but ungrammatical with another, such as (\ref{ex:have-years-sem}) \textit{I have 16 years}. While syntactically identical to grammatical expressions like \mention{I have 16 dollars}, this construction becomes ungrammatical specifically when used to express age. A purely syntactic account would have to explain why the same structure is well-formed in one case but ill-formed in another, despite no apparent syntactic differences. OVMG, in contrast, locates the source of ungrammaticality in the community's form--value conventions: \mention{have}+numeral years has become conventionalized for expressing duration or future time (\textit{I have 16 years until retirement}) but lacks positive evidence for expressing age, where a different construction (\textit{I am 16 years old}) is the established pattern. Similar cases arise with plural forms that are grammatical with some meanings but not others (e.g.\, \mention{peoples} for ethnic groups but not multiple individuals) and with verbs that resist certain arguments despite no obvious syntactic prohibition (e.g.\, \mention{discuss about}). These meaning-dependent grammaticality patterns suggest that what gets down-weighted or up-weighted often depends on specific form--value associations rather than purely structural constraints.
  1535	
  1536	\subsection{Relationship to Construction Grammar}
  1537	
  1538	Construction Grammar (CxG) \autocite{fillmore1988mechanisms, fillmore1988, kay1999grammatical, goldberg1995constructions, goldberg2019, sag2012sign} represents a significant theoretical advance in the understanding of linguistic knowledge. At its core, CxG argues that language consists of learned relationships between form and meaning at multiple levels of complexity. These form--value relations, or constructions, range from individual morphemes to abstract syntactic patterns. This perspective helps explain phenomena that proved challenging for earlier approaches, including idiomatic and partially schematic expressions that don't fit a clean core/periphery split.
  1539	
  1540	CxG's most valuable contribution lies in demonstrating that meaning suffuses all levels of grammatical organization. Rather than treating syntax as an autonomous formal system that interfaces with semantics only at designated points, CxG reveals how meaning and form are inseparable aspects of linguistic knowledge. For instance, the \mention{What's X doing Y?} construction carries an implication of incongruity that can't be derived from its component parts \autocite{kay1999grammatical}. Recent work connects this framework to model-internal analysis: \textcite{weissweiler2023construction} use construction grammar to probe how neural language models handle different levels of linguistic abstraction.
  1541	
  1542	OVMG shares these CxG commitments about form--value relations and constructional abstraction. Its difference is not that one substance class is intrinsically privileged. The companion operator-stratum paper explicitly denies a substance-based boundary: phonological, gestural, lexical, morphological, and syntactic material can all realize operator contrasts when they enter a closed, accountable public-update repertoire \autocite{reynolds2026operators}. The OVMG claim is narrower. Categorical grammaticality clusters around form--value relations with a particular role: they are high-opportunity, closed-paradigm contrasts that configure update, role allocation, scope, reference, or repair.
  1543	
  1544	This difference becomes clear when speakers judge violations. Register, politeness, genre, and many lexical choices can be stable and socially important without blocking the public update itself. A wrong tense, agreement value, clause-linking marker, or obligatory allomorph can mis-set the update instruction even when the intended message is recoverable. The age-predication example (\mention{I have 25 years}) is transparent but not licensed for that English frame; its rejection comes from non-licensing in a high-opportunity constructional niche, not from the substance of syntax or morphology as such.
  1545	
  1546	OVMG therefore preserves CxG's systematic treatment of constructions while adding a prediction about which constructional contrasts attract categorical policing. The relevant contrasts are those whose closure and opportunity structure drive the licensing posterior toward the bimodal regimes in \S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}. That lets CxG's continuum of constructions coexist with a non-arbitrary explanation of why some violations are heard as grammar and others as style, stance, or appropriateness.
  1547	
  1548	\subsection{Relationship to usage-based approaches}
  1549	
  1550	OVMG shares with usage-based approaches (UBA) \autocite{bybee2006, bybee2007frequency, bybee2010} the idea that linguistic knowledge develops from patterns of actual language use rather than from an autonomous formal system. Both perspectives reject the notion that grammaticality can be reduced to abstract rules operating independently of meaning and context.
  1551	
  1552	Two distinctions that organize the formal core have clear precedents in this tradition, and it is worth marking the debts precisely rather than claiming novelty for the distinctions themselves. The individual/population split~-- $\Lambda_{i,t}$ for a speaker's own licensing state, $C_t$ for the population rate~-- restates \posscite{schmid2020} separation of \term{entrenchment} (the cognitive reorganization of a speaker's knowledge) from \term{conventionalization} (the social establishment of a community's regularities), the two coupled feedback cycles of his Entrenchment-and-Conventionalization model. The population dynamics themselves descend from \posscite{croft2000} utterance-selection theory, in which variants are replicated and selected within a community; the S-curve results the model reuses are already worked out in that lineage \autocite{BlytheCroft2012}. Frequency per opportunity is likewise not new: it is the core of \posscite{Stefanowitsch_2006_NegativeEvidence} case that corpora contain negative evidence, where a form counts as \enquote{significantly absent} rather than \enquote{accidentally absent} only once its observed frequency is weighed against the frequency expected given its opportunities. Variationist sociolinguistics has normalized by opportunity since Labov's principle of accountability \autocite{labov1972}.
  1553	
  1554	What OVMG adds is not these distinctions but their joint formalization and a prediction. The licensing-versus-selection factorization $\pi_t \approx C_t \cdot \rho_t^\star$ turns Croft's replication-versus-selection into an estimable product; effective preemption mass $p_t = N_t \cdot \rho_t^\star$ puts a quantity on the gap that \citeauthor{Stefanowitsch_2006_NegativeEvidence} left open, since he showed how to establish that a form is significantly absent but stressed that significant absence \enquote{does not, in itself, provide any clues as to why}. Licensing-versus-selection is exactly that missing clue: a significantly absent form may be unlicensed ($C_t \approx 0$) or licensed but dispreferred ($\rho_t^\star \approx 0$), and the two project differently. The Beta dynamics then convert these quantities into the bimodality result (\S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}) and the prediction about which contrasts attract categorical policing. The relation to \posscite{goldberg2019} \term{coverage} and statistical preemption is one of formalization in the same spirit: her \enquote{explain me this} puzzle~-- a high-coverage double-object frame preempted by the prepositional dative~-- is a preemption-mass story in which the competitor wins a high-opportunity niche. One caution follows from \textcite{AmbridgeEtAl2015}, who find that overall form frequency (entrenchment) predicts the retreat from overgeneralization more robustly than competitor frequency (preemption), and who propose collapsing the two into a single construction-competition process. OVMG keeps them analytically separate~-- error evidence $e_t$ and preemption mass $p_t$ are distinct streams~-- but they enter the Beta update additively (\S\ref{sec:update}), so the model can represent Ambridge and colleagues' single-process view as their sum while leaving the separability an empirical question rather than a stipulation.
  1555	
  1556	The analysis of independent relative \mention{whose}, as in \textsuperscript{?}\textit{I saw Joan, a friend of whose was visiting} is a case in point. A simple UBA account might predict that this construction's marginality stems from its low frequency. But this explanation proves insufficient: the construction isn't merely rare but dramatically rarer than one would expect given the frequency of its component parts. Independent \mention{whose} appears in interrogatives (\mention{Whose is that?}), and the relative \mention{whose} is common in dependent contexts (\mention{the student whose paper was late}). Given these frequencies, analogical extension should make the independent relative use more common than it is. The extreme rarity of independent relative \mention{whose} in corpora, despite the grammatical availability of comparable elements and contexts, marks it as more than just infrequent~-- it points to a systematic gap in form--value relations.
  1557	
  1558	The difference is not that OVMG exempts the operator stratum from usage dynamics. It makes the dynamics more specific. A rare form can remain grammatical when the opportunity set is tiny or when higher-grain constructions license the token by partial pooling. A transparent candidate can become categorically rejected when the opportunity set is large, the analogical candidate would have had high $\rho_t^\star$, and an entrenched competitor wins every time.
  1559	
  1560	This position lets OVMG incorporate usage-based findings about frequency and entrenchment while predicting when frequency becomes preemption. Cases like independent relative \mention{whose} demonstrate the need for a theory that distinguishes simple rarity from systematic non-licensing. The same machinery explains why high-opportunity operator contrasts often yield sharp judgments: the evidence stream is dense enough for effective preemption mass to accumulate.

  1562	\subsection{Relationship to logicality of language accounts}
  1563	
  1564	An alternative perspective, prominent in recent formal semantics, suggests certain types of unacceptability stem from the language faculty itself possessing a deductive system that identifies and filters sentences with logically trivial meanings (tautologies or contradictions) \autocite[cf.][]{del_pinal_logicality_2019, chierchia_logic_2013, fox_economy_2000}. This \enquote{logicality of language} hypothesis attempts to explain, for example, systematic restrictions on quantifiers by arguing the unacceptable cases are \enquote{L-trivial}~-- their triviality arises solely from the meaning and configuration of logical/functional terms (like \mention{every}, \mention{some}, \mention{not}), irrespective of the open-class words (like \mention{student}, \mention{run}) \autocite{gajewski_l-triviality_2009}. A key challenge is explaining why simple tautologies or contradictions (e.g.\, \mention{It is raining and it is not raining}) are often acceptable. \textcite{del_pinal_logicality_2019} argues against \enquote{Logical Skeletons} (which assume the system ignores open-class word identity) in favour of \enquote{LF+RESCALE}, a view where the system sees standard logical forms but allows optional, context-dependent modulation of open-class terms (e.g.\, interpreting the second `raining' as `raining hard') to yield non-trivial meanings.
  1565	
  1566	OVMG offers a potentially broader and more unified account. While `logicality' approaches excel at explaining restrictions tied to functional vocabulary via L-triviality, OVMG aims to cover a wider spectrum of ungrammaticality, including cases not easily reducible to logical contradiction, such as absent form--value relations (\ref{ex:nonsense}), strong deviations from conventional community forms (\ref{ex:sheeps-entrench}), or extreme, unexpected rarity (\ref{ex:whose}). By grounding grammaticality in community-specific conventions (\S\ref{sec:f-m-pair-in-community}) and allowing for gradient compatibility ($K(u,c)$) and acceptance ($C_t(u,c)$), OVMG inherently accommodates cross-linguistic variation and degrees of acceptability, aspects less central to the L-triviality filter. While LF+RESCALE provides a specific mechanism for acceptable `trivialities', OVMG suggests these might arise from more general principles of interpretation within community norms, potentially avoiding the need to posit a dedicated deductive module and specific operators like RESCALE, and offering a clearer distinction between objective grammatical status and subjective processing effects or `feelings' (§\ref{sec:feeling-new}). Thus, OVMG frames grammaticality as an emergent consequence of communicative practice in the formal sense of \S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}, rather than a direct output of logical computation within syntax.

  1570	\subsection{Relationship to relevance-theoretic accounts}
  1571	
  1572	Recent work by \textcite{scottphillips2024communication} offers a fundamental observation: linguistic intuitions about acceptability arise as byproduct effects of cognitive systems for interpreting communicative acts. Just as people immediately sense when a visual stimulus violates core assumptions about physical objects (as with impossible objects), they detect when utterances violate basic presumptions about communicative efficiency. Significantly, Scott-Phillips argues that unacceptability occurs not from mere inefficiency, but from an inherent impossibility of interpreting an utterance consistently with these presumptions~-- similar to how an impossible trident (Figure~\ref{fig:impossible trident}) can't be interpreted as physically cohesive in any context.
  1573	
  1574	\begin{figure}
  1575	    \centering
  1576	    \includegraphics[width=0.2\linewidth]{trident.jpg}
  1577	    \caption{Impossible trident}
  1578	    \label{fig:impossible trident}
  1579	\end{figure}
  1580	
  1581	OVMG shares several key premises with this account. Both frameworks reject the need for an innate grammar faculty, locating linguistic intuitions instead within general cognitive systems. Both recognize that language develops from communicative needs rather than autonomous syntactic principles. OVMG's emphasis on situation-specific form--value relations builds directly on Scott-Phillips's arguments about how communicative pressures shape linguistic conventions.
  1582	
  1583	The frameworks differ primarily in their explanatory mechanisms. Where Scott-Phillips argues that grammaticality judgments reduce to impossibilities of efficient interpretation, OVMG suggests that while communicative pressures shape which form--value relations become conventionalized, these relationships then create systematic constraints that can't be reduced to efficiency alone. For Scott-Phillips, one would have to demonstrate that (\ref{ex:tense}) contains inherent contradictions making efficient interpretation impossible. OVMG instead analyzes how the present perfect's tense--aspect operator value clashes with the meaning of the lexeme \mention{yesterday}. While these analyses might ultimately converge, it remains unclear why the criterion of inherent impossibility of interpretation should apply specifically to operator-value violations rather than to lexical-lexical conflicts or certain phonological patterns. The scope of what constitutes an interpretive impossibility requires further theoretical development.
  1584	
  1585	This difference has important empirical implications. OVMG makes relatively concrete demands: it is possible to test whether specific form--value relations are stable within a communicative situation and identify precise points of operator-value conflict. The challenge for relevance-theoretic accounts, as Scott-Phillips (personal communication, Dec. 16, 2024) acknowledges, lies in establishing independent, empirically vulnerable claims about what makes efficient interpretation inherently impossible rather than merely difficult. Future work comparing specific predictions of each approach~-- particularly around how novel constructions become acceptable or unacceptable~-- could help clarify their relationship and complementary insights.

  1587	\section{Limitations and future directions}\label{sec:limitations}
  1588	
  1589	The account has limits worth marking, and each points to work it invites rather than forecloses. One is its reliance on the concept of \enquote{communicative situation}. Defining the relevant conditioning context and determining the precise set of shared norms that govern grammaticality judgments can be challenging. As noted in \S\ref{sec:f-m-pair-in-community}, speakers navigate overlapping contexts that shift on multiple dimensions~-- immediate situation, durable social positions, and the norms they orient to. The framework acknowledges this fluidity and heterogeneity, but further research is needed to develop more precise methods for operationalizing these dimensions and measuring their influence on grammatical stability.
  1590	
  1591	A second limit is stylistic variation and individual preference. The account handles broad patterns of acceptance and rejection, but it doesn't yet model the finer stylistic choices that fall within grammatical acceptability, or how they interact with the core.
  1592	
  1593	A third concerns purely formal constraints that seem to exist independently of meaning or communicative function. The paper argues that many apparently arbitrary restrictions follow from historical processes and competing motivations, but there may be residual cases that resist even that treatment, and they would sharpen the boundary of the account.

  1595	\subsection{Methodological considerations}\label{sec:Methodological-Considerations}
  1596	
  1597	The development of experimental syntax since the 2000s \autocite{schutze2016} has transformed how gradient acceptability is measured, letting researchers quantify subtle differences in judgments and track how they change with exposure. The framework's predictions invite corpus-based, experimental, and cross-linguistic investigation. Several avenues stand out:
  1598	
  1599	\begin{enumerate}
  1600	\item \textit{Corpus analysis:} Large, balanced corpora allow researchers to track frequency patterns and stability over time. Investigating rare or marginal forms (e.g.\ the independent relative \mention{whose}) in corpora can reveal whether low frequency corresponds to genuinely unstable operator-value patterns or merely a sampling gap. Comparative corpus research in multiple languages can test predictions about how community values influence grammatical distinctions.
  1601	
  1602	\item \textit{Grammaticality judgment experiments:} Controlled psycholinguistic tasks, including magnitude estimation or forced-choice paradigms, can assess subtle gradience in grammaticality judgments. By manipulating exposure and increasing familiarity with marginal forms, researchers can measure satiation effects and distinguish between stable categorical constraints and constructions that become more acceptable through repeated exposure.
  1603	
  1604	\item \textit{Processing measures:} Eye-tracking, self-paced reading, and EEG studies can identify whether some judgments arise from processing overload rather than stable grammatical constraints. If a form becomes easier to parse with practice, it suggests that processing complexity, rather than an immutable rule, lies behind initial judgments of ungrammaticality.
  1605	
  1606	\item \textit{Sociolinguistic fieldwork:} Investigations in communities with distinct dialects or multilingual practices can clarify how social and pragmatic motivations shape grammatical stability. Elicitation tasks and careful comparisons across speech communities can confirm that what counts as grammatical depends on local norms rather than universal principles.
  1607	
  1608	\item \textit{Longitudinal and historical studies:} Diachronic corpora and historical grammars can trace how marginal constructions evolve, testing predictions about which factors lead certain patterns to stabilize, which fade, and which undergo reanalysis. Tracking a community's acceptance of previously dubious constructions over time provides direct evidence for the gradual entrenchment processes posited by the framework.
  1609	
  1610	\item \textit{A threshold-reality test:} The categorical predicate $G_t$ earns
  1611	standing only if community treatment of a form changes \emph{discontinuously} as
  1612	the graded state crosses $\tau(c)$, over and above the smooth effect of
  1613	$\widetilde{G}_t$. That's a regression-discontinuity question. Hearer responses
  1614	are naturally multinomial (accept, repair, request clarification, sanction), and
  1615	covariate-adjusted local-likelihood estimators for discontinuities in
  1616	multinomial response probabilities exist off the shelf \autocite{Lazzaro2026},
  1617	with the processing-cost components of $\mathbf{R}_i$ entering as predetermined
  1618	covariates. Two caveats are design constraints rather than afterthoughts:
  1619	$\tau(c)$ is latent, so it has to be estimated, and pre-registered, before the
  1620	outcome data is touched; and the bimodality result
  1621	(\S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}) predicts that the density of forms near
  1622	the threshold is thin, so power is scarce exactly where the test bites. The
  1623	design also yields a further distinctive prediction: because $\tau(c)$ is
  1624	decision-theoretic (\S\ref{sec:objective-G}), independently manipulating the
  1625	stakes should move the location of the discontinuity. Smoothness through the
  1626	threshold, by contrast, would demote $G_t$ to bookkeeping over $\widetilde{G}_t$.
  1627	\end{enumerate}

  1658	\section{Conclusion}
  1659	
  1660	Grammaticality, de-idealized, isn't a categorical fact about an ideal speaker in a homogeneous community but a conditioned form--value relation. Its profile~-- whether an operator value is structurally realizable ($\mathsf{map}$), coherent ($K$), and licensed across the population ($C_t$)~-- is specified relative to a communicative situation and read through a graded licensing posterior. The old competence--performance split becomes two layers with separate evidence streams, an objective, conventional status and a subjective feeling of anomaly, identified by different measures so that they can falsify each other (\S\ref{sec:identification-c}). Categorical grammaticality is then derived rather than stipulated, an emergent bimodality of the licensing state under preemption (\S\ref{sec:emergent-categoricality}), which leaves a hard representational ban as a last resort rather than the default.
  1661	
  1662	What the profile is for is projection. A category earns its standing not from the mechanisms that hold it together but from what its partial observation licenses us to expect: repair, transmission fidelity, satiation, and trajectories of change, over the licensing state as such, its concentration as well as its mean (\S\ref{sec:projectible-G}). The supporting claims are graded separately. Bimodal dynamics stabilize the profile and the production--repair loop maintains it, while the stronger claim of corrective control stays open rather than being read off the word \enquote{emergent}. On this account grammaticality is a projectible kind, and its mechanisms matter exactly insofar as they explain why the projections hold.
  1663	
  1664	These commitments are testable, and some put the framework at risk. The model predicts that satiation should be more readily inducible for low-evidence constructions than for high-opportunity stable gaps, that artificial-language learners should distinguish zero evidence from strong preemption, and that early second-language rejections should track first-language preemption mass rather than second-language frequency alone. The categorical predicate itself is exposed: community treatment of a form should change discontinuously as licensing crosses the situational threshold, or the predicate demotes to bookkeeping over the graded score (\S\ref{sec:Methodological-Considerations}).
  1665	
  1666	The program these predictions define is measurement-first. Estimate $C_t$ from converging non-rating indicators (corpus rate per opportunity, elicited production, repair), fix the conditioning state before predicting rather than after, and prioritize cross-linguistic contrasts whose operator status differs, since that's where the model expects categoricality to form or dissolve. De-idealizing grammaticality trades a tractable idealization for a profile that answers to evidence. What it buys is not a tidier definition but a category that predicts: which rejections soften, which forms transmit, and which gaps hold.

  1671	\section{Turkish vowel harmony and operator-exponent realization}
  1672	\label{app:turkish-harmony}
  1673	
  1674	Turkish illustrates a sharp distinction between \term{lexical} disharmony inside stems and
  1675	\term{allomorphic} harmony on inflectional suffixes.%
  1676	\footnote{See \autocite{Sezer1981,KabakVogel2001} for discussion of the phonology and \autocite{Arik2015} for experimental evidence on native judgments.} 
  1677	Only the latter interacts with grammaticality in the sense of the OVMG because
  1678	suffixal harmony is part of how an inflectional operator exponent is realized.
  1679	
  1680	\textsc{Stem-internal vowels: disharmony tolerated.}
  1681	Loanwords such as \mention{doktor} `doctor' violate backness harmony, but are fully acceptable;
  1682	the language community simply memorizes the form, so $C_t(u,c)\approx 1$ and $K(u,c)\approx 1$.
  1683	
  1684	\ea \label{ex:doktor}
  1685	\gll doktor \\
  1686	     doctor\\
  1687	\glt `doctor' (disharmonic stem, grammatical)
  1688	\z
  1689	Because no operator feature is left unrealized, the form--value mapping succeeds and
  1690	\begin{equation*}
  1691	\widetilde{G}_t(u,c)=C_t(u,c)\cdot K(u,c)\cdot\mathsf{map}(u,c)\approx 1.
  1692	\end{equation*}
  1693	
  1694	\textsc{Suffixal harmony: operator-exponent requirement.}
  1695	Inflectional morphemes are lexicalized with an underspecified vowel;
  1696	the correct allomorph has to copy \textsc{[$\pm$back]} (and, for some
  1697	suffixes, \textsc{[$\pm$round]}) from the final stem vowel.
  1698	Using the \enquote{wrong} vowel leaves part of the exponent-selection requirement
  1699	unsatisfied, so the null outcome in the $K$ calculation wins and the word is
  1700	ungrammatical.
  1701	
  1702	\ea  \label{ex:kitap-pl}
  1703	\ea[]{\gll kitap-lar\\
  1704	          book-\textsc{pl.back}\\
  1705	      \glt `books' (harmonic, grammatical)}
  1706	\ex[*]{\gll kitap-ler\\
  1707	          book-\textsc{pl.front}\\
  1708	      \glt intended \enquote{books} (suffix harmony violation)}
  1709	\z
  1710	\z
  1711	
  1712	\ea \label{ex:gul-past}
  1713	\ea[]{\gll gül-dü\\
  1714	          laugh-\textsc{pst.front.round}\\
  1715	      \glt `s/he laughed' (harmonic, grammatical)}
  1716	\ex[*]{\gll gül-du\\
  1717	          laugh-\textsc{pst.back.round}\\
  1718	      \glt intended \enquote{s/he laughed} (suffix harmony violation)}
  1719	\z
  1720	\z
  1721	
  1722	\textsc{OVMG account.}
  1723	For the ill-formed *\mention{kitap-ler} and *\mention{gül-du}:
  1724	
  1725	\begin{itemize}
  1726	\item \emph{Mapping} succeeds: the plural / past node selects an exponent.
  1727	\item \emph{Compatibility} $K(u,c)\approx 0$: the chosen allomorph fails to realize the
  1728	      \textsc{[back]} feature copied from the stem.
  1729	\item \emph{Community acceptance} $C_t(g,c)\approx 1$ at the node level: the
  1730	      plural and past nodes are licensed for every speaker. What's entrenched is
  1731	      the node, not the ill-formed token; node-level licensing pools to the
  1732	      token by the coverage equation in \S\ref{sec:objects}, and the failure
  1733	      lives in $K$.
  1734	\end{itemize}
  1735	
  1736	Hence $G_t(u,c)=0$ and speakers judge the word as categorically wrong, not merely
  1737	odd-sounding. By contrast, \mention{doktor} in (\ref{ex:doktor}) keeps $K(u,c)=1$;
  1738	harmony is a phonotactic preference that affects only the phonological
  1739	well-formedness component of $F_{i,t}(u,c)$, not grammaticality.
  1740	
  1741	\textsc{Localized exceptions.}
  1742	Some derivational suffixes (e.g.~\mention{-imsi} `-ish') are lexically
  1743	marked \enquote{disharmonic}. The community memorizes them with $C_t(u,c)\approx 1$, so no
  1744	feature remains unrealized and $G_t(u,c)=1$ despite vowel mismatch. Clitic
  1745	boundaries that start a fresh harmony cycle \autocite{KabakVogel2001} are
  1746	handled the same way: they satisfy the operator-exponent requirement and leave
  1747	harmony to phonology alone.
  1748	
  1749	In sum, Turkish suffix harmony errors are grammaticality failures because they
  1750	mis-realize a closed inflectional operator exponent. Stem disharmony merely lowers phonological well-formedness and so affects only
  1751	the listener's feeling $F_{i,t}(u,c)$.
  1752	
  1753	
  1754	
  1755	If it could be shown that phonology alone caused feelings of ungrammaticality, without realizing an operator contrast or expression-shape requirement, that would constitute a serious challenge to this framework's central claim about the functional target of grammaticality.

I treated this as a single methodologist review, not a simulated review board, because you asked me not to read other reviewers’ outputs.

**1. Summary**
OVMG argues that grammaticality is a conditioned population licensing state over operator-value form relations, with categoricality emerging from opportunity-sensitive preemption rather than from primitive structural bans.

**2. Strengths**
- The profile/projection/support ordering is the paper’s strongest move: the introduction and formal core make clear that mechanisms matter only insofar as they underwrite predictions about repair, satiation, transmission, and change ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:233), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1451)).
- The mean-vs-concentration distinction is a real improvement. The paper explicitly distinguishes stable disagreement from shared uncertainty and uses that distinction to separate *friend of whose*, defective cells, and stable gaps ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:880), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:934)).
- The preemption-mass formalization is productive: \(p_t=N_t\rho_t^\star\) gives a clear way to distinguish rarity from significant absence, especially in the artificial-language and L2-transfer predictions ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1316), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1485)).

**3. Weaknesses**
- Identifiability remains the major problem. The usage equation says observed rate is approximately \(C_t\rho_t^\star\), but the measurement section treats corpus-rate-per-opportunity as an estimator of \(C_t\). Unless \(\rho_t^\star\) is independently normed before touching licensing evidence, the product is underidentified ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1299), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1163)). The preregistration discipline helps, but it is not yet a statistical identification strategy.
- The conditioning state \(c\) is still too elastic. The paper says \(c\) must be fixed before prediction and that post-hoc repartitioning counts against the framework ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1077), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1095)), which is the right norm. But the model also lets \(c\) absorb norm-centre, register, ascription, diachrony, and interlocutor uncertainty. It needs a pre-specified variance decomposition distinguishing stable disagreement from diachronic change, context-sensitivity, ambiguity, survey-item mismatch, terminological drift, internal contradiction, and confusion.
- Formal hygiene needs tightening. \(F\in(-1,0]\) is mathematically fine under the bounded-link definition ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1110)). But \(\mathrm{logit}(C_t)\) is undefined at \(C_t=0,1\), while the prose and table allow boundary values ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:756), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1171)). The logistic-vs-hyperbolic issue is mostly handled by the note at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1392), but the paper should say explicitly that finite Beta updating gives near-boundaries, not literal boundary posteriors.

On the locality claim: the “59-73%” attribution is defensible only if restricted to collapsed regional analyses. Grodner & Gibson report 59% in Experiment 1 and 72.9% for Experiment 2 regional means, but much lower values when item-specific variance is included. ([www2.bcs.rochester.edu](https://www2.bcs.rochester.edu/sites/fjaeger/teaching/LabSyntax2006/readings/Grodner_Gibson_Cog_Sci_2005.pdf)) ([www2.bcs.rochester.edu](https://www2.bcs.rochester.edu/sites/fjaeger/teaching/LabSyntax2006/readings/Grodner_Gibson_Cog_Sci_2005.pdf))

**4. Q&A Question**
What independent data would let us estimate \(\rho_t^\star\) before estimating \(C_t\), so that a low corpus rate is diagnosed as non-licensing rather than licensed-but-dispreferred selection?

**5. Verdict**
Revise & Resubmit. The framework is promising and unusually explicit, but the \(C_t\)/\(\rho_t^\star\)/\(F\) identification problem and the degrees of freedom in \(c\) must be made empirically tighter before the falsifiability claim is fully credible.
