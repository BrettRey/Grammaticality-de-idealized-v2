I treated this as a single methodologist review, not a simulated review board, because you asked me not to read other reviewers’ outputs.

**1. Summary**
OVMG argues that grammaticality is a conditioned population licensing state over operator-value form relations, with categoricality emerging from opportunity-sensitive preemption rather than from primitive structural bans.

**2. Strengths**
- The profile/projection/support ordering is the paper’s strongest move: the introduction and formal core make clear that mechanisms matter only insofar as they underwrite predictions about repair, satiation, transmission, and change ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:233), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1451)).
- The mean-vs-concentration distinction is a real improvement. The paper explicitly distinguishes stable disagreement from shared uncertainty and uses that distinction to separate *friend of whose*, defective cells, and stable gaps ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:880), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:934)).
- The preemption-mass formalization is productive: \(p_t=N_t\rho_t^\star\) gives a clear way to distinguish rarity from significant absence, especially in the artificial-language and L2-transfer predictions ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1316), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1485)).

**3. Weaknesses**
- Identifiability remains the major problem. The usage equation says observed rate is approximately \(C_t\rho_t^\star\), but the measurement section treats corpus-rate-per-opportunity as an estimator of \(C_t\). Unless \(\rho_t^\star\) is independently normed before touching licensing evidence, the product is underidentified ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1299), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1163)). The preregistration discipline helps, but it is not yet a statistical identification strategy.
- The conditioning state \(c\) is still too elastic. The paper says \(c\) must be fixed before prediction and that post-hoc repartitioning counts against the framework ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1077), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1095)), which is the right norm. But the model also lets \(c\) absorb norm-centre, register, ascription, diachrony, and interlocutor uncertainty. It needs a pre-specified variance decomposition distinguishing stable disagreement from diachronic change, context-sensitivity, ambiguity, survey-item mismatch, terminological drift, internal contradiction, and confusion.
- Formal hygiene needs tightening. \(F\in(-1,0]\) is mathematically fine under the bounded-link definition ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1110)). But \(\mathrm{logit}(C_t)\) is undefined at \(C_t=0,1\), while the prose and table allow boundary values ([main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:756), [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1171)). The logistic-vs-hyperbolic issue is mostly handled by the note at [main.tex](/Users/brettreynolds/projects/LLM-CLI-projects/papers/active/grammaticality-de-idealized/main.tex:1392), but the paper should say explicitly that finite Beta updating gives near-boundaries, not literal boundary posteriors.

On the locality claim: the “59-73%” attribution is defensible only if restricted to collapsed regional analyses. Grodner & Gibson report 59% in Experiment 1 and 72.9% for Experiment 2 regional means, but much lower values when item-specific variance is included. ([www2.bcs.rochester.edu](https://www2.bcs.rochester.edu/sites/fjaeger/teaching/LabSyntax2006/readings/Grodner_Gibson_Cog_Sci_2005.pdf)) ([www2.bcs.rochester.edu](https://www2.bcs.rochester.edu/sites/fjaeger/teaching/LabSyntax2006/readings/Grodner_Gibson_Cog_Sci_2005.pdf))

**4. Q&A Question**
What independent data would let us estimate \(\rho_t^\star\) before estimating \(C_t\), so that a low corpus rate is diagnosed as non-licensing rather than licensed-but-dispreferred selection?

**5. Verdict**
Revise & Resubmit. The framework is promising and unusually explicit, but the \(C_t\)/\(\rho_t^\star\)/\(F\) identification problem and the degrees of freedom in \(c\) must be made empirically tighter before the falsifiability claim is fully credible.
