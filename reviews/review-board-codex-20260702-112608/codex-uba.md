**1. Summary**

The paper argues that grammaticality is a conditioned, population-level form-value licensing state: `map * K * C_t`, with categorical judgments emerging from opportunity-sensitive preemption dynamics rather than from an idealized binary grammar.

**2. Strengths**

- The strongest value-added is the rare-uncertain vs strongly-preempted distinction. The contrast between independent relative `whose` and left-branch extraction in “Projectible use of the graded score” and Figure 2 is genuinely useful: same low mean, different posterior concentration, hence different predictions for satiation/framing/change.

- The “Relationship to usage-based approaches” subsection is unusually candid about debts. It fairly credits Schmid for entrenchment/conventionalization, Croft/Blythe-Croft for population dynamics, Stefanowitsch/Labov for opportunity normalization, Goldberg for coverage/preemption, and Ambridge et al. for the entrenchment-vs-preemption caution.

- The two-layer separation between conventional status and subjective feeling is productive. The identification section’s insistence that `C_t` come from corpus-per-opportunity, production, and repair rather than ratings alone is exactly the right usage-based move.

**3. Weaknesses**

- **Value-added is real but narrower than advertised.** The new contribution is not “usage plus form-value relations” or “constructional meaning,” which CxG/UBA already own. It is the measurement architecture: licensing vs selection, `p_t = N_t * rho*`, posterior concentration, and the separate feeling channel. The paper should make that the central novelty claim and reduce any implication that CxG/UBA lacked the conceptual ingredients.

- **“Operator” is not yet doing enough independent work inside this paper.** The definition appears as “closed-paradigm, update-configuring contrast” and is repeatedly deferred to the companion paper. But because `operator` determines the scope of `K`, the categoricality prediction, and the Turkish appendix, the present paper needs a self-contained diagnostic table: operator / non-operator / borderline, with predictions that differ from “closed-class,” “grammaticalized,” or “high-frequency construction.”

- **The independent-relative `whose` analysis is promising but under-reported.** The draft says COCA has zero hits and that the opportunity set is tiny, which supports uncertainty rather than rejection. But later it says the construction is “dramatically rarer than expected” given its components, which sounds like significant absence/preemption. Those claims need one shared denominator: query strings, opportunity definition, expected-rate model, upper bound, and examples of counted opportunities. Otherwise zero hits in COCA are not enough.

- **AGR evidence is illustrative, not yet auditable in the paper.** The figure gives denominators and Wilson intervals, and the underlying project files appear to have detailed KWIC coding. But the manuscript itself should report exact cells, counts, filtering exclusions, corpus version/date, and the rival baseline beaten. A short table would fix this.

- **Saussurean “value” mostly renames constructional meaning unless sharpened.** It helps avoid “feature value” confusion and emphasizes contrast, but CxG already treats constructions as form-meaning pairings in networks. The paper should either cash out “value” as contrastive/operator-specific prediction, or compress the Saussure framing.

**4. Q&A Question**

What empirical result would force you to abandon the operator stratum rather than merely reclassify a construction as operator-like after the fact?

**5. Verdict**

Revise & Resubmit: the framework has a real usage-based value-added in its opportunity-sensitive posterior dynamics, but the operator boundary and the `whose`/COCA evidence need to be made self-contained and auditable.
