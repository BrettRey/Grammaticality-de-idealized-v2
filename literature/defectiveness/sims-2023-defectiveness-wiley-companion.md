Morphological defectiveness is a situation in which an inflected or derived word is expected in a language, but no word with that meaning exists. A 1sg form of pobedit’ is expected because the same lexeme is well-formed in other person-number combinations (e.g. pobedim ‘we will be victorious’) and 1sg is well-formed in other verbs (e.g. sprošu ‘I will ask’). Moreover, it is possible to form sentence frames that require a 1sg form of this verb. Yet it is widely agreed that speakers reject any 1sg inflected form of pobedit’, including the expected form pobežu. This renders any otherwise well-formed sentence requiring the 1sg of this verb unacceptable, requiring speakers to find a workaround using a different construction. This illustrates the key property of morphological defectiveness – it is characterized by ineffability, the absolute lack of a grammatical construction. Instances of defectiveness in inflectional and derivational morphology are paradigm gaps and derivational gaps, respectively.

The central mystery surrounding defectiveness has to do with why we ever observe it in the first place. Given the generally productive nature of (especially inflectional) morphology, why does the grammar sometimes fail to produce an output form? Given communicative need, why do speakers tolerate gaps? Why do they not simply fill them? Indeed, speakers do sometimes fill gaps by taking advantage of other morphological or lexical forms. In Central Alaskan Yup’ik, for example, speakers ‘… find it impossible to create some forms, recognize that they have no memory of them, and substitute different ones… Such substitution is of course a source of syncretism’ (Mithun 2010, 131). Suppletion (Anderson 2010; Rebrus and Törkenczy 2009), heteroclisis (Stump 2006, 299), and periphrasis (Sims 2006a) have similarly been noted to be gap-filling strategies.1 For gaps that stem from phonological or morphological ill-formedness, structural repair is also an option. So it initially seems surprising that we do not always find gaps being filled in these ways.

Against this backdrop, morphological defectiveness seems anomalous – a situation in which the grammar fails and in doing so, undercuts speakers’ ability to communicate. A closer look suggests a more complex story, however. This entry examines ways in which morphological defectiveness manifests and how it interacts with the working of a language’s morphology. It considers some of the ways in which the phenomenon is integrated with and sheds light on the structure and functioning of morphological systems.

### 2 What it means for a word to be ‘missing’: definitionaland methodological issues

This section considers three definitional and methodological questions. First, is defectiveness an empirically unitary phenomenon? Second, is defectiveness in derivation qualitatively different from defectiveness in inflection? Third, how can gaps be identified, given gradient attestation of gap-filling forms and the Zipfian distribution of word frequency?

2.1 Canonical and non-canonical defectiveness

In the domain of morphology, the terms defective and gap are used to refer to diverse phenomena. These include the Russian 1sg verbal gaps illustrated in Table 1 above but also, for example, phenomena like uninflectedness and zero expression. Some of these uses fall outside of the scope of this entry, reflecting wholly different phenomena. Uninflectedness and zero expression can be distinguished from defectiveness by the fact that they result in well-formed, grammatical structures. Ineffability is taken here as a minimum criterion for some linguistic pattern to be considered defective. At the same time, even within this definition, defectiveness is a fairly diverse phenomenon. It is therefore useful to ask how canonically defective some pattern is.

Baerman and Corbett (2010, 2) define canonical defectiveness in terms of idiosyncrasy and lexical restrictedness: ‘… the more idiosyncratic and lexically restricted the gap, the more canonically defective it is…’. For inflection, at least, it is useful to pursue this idea in the context of two canonical properties of morphosyntactic or morphosemantic feature values: exhaustivity and unlimited applicability. This approach is influenced by the canonical typology framework, especially Corbett’s (2012) work defining canonical morphosyntactic features, although the formulation here differs in some respects.2

(1) Exhaustivity: The paradigm of some lexeme is exhaustive when morphosyntactic or morphosemantic values belonging to different features combine without restriction.

In a system in which, say, verbs express three values for person (1st, 2nd, 3rd), two values for number (singular, plural), and three values of tense (past, present, future), verb paradigms are exhaustive (Spencer 2003) if they have 18 inflected forms corresponding to the 3 × 2 × 3 combinatorics of these values.3

- (3) Canonical defectiveness: An instance of defectiveness is canonical to the extent that it manifests in feature values that exhibit exhaustivity and unlimited applicability, and to the extent that it is lexically restricted.


The Russian 1sg verbal gaps introduced in Table 1 come close to fitting the model of canonical defectiveness. Within the non-past subparadigm, person and number values are fully combinable and apply to all verbs – even the defective ones. (Remember that it is possible to form a syntactic frame requiring 1sg for the defective verbs, even though the morphology does not provide a form to realize this cell.) Moreover, the gaps occur in a fairly small number of verbs and are lexically restricted to this extent.

Defining defectiveness in terms of its canonical properties allows other kinds of defectiveness to be identified without having to commit to any particular theoretical analysis, or even whether defectiveness is a unified phenomenon empirically. Of particular note here is defectiveness due to underexhaustivity. This is a situation in which paradigms are systematically incomplete in the sense that they ‘… demand a set of feature co-occurrence restrictions to be defined over the potential space of forms implied by the basic feature set and its combinatorics…’ (Spencer 2003, 253).6 Russian verbs are underexhaustive in the perfective: in imperfective indicative, Russian verbs distinguish past, present and future tenses, but only past and future are distinguished in perfective indicative verbs, as shown in Table 2.7 (Only the singular forms are shown for reasons of space.)

##### 2.2 Inflectional versus derivational defectiveness

Inflectional values typically exhibit greater exhaustivity and more applicability than derivational meanings and the resulting greater regularity in the structure of inflectional paradigms creates a clear expectation that a given set of morphological properties should have a form realizing it. A question that arises is thus whether paradigm gaps and derivational gaps are qualitatively different phenomena, or whether they simply tend to differ in their canonicity.

Derivation is less easily interpreted in terms of the intersecting typed feature values that are generally considered to structure inflection. It therefore does not always make sense to talk about the exhaustivity of derivational meanings. However, a relational approach offers a possible way to consider inflectional and derivational defectiveness jointly. Bonami and Strnadová (2019) propose to reformulate the definition of paradigms in terms of the content relations and form relations holding among lexical items. Applied to inflection, the paradigm of some lexeme is exhaustive to the extent that its inflected forms instantiate all possible content relations. A content relation likewise has unlimited applicability if it holds among relevant inflected forms of all lexemes belonging to some lexical category. Focusing on the relational aspect of morphological structure makes it possible to identify morphological relations in cases that are not amenable to features, and as a corollary, to find derivational gaps when some relation fails to be extended across the lexicon.

For example, in French a semantic relation can be identified that relates the word referring to a game to the word referring to players of that game, as in (6).

Is *artism a gap? What about *folklorism, *diarism, *novelism and *linguism? The relational structure established by the examples in (7a) does not extend to the ones in (7b), given the different meanings of the -ist forms in each group. Support in the relational structure for having a lexeme with the meaning ‘philosophy of art’ (artism) is in this respect weak.10 The important point here is that we can view the status of *artism – whether it is a gap – as deriving not simply from the unrealized possibility of combining art and -ism, but rather, from the derivational relations that the system motivates.

Turning to the examples in (7c), Stump (2019) argues that cases such as characteristic and shamanistic should be analysed as examples of rule conflation, in which two affixes exhibit dependencies such that they are partly telescoped.11 In a rule conflation, -ist and -ic compose into -istic, which then suffixes to character. (See O’Donnell (2015) for a similar idea from the perspective of chunking as a property of lexical storage.) The analytic intuition is thus that characteristic forms directly from character. Moreover, if there is no clear derivational relation between characteristic and a corresponding agentive noun, *characterist is at best a highly non-canonical gap, on the grounds that there is minimal expectation that there should be a form with such a meaning.

##### 2.3 Gradient frequency of use and gradient acceptability

It is often possible to observe words being used that fill supposed paradigm gaps or derivational gaps. For example, frequency counts suggest that many English speakers do not use the past tense of forgo (Sims 2015, 53–54), reflecting competition between the possible forms forgoed and forwent and resulting uncertainty about what the form should be. Nonetheless, there are examples in the Corpus of Contemporary American English (COCA, Davies 2008) from newspapers (8a), blogs (8b) and fiction (8c), among other genres.12

- (8) a. In a separate statement, Greitens’ attorney Ed Dowd said the computer tampering charge ‘makes no sense at all’ and called his client’s early charity work – when he forewent a salary from the organization – ‘an extraordinary act of public service’.


- b. Being included in something so fun, so exciting is far more nourishing than the cheese I forewent to afford this artist, who inspires me at least four times a day…
- c. The knight forwent the bow this time, but one was implicit in a slight tip of his head.


Even setting aside the fact that speakers sometimes produce speech errors and other kinds of ungrammatical structures, and that they may mention gap-filling forms (as opposed to using them), it is clear that speakers do sometimes fill supposed gaps. This raises the question of what it means for a form to be ‘missing’ and whether non-attestation is a necessary criterion for the definition of defectiveness.

Janda and Tyers (2021, 114) take a corpus-based approach to defining defectiveness in terms of non-attestation, as ‘any situation in which a word form… of a lexeme is rare or unattested’. Given the Zipfian distribution of word frequency and the fact that some inflected forms tend to be more frequently used than others, this results in all but the very most frequent lexemes of a language being identified as defective in one or more paradigm cells. When presented with gap-filling forms, however, speakers tend to reject all possibilities, choosing not to produce or accept any form at all. This judgement may be gradient (Albright 2003; Lukács et al. 2010; Sims 2006a, 2009), but it represents a qualitatively different pattern than that found with nonce words. This strongly suggests that a definition based (only) on non-attestation conflates distinct phenomena. The problem then becomes one of precision – it is difficult to distinguish items that are defective in the language from ‘false hits’, namely items that are merely unattested in some corpus.

At the same time, the problem of gap-filling forms remains. Consistent with the idea of gaps as gradient phenomena, Sims (2015, 52–55) suggests that missing forms be identified based on having low frequency of occurrence relative to their expected frequency. However, attempts to implement even this weaker notion of what it means for a morphological form to be ‘missing’ have produced mixed results. Copot and Bonami (2020) show that for French verbs identified by grammars as having gaps in the imperfect third singular, the frequency of occurrence of gap-filling forms in the FrCoW corpus (Schäfer 2015) does not differ significantly from the frequency of imperfect third-singular forms in general, when controlling for lemma frequency. Assuming we accept that these forms are in fact defective, it seems that non-attestation even relative to expected frequency is not a necessary property. This is a problem of recall – trying to identify gaps in a corpus based on non-attestation, or even lower-than-expected attestation, fails to find many true examples. Somewhat more encouragingly, Ayala and Hansson (2021) find that genitive plural gaps in Icelandic nouns that are motivated by homophony avoidance are significantly underattested in the Icelandic Gigaword Corpus (Steingrímsson et al. 2018), but like Copot and Bonami, they do not find evidence that genitive plural gaps motivated by uncertainty about the form of the genitive plural are underattested.

Taken together, these precision and recall problems create significant methodological challenges for detection of gaps in corpora.

Assuming that defectiveness is a property of individual speakers’ grammars, attestation of gap-filling forms may be an illusion created by individual-level variation. It is common for the set of defective words in some languages to vary from speaker to speaker. This has been reported from studies using both field methods (e.g. Mithun 2010, 134) and experimental methods (e.g. Löwenadler 2010a). For Hungarian, Rebrus and Törkenczy (2009, 215) observe that ‘… although all of the 65 stems we classified as defective are defective for some speakers of standard Hungarian, not all of them are defective for all speakers’. The possibility that individual-level variation may be the norm for defectiveness rather than the exception highlights the need for methods that are appropriate to studying defectiveness at the level of the individual.

Experimental methods are the most obvious path forward in this respect. There is a disconnect between the sources used to identify gaps – typically, examples are culled from dictionaries or grammars – and the goal of studying the individual.

### 3 Structural (form-level) causes of defectiveness

As noted in the introduction, a central mystery surrounding defectiveness has to do with why we ever observe it in the first place. Some gaps have straightforward semantic causes, such as an incongruence between a lexeme’s meaning and the meaning of a grammatical category. For instance, Aristar (1997) reports that in Basque, the instrumental form of animate nouns tends to be defective because humans tend to be agents of the action, not instruments.13 Gaps with semantic causes may in fact be the most common type of defectiveness. Singularia and pluralia tantum nouns often have a semantic basis, for example. However, gaps with structural causes, especially lexically restricted gaps (i.e. ones towards the more canonical end of the scale) involving form-level causes are perhaps more interesting for morphology, since they are the scenarios in which it is most surprising that the gap is not filled. In this section I review some such factors.

The following discussion is by necessity not comprehensive, but a central theme is that while structural factors are implicated as causes of defectiveness in various ways, they do not generally constitute sufficient explanations by themselves. While structural factors might describe the conditions under which gaps arise, they generally cannot account for why repairs do not. This is made particularly clear by the fact that the language often also has non-defective words with the same structural properties. The picture that emerges is thus one in which it is possible to identify structural factors that are shared by defective words, but which are not sufficient for deterministically identifying the set of defective words.

However, in at least 30 adjectives (Löwenadler 2010b, 74) these phonotactic violations fail to be repaired and the neuter form of an adjective is defective; see kåt ‘horny’, pryd ‘prudish’ and kry ‘healthy’.

While analyses of the Swedish data agree that the gaps are rooted in these phonotactic constraints, the question is why some words undergo consonant cluster reduction and/or vowel shortening, while others have paradigm gaps instead. Here analyses differ. Löwenadler (2010b) suggests that defective and non-defective adjectives are differentiated by communicative need. The defective adjectives tend to describe inherent properties of animate entities, which themselves tend to be referred to with common gender nouns. There may therefore be less communicative need for a neuter form of these adjectives. The situation in which the phonological conditions in which gaps are found are not by themselves sufficient to distinguish defective from non-defective words is typical of phonotactically-rooted gaps.

##### 3.4 Anti-allomorphy effects

Some gaps seem to be tied to a dispreference for novel allomorphs. For instance, the English suffix -ize strongly disprefers stress to fall on the syllable immediately before the suffix. Raffelsiefen (2004, 115) observes that stress patterns that violate this restriction (e.g. Sudán ➔ ??Sudánize) can be repaired when a more optimal stress pattern is found in a related form: ‘… for speakers who know Sùdanése the formation Súdanìse may be acceptable…’. However, when the only stem allomorph found in related forms has stress on the final stem syllable, defectiveness may result. Examples include *Tibetize, *Brazilize and *Peruize. (Contrast Sudán-Sùdanése with Tibét-Tibétan, Brazíl-Brazílian, and Perú-Perúvian.) The essence of the argument is thus that the English derivational gaps result because of a dispreference for innovating a new allomorph of a given stem, even on a pattern that exists in other -ize words.

Pertsova extends the idea of anti-allomorphy effects (what she calls lexical conservatism following Steriade (1999)) to explanation of paradigm gaps in the genitive plural of Russian nouns (Pertsova 2005) and the first singular of Russian verbs (Pertsova 2016; Pertsova and Kuznetsova 2015). ##### 3.5 Anti-homophony effects

### 4 Non-structural causes of defectiveness

This section examines some of the non-structural causes of gaps that have been documented. Again the discussion is necessarily illustrative rather than comprehensive, but it suggests how independent facts of language change, in particular, can lead to defectiveness.

- 4.1 Incomplete morphologization


The example of English beware illustrates how defectiveness can arise from incomplete morphologization, in this case, univerbation from a copula + adjective. - 4.3 Incomplete morphological integration of borrowings


Language contact is implicated as a cause of defectiveness in connection with morphological integration of borrowings. ### 5 On the nature of causality in the context of defectiveness

The preceding discussion highlights that defectiveness is often linked to structural grammatical factors – areas of the grammar in which the expected output would be illicit, where the grammar is indeterminate, and so on. At the same time, these

In this section I argue that in seeking an answer to this question, it is important to distinguish between causal explanation and mechanistic explanation. In philosophy of science, causal explanation answers a ‘how come’ question, identifying preconditions for some outcome, while mechanistic explanation answers a ‘how’ question. To borrow a metaphor from Williamson (2011a), without penicillin (or other antibiotics), pneumonia is often not curable. Yet it is not the existence of penicillin that cures pneumonia exactly, but rather, it is the biological processes induced by a shot of penicillin that result in someone with pneumonia being cured. Penicillin thus has the power of causal explanation for why someone’s pneumonia is cured, but a full explanation also requires specifying the mechanisms – the biological processes – involved. These biological processes have the power of mechanistic explanation because they answer the ‘how’ question. In mechanistic theories of causality, mechanistic explanation and causal explanation are tightly connected. In particular, ‘… two events are typically accepted as causally connected if and only if they are connected by an underlying physical mechanism of the appropriate sort’ (Williamson 2011a, 421). Additionally, the causing factor must generally effect some change in the caused outcome – it must be difference-making.

The reasoning invoked in Section 3 about structural factors as causes of defectiveness implicitly involved counterfactuality. Taking indeterminate grammar as an example (although the other structural factors could be substituted here), an argument that indeterminate grammar causes defectiveness relies on the logic that if there were no grammar indeterminacy, there would be no defectiveness. Counterfactual reasoning of this sort inherently encodes the claim that the structural preconditions are difference-making for the outcome of defectiveness. Yet the structural factors clearly do not form a complete causal story. In particular, they fail to answer the how question. How does indeterminate grammar lead to defectiveness?

If we were able to establish that a repair or other resolution of indeterminate grammatical structures were unavailable, we could conclude that there is explanation by counterfactual – because there is no repair mechanism, defectiveness results. Perhaps there are such cases in language. Instances in which gaps are a product of morphosyntactic feature co-occurrence restrictions (see Section 2) might fit here. However, if we accept that alternative outcomes – outcomes other than gaps – are generally available and if we cannot establish their principled absence for some particular case, then we must conclude that the answer is incomplete by virtue of not answering the how question. We must also seek an explanation at the level of mechanisms. Stated differently, grammar indeterminacy and the other structural factors reviewed above offer explanation, but not prediction (for this distinction, see Williamson 2011b, 435).

An example comes from one of the earliest theoretical studies of morphological defectiveness, Hetzron’s (1975) analysis of the Russian 1sg verbal gaps introduced in Table 1 and repeated as Table 10.

Hetzron argues that stem alternation only in the 1sg (compare stem-final [ʃ] in 1sg sprošu to [sj] in other inflected forms) ‘… is quite an uneconomical use of a morphophonemic rule. What is surprising, then, is not so much that the 1sg. non-past forms, with their special morphophonemic demands, are troublesome, but that only a limited number of verbs of this type present such difficulties, while the rest do put up with the inconvenience of consonant alternation for a single form’ (Hetzron 1975, 861). Here, Hetzron locates the explanation of the 1sg gaps in a grammar problem – ‘morphophonemic demands’. In other words, he ‘solves’ the problem of gaps by defining stem alternation occurring only in a single inflected form as uneconomical and thus inherently demanding and problematic. Yet in the absence of independent evidence that stem alternation is problematic, the explanation is vacuous and circular. Problematic reasoning of this sort, which starts from the assumption that defectiveness results from grammar failure and reasons backwards to a structural problem, is surprisingly common. Yet it fails to recognize that the mechanisms connecting structural facts (here, stem consonant alternation) to defectiveness might not require that the structural conditions are in any way problematic. In other words, it misses that the role of structural factors in causing defectiveness depends on the mechanisms connecting the two.

Daland et al. (2007) and Sims (2015, Chapter 7) present a fundamentally mechanistic account of the Russian 1sg verb gaps. Stripped to its essence, their argument is that gaps exist in modern Russian because the mechanisms employed in language learning are sufficient to learn them.24 This does not mean that structural factors are irrelevant, however. They show that most verbs in Russian are too sparsely attested overall for the baseline version of a Bayesian learning model to correctly learn gaps in the 1sg form, but when the model is made sensitive to the morphophonological structure of verbs’ lexical neighbourhoods, its ability to pick out verbs with gaps in the 1sg improves substantially. Sims (2015) argues that this is a lexical gang effect on par with what is found for English past-tense verbs

The point is that mechanistic explanation has the potential to lead to different understandings of the role of structural factors in causing defectiveness. And no matter what analysis we adopt of the Russian facts, it is clear that not all examples of gaps are associated with illicit, indeterminate or otherwise potentially problematic grammatical structures (see the example of beware in Section 4.1 above). A broader understanding of the causes of gaps that extends beyond structural and semantic factors to include mechanistic explanation is thus required, and mechanistic explanation should be part of the story even in cases of gaps that are connected to structural factors.

This frames the question of why defectiveness occurs, rather than some repair or other gap-filling strategy, in terms of what the relevant mechanisms are that give rise to defectiveness and what the conditions are under which such mechanisms are triggered by some structural condition. One possible mechanism is avoidance (Sims 2006b). Returning to the example of grammar indeterminacy, just as penicillin does not cure pneumonia by its mere existence, we can hypothesize that it is not grammar indeterminacy that produces gaps by its mere existence, but rather, by the actions that speakers take in response to grammar indeterminacy. The same can be said of other structural causes, including anti-allomorphy effects, anti-homophony effects, and phonotactic ill-formedness. Avoidance may be a mechanism that is widespread as a cause of defectiveness. Moreover, it seems highly likely that other mechanisms are also at work. For instance, Daland et al.’s (2007) account of the Russian 1sg verbal gaps is rooted in learning mechanisms. Examples like beware suggest that the cognitive processes involved in lexicalization may be important sources of mechanistic explanation, perhaps even independently of particular grammar-internal causes. However, this is work that the field largely has yet to take up.

Ultimately, to the extent that the fundamental mystery surrounding gaps has to do with why speakers do not fill them, it is important to recognize that this formulation of the problem is rooted implicitly or explicitly in an understanding of gaps as the result of grammar failures. This is reflected in the ways in which the existence of gaps under certain structural conditions is often interpreted (without independent support) as evidence that the relevant structural conditions are inherently problematic. While this may sometimes be true, this is difficult to reconcile with observations that the same kinds of conditions (in the same

### 6 Learning

In this section I consider how the preceding discussion relates to questions of how gaps are learned. Some studies have been concerned with defectiveness in the context of the poverty of the stimulus (Chomsky 1980), the argument that the language input that children are exposed is not rich enough on its own to allow them to acquire all aspects of natural language. There is not space here to review the debate over poverty of the stimulus, but a central issue has to do with the evidence available to children to learn the ungrammaticality of certain structures, in other words, what is not possible in language. How defectiveness is learned has obvious relevance in the context of this debate.

For those scholars who accept that neither direct negative evidence of ungrammaticality (e.g. explicit statements about the defectiveness of some forms) or indirect negative evidence of ungrammaticality (e.g. the absence of a form that is expected to exist) is generally available to children, and that children’s input is impoverished in this way, how defectiveness is learned has been interpreted as presenting a challenge (Baronian 2005; Hudson 2000; Nevins et al. 2014; Rice 2003; Wolf and McCarthy 2009). For example, Hudson (2000, 298) suggests that: ‘[S]ince learners have no positive evidence for the [English *amn’t] gap they need never be aware of it, so their experience, to all intents and purposes, presents them with a paradigm that has no gap – and yet we all know that the gap is real’. Sims (2015, 217–220) points out that the (claimed) non-availability of evidence for gaps has been used to motivate a strong claim that gaps must have structural causes – specifically, that gaps must be epiphenomenal byproducts of grammar conflicts or grammar indeterminacy – since they cannot be learned otherwise.

Not all linguists accept that implicit evidence for gaps is unavailable to learners (Johansson 1999; Orgun and Sprouse 1999; Sims 2015). If learners are able to infer the absence of an expected wordform or lexeme, at least in some conditions, then paradigm gaps present no inherent challenge to theories of learnability. There are significant questions, of course, about what the conditions for learnability are. But as Sims (2015, 217–220) argues, to the extent that the full set of known examples of defectiveness is hard to reconcile with a strong claim that gaps can occur only in the context of structural properties that are sufficient to render them learnable, an approach that allows implicit evidence seems necessary. Moreover, implicit or explicit claims that gaps must fall out epiphenomenally from the grammar are too strong.

The question of how defectiveness is learned is thus tied up with broader debates about the role and availability of negative evidence in language learning. Some of the differences of opinion may reflect inclarity about what is meant by ‘negative evidence’. Nonetheless, one interesting aspect of the learning question has been how it has influenced claims about causality. In other contexts in which learners successfully acquire knowledge of the ungrammaticality of some construction, linguists have often appealed to universal grammar (UG) to provide innate knowledge. Yet when it comes to defectiveness, it is implausible that knowledge of paradigm gaps or derivational gaps is encoded in UG. Some linguists have in this situation looked to grammar conflicts or other ‘inadequacies’ to motivate gaps and obviate the need for learning from negative evidence. This is another way in which claims about mechanisms shape claims about the causal relationship between grammatical structure and defectiveness.

### 7 Formal analyses

As something of a postscript, this section briefly considers ways in which gaps have been analysed within formal grammar. Many theoretical frameworks are designed such that the grammar always produces an output. In Paradigm Function Morphology (Stump 2001), for instance, the Identity Function Default universally defines the stem as the output when the morphology does not provide a specific exponent to realize some set of grammatical properties. This essentially makes it impossible for the morphology not to output an inflected form for a well-defined combination of a stem and morphosyntactic values. In a different vein, a foundational principle of Optimality Theory (Prince and Smolensky 2002 [1993]) is that well-formedness is a relative requirement, and so the lack of a candidate form that is fully well-formed does not, by itself, prevent the evaluation component from producing an output. These are only two examples, but the idea that the grammar always produces an output form for a well-formed set of grammatical properties is a deeply ingrained design principle of many frameworks. For these, gaps pose a technical problem but also a more deeply conceptual one.

Consistent with the observation that gaps are not an empirically unified phenomenon, different analyses have been proposed for different kinds of gaps. The earliest formal analysis comes from Halle (1973), who in the context of the Russian 1sg verbal gaps proposes a lexically specific feature as part of defective verbs’ representations, [-lexical insertion], that keeps a generated form from being inserted into syntax. Albright (2009) criticizes this formal mechanism on the grounds that in principle, any word could be specified as having the [-lexical insertion] feature, missing the generalization that many examples of gaps, including the Russian ones, occur in morphophonologically-defined environments. Blocking of this sort is thus too powerful. Sims (2015, 246) points out that lexicalized gaps may share morphophonological properties for historical reasons. Halle’s approach, or some modern equivalent thereof, may thus be adequate at least for lexicalized gaps if there is mechanistic explanation for their morphophonological distribution.

10.1002/9781119693604.morphcom022, Downloaded from https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/9781119693604.morphcom022 by University Of Toronto, Wiley Online Library on [03/07/2026]. See the Terms and Conditions (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/terms-and-conditions) on Wiley Online Library for rules of use; OA articles are governed by the applicable Creative Commons License

Stump (2010) proposes to account for lexicalized paradigm gaps in Sanskrit in terms of morphosyntactic feature licensing. Postulating two levels of morphosyntactic features, one having to do with syntactic contrasts (the content paradigm) and the other with morphophonological contrasts (the form paradigm), with cells in the two paradigms linked by statements of the grammar, Stump posits that gaps can be accounted for as non-licensed paradigm cells at either level. In other words, this approach evades the problem caused by default realizations by treating defectiveness as a property of the paradigm cell being realized rather than the exponents realizing it. This sort of approach could also be applied more generally to morphosyntactically rooted gaps of the type already observed in Section 2.1 above.

Gaps connected to indeterminate realization raise particular problems for models in which default morphological realizations play a central role. These gaps require that there neither be a memorized form of the word to draw on, nor a default form that can be used to generate the required form. In short, they seem to ‘fall through the cracks’ of the grammar. It is unclear how this can happen in a framework in which a default is always defined. In frameworks that do not encode defaults, such an analysis is generally easier.

Finally, most of the debate about formal mechanisms has revolved around gaps that are motivated by phonotactic ill-formedness. Three main competing approaches within Optimality Theory have been proposed: the null parse (Prince and Smolensky 2002 [1993]),26 MParse (Raffelsiefen 2004; Wolf and McCarthy 2009), and a CONTROL component (Fanselow and Féry 2002; Orgun and Sprouse 1999; Orgun and Sprouse 2009). The Null Parse and MParse both involve constraints within the candidate evaluation component (EVAL) that force a null output. In contrast, Orgun and Sprouse propose that in addition to the EVAL component in which constraints are violable, there is a separate CONTROL component that is ordered after EVAL in the derivation. Constraints in the CONTROL component are inviolable, so any output from EVAL that violates a CONTROL constraint results in ineffability and a gap.

Among these mechanisms, the strongest predictions are made by CONTROL. Since CONTROL requires that constraints producing defectiveness are strictly separate from those in the EVAL component, interactions between constraints in different components, or duplication of CONTROL constraints in the EVAL component, are predicted not to occur. These predictions have been criticized as incorrect (Löwenadler 2010b; Raffelsiefen 2004). While these criticisms are convincing that CONTROL is not sufficient by itself to account for all gaps, the heterogeneous nature of gaps probably means that no single mechanism is likely to be adequate. CONTROL, which is reminiscent of Halle’s [-lexical insertion] to the extent that it is a surface filter on the productive output of the grammar, may be adequate to some kinds of gaps but not others. This is difficult to assess based on the current state of the field.

### 8 Conclusions

Defectiveness is not a monolithic phenomenon. Derivational and inflectional forms may ‘go missing’ for reasons having to do with semantics, morphosyntactic feature

structure, morphophonological structure (lack of a lexicalized allomorph, indeterminate morphological rule application, anti-allomorphy effects, anti-homophony effects, etc.), and phonotactic structure – in short, seemingly any aspect of the grammar that the morphology touches. Gaps may also have no synchronic structural cause at all and be partly or fully lexicalized, or have causes from outside of the formal grammar, resulting for example from processes of lexicalization, inflectional loss, or integration of borrowings. Many, probably even most or all, examples reflect a mixture of causes.

It initially seems surprising that gaps are found at all, particularly since it is common for the same structural properties to lead in a given language to defectiveness in some words and repair or tolerated non-optimality in others. Moreover, speakers can extend words from elsewhere in the morphological system to fill gaps. Yet a closer look suggests that linguists’ surprise at finding gaps may stem from the kinds of causal factors that linguists have prioritized (i.e. structural ones), as much as it does the nature of defectiveness itself. While research in the past 20 years or so has identified a range of structural conditions that are repeatedly found in cases of morphological defectiveness, these are only one piece of the puzzle. They create the potential for morphological defectiveness to arise, but do not generally answer the question of why defectiveness occurs rather than some other outcome.

The current state of the field suggests a few directions for future work. First, to more fully answer the question of why defectiveness occurs rather than some other outcome, more attention needs to be paid to the role of mechanistic explanation, specifically to processes of learning and change. Second, while methods for identifying gaps in corpora have produced limited results so far, corpus data may offer useful insight into differences between defective and comparable non-defective forms. Intersecting with this, individual differences in whether some word is considered defective are also promising for deeper investigation, since individual variation appears anecdotally to be rampant. Finally, recent interest in defining and investigating the structure of derivational paradigms feeds questions about the relationship between inflectional defectiveness and derivational defectiveness.

Interest in morphological defectiveness has grown significantly in the past two decades. While there is still much to be learned, defectiveness has increasingly been recognized as being an important source of information about morphological systems. In this entry I have tried to present some of the ways in which gaps, while often considered simply exceptions to morphological structure, in fact reflect the morphology’s inner workings, including complicated questions about the nature of structural causes as explanation for defectiveness.
